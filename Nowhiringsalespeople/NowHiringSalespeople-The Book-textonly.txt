Introduction


No one grows up dreaming of becoming an outside, Business to Business or B2B salesperson. There are no kindergarten career days where someone says, “When I grow up, I want to knock on doors, get rejected ten to fifteen times before lunch, manage a pipeline and live or die by a quota.” Yet every year, hundreds of thousands of people enter this amazing profession. 

Some of you are business majors who don’t know what else to do. Others may be athletes looking to still compete in a competitive arena after sports have ended. Some could be high achievers drawn to uncapped income and fast advancement opportunities. And some of you may have chosen one area of education and realized it wasn’t what you wanted to do and now sales seems like a viable option. Or you simply need a job and discover, almost by chance, that sales can open doors most careers never will.

So many people I have interviewed have this internal clock about achievement and advancement that puts so much pressure on them. I feel like I need to say this to you right at the start of this book. However you got here, you are not alone and you’re not too late. You’re standing at the entrance to one of the most challenging, rewarding and opportunity rich professions in business. You should be excited!

You would think that getting a sales job should be straightforward. Companies need salespeople. Candidates want opportunity. Interviews happen all the time and offers get made everyday. It should be easy but in reality it’s rarely that simple. If you’ve applied to internships through college or you’ve been applying to sales roles now you’ve probably seen the contradictions.

Companies say they want “entry-level candidates,” yet ask for experience. They say they’ll train you, but expect you to already act like a salesperson. They emphasize culture and attitude, yet make decisions that seem impossible to predict. You may even think you interview well, feel confident and still hear nothing back. Meanwhile someone else, with similar qualifications, gets the offer. It can feel arbitrary. It isn’t.

The problem is that no one teaches you how sales hiring actually works. Most people enter the sales job market with almost no real guidance. College career centers offer generic advice about resumes and networking. Family and friends give well intentioned advice but their tips seem outdated. Online articles recycle the same surface level interview tactics. 

Few resources explain how sales managers truly evaluate candidates especially for outside B2B roles where performance matters more than credentials. Sales hiring is different from most other fields. Managers aren’t just looking for technical skills or academic achievement. They are trying to predict the future performance in an environment defined by uncertainty, rejection, independence and pressure.

They are asking questions like:

- Will this person prospect consistently when no one is watching?
- Can they handle rejection without losing confidence?
- Will they represent the company professionally in front of the customers?
- Would they buy from you?
- Are they coachable or do they think they know everything?
- Will they still be here in a year?

The problem is, you may never hear those questions asked out loud. But trust me, they are always being asked. If you don’t understand what decision makers are really evaluating, the process feels random. Once you do, it becomes something you can navigate and even feels strategic.

There’s a hidden opportunity that most candidates don’t realize. Sales is one of the few professions where you can land a high earning career without decades of experience, advanced degrees or specialized technical skills. Companies regularly hire recent graduates and career changers into roles with; significant income potential, rapid advancement opportunities, autonomy and independence, exposure to leadership and decision makers and a clear path into management or entrepreneurship.

But those opportunities go to candidates who demonstrate the right traits, not necessarily the strongest resumes. This creates a massive advantage for prepared applicants. You don’t need to out credential everyone. You don’t need to be the most polished speaker. You don’t even need prior sales experience. What you need is the ability to clearly articulate that you have the foundations of what successful salespeople are made of.

This book exists because I’ve interviewed, hired, trained, promoted and even fired salespeople my entire career. I’ve interviewed thousands of candidates across multiple industries and organizations. I’ve sat on both sides of the interview table for years. I’ve been the candidate trying to get in and as the executive deciding who gets the offer.

I can tell you this. A lot of strong candidates have lost opportunities they could have won. Most weak candidates never realize why they were rejected. And hiring managers frequently struggle to identify the best people quickly. The result is frustration on both sides. This book exists to eliminate that confusion.

This book isn’t a theory book. It’s not a motivational speech. It’s not a generic guide. It’s a practical road map for securing a high quality outside B2B sales role.

Inside these pages, you’ll learn how to approach the entire process with intention. From deciding if sales is right for you to your first 90 days on the job. We’ll cover each step of the process and prepare you to ace each one of them. Think of this as an insider’s manual. The guide most hiring managers wish candidates had read before walking into the interview.
This book is designed for people pursuing entry level or early career roles in outside sales. From recent college graduates, career changers seeking income growth, individuals with little or no formal sales experience and anyone considering sales as a professional growth path.

If you’re willing to work hard, learn quickly, and bet on your own potential, this profession can change your life. But if you don’t nail the interview, getting in the right door doesn’t matter.

A final thought before you get started. You should know that sales is not an easy path. It demands discipline, resilience and accountability. It also offers something increasingly rare in today’s world. Control. Control over your income, control over your growth and control over how far you can go.

You don’t need permission to succeed, just the opportunity. This book is about earning the opportunity and being able to confidently crush the interview process each step of the way. This book isn’t about gaming the system. It’s not meant for you to pretend to be someone you’re not. It’s about understanding what companies truly need and demonstrating that you can deliver it.

If you’re ready to pursue a career where performance matters more than background, where effort can out work experience and where your future is largely in your own hands, then you’re in the right place. 

Turn the page.

Your sales career begins long before your first day on the job. It begins with winning the job itself!

Chapter 1

Why Sales?

In my early twenties I had no idea what I was going to do with my life. I couldn’t figure out how I was going to fit into a career. Worse, I didn’t know what the right first step was. When I asked people what they thought I should do, almost everyone told me that I should go into sales.They all thought I had the personality to be successful in the sales world. Afterall I was voted the class clown in my high school and I always had something to say. I was always quick with a joke and always made light of any situation. I‘m one of those people that can get along with anyone anywhere. My mother-in-law says that I make friends with people at the grocery checkout and she’s not wrong. In their eyes and mine, I was a perfect fit for sales. They thought sales was all about charisma and personality. Unfortunately when I finally did make my way into B2B or Business To Business sales, that personality proved to be a huge problem. A good personality does fit into sales but it doesn’t get you deals, and that’s the biggest lesson I had to learn. Your personality will get you liked, but it doesn’t get you deals.

The truth about sales is that it is a very big umbrella with a multitude of different channels that all get lumped together. If you sell cell phones, you’re in sales. If you sell SaaS (Software as a Service), you’re in sales. If you sell pharmaceuticals, you are in sales. The same goes for cars, real estate, solar, legal services, copiers, uniforms, rental equipment, even owning and running your own business and so on and so on. The bigger truth is, if you haven’t realized it yet, EVERYTHING IS SALES! Which means if you're going to start somewhere, starting in sales is a great start! 

I’ve worked with so many people who have started a business because they wanted the freedom of working for themselves but they failed. Why? Because they sucked at selling! They could lead, they could manage a P&L and were some of the smartest people I’ve met, but they still sucked at selling. There’s two things that happen when these folks start a business. The first is that they convince themselves they can sell, the second is when it actually comes time to sell their product or service, they can’t. Sadly, they don’t realize it right away. Denial sets in slowly. They busy themselves with everything but selling. The fear of selling keeps them buried in the administrative work of their business. They gravitate to what they know, not what they don’t. And they don’t know sales. 

Most people want to sell something that they can see other people buying. In fact, that’s the biggest misconception in the sales world. In the spirit of, “there’s nothing new under the sun” you are most likely not going to be selling water in the desert. At best you’re going to be selling a better alternative to a current solution. Most people getting into sales miss this when they are looking at potential roles to get into. Most prospects already have a solution or an answer to what you’re selling. In sales you have to overcome a lot of hurdles and objections; two of the biggest are that your prospect most likely has a current solution in place and the second is they just don’t want to change. Change is difficult for people and it’s always easier to just say no. See what you’re getting into here? 
What Types of Sales Jobs Are There?


Let’s dig in first as to the different types of sales. Usually sales is grouped by who you’re selling to, how you sell, and what you’re selling. Here’s the breakdown of the main categories:

There are three types of sales segmented into buyer types and for the sake of this book and getting you a job, we are only going to focus on the first two categories. The third category is business to government or B2G and we’re going to skip that one as most people don’t go into that type of sales, at least not at first. It’s a lucrative sales career but usually saved for someone whose career is well established. 

Taking out B2G leaves us with Business to Consumer or B2C and Business to Business or B2B. In a typical B2B role, you’ll sell products or services from one business to another. This is where your technology sales, professional services, distribution, logistics, financial services, HR services, payroll services, recruiting, facilities and maintenance and healthcare all live. In the B2C roles you’ll find retail sales, inside sales, real estate, automotive sales, insurance agents, door-to-door or direct sales, luxury goods, hospitality, fitness, and E-commerce. 


						
B2C also differs by being very features and benefits driven while B2B is more solution driven with a results oriented approach. For the sake of this book, we are going to dig into specifically interviewing for B2B roles. But the questions and answers will still work for B2C roles but the interviews aren’t as robust as a B2B interview would be.

If I could go back in time I would start out in a B2B sales role. The training, support and experience you get in a formal B2B role will last you the rest of your life. Here’s why you want a B2B sales job.

Higher earning potential - B2B sales typically involve larger deal sizes and recurring revenue, think software subscriptions, service contracts or multi-year deals. The commissions are often larger per sale as well. So many B2B roles offer a base + commission structure that grows as you move into higher roles. (We will talk about career trajectory later in this book) And of course pay wherein high performers can earn six figures or more. 

Strategic and Consultative Selling - B2B salespeople tend to prefer solving business problems, not just pushing products. These sales reps engage in longer, more strategic conversations about ROI (Return on Investment), efficiency, and growth. It’s more about consultative and influencing decision-makers than pure persuasion. B2B salespeople also work cross functionally with almost all other departments including finance, operations, IT and marketing. 

Professional Growth and Career Development - B2B sales environments typically have more defined career paths. From SDR (Sales Development Rep) ? AE (Account Executive) ? Enterprise AE ? Manager ? Director ? VP of Sales ? CRO (Chief Revenue Officer).  From the beginning of their career, salespeople learn pipeline management, forecasting, CRM (Customer Relationship Management) systems, and strategic account planning which happen to be incredibly transferable leadership skills. B2B sales roles also align with executive career paths including VP of Sales, Chief Commercial Officers, Chief Revenue Officers and even CEO’s. 

*Author's Note: There're a lot of great CEOs who started out in sales. Here’s some examples. Howard Schultz the founder of Starbucks was a sales rep for Xerox. Mark Cuban, from Shark Tank and Dallas Mavericks fame began his career as a software salesman before founding several tech companies and becoming a billionaire investor. Warren Buffet, legendary investor, worked as a securities salesman for several years before building his investment empire. Sara Blakely, the founder of Spanx started out in sales, selling fax machines door to door.}

Long-Term Relationship Building - Many salespeople thrive on building deep, ongoing relationships with clients. B2B selling often includes renewals, upsells, and expansion within accounts. The goal of any salesperson is to become a trusted advisor, not just a vendor. These relationships have increased with LinkedIn and the adoption of texting customers and prospects. 

Intellectual Challenge and Business Impact - B2B sales involves complex problem-solving, industry research, and multi-stakeholder influence. You’re selling to smart, analytical buyers, often executives who weigh each decision and its impact carefully because they are responsible for producing measurable results. In B2B you’re constantly playing chess in regard to strategy and winning deals. 
Training, Tools and Systems - B2B sales organizations have incredible training programs designed to make their salespeople as effective as possible. They teach a sales process including strategic rapport, discovery, and presenting. The training a larger B2B company will provide you will be foundational to your sales career. 

What Do The Numbers Look Like?

Let’s talk about the number of people entering the salesforce every year. According to the Sales Education Foundation (SEF), over 50% of college graduates, regardless of major, take their first job in a sales role. For business majors, that number can be over 60% and for marketing majors specifically, that number can go up to 88% of graduates that will go into sales as their first job. If you feel like you’re up against the world here don’t worry. According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) there are approximately 13.4 million people employed in Sales and Related Occupations. The BLS also projects that the 1.8 million job openings per year, on average, will rise in sales occupations due mostly to replacement (people leaving) rather than net growth. If you’re thinking, “that’s a lot of turnover”, you’re absolutely correct. Welcome to sales. 

I hate to be harsh here, but you should realize that turnover is a reality of sales and you should also know that there is a lot of competition for these sales roles. It’s up to you to walk into these interviews prepared and crush them. That’s why you bought this book and that’s what we are going to do. Here’s another hard truth, you’re looking for a sales job, not a home. The goal of any sales professional shouldn’t be to stay where you are, it should be to take the experience you get and leverage that into a future role whether that’s management or a new sales role with a higher base and the potential for more commission. Fortunately the idea of companies being a “family” is starting to lose traction in the work force. And it should, companies aren’t a family. You don’t cut people out of your family for lack of performance. You cut teammates that aren’t performing well. At best, you should feel like a part of a team. 

Want to hear some more numbers?  The average sales reps tenure is about 18 months (another reason you should get good at interviewing). There are several reasons for that number. The first is fit. B2B sales isn’t for everyone. It’s demanding, quota driven, measured and every sales activity you do is held to a certain degree of accountability. Some people who go into sales just don’t make it. The job wasn’t what they thought it was going to be or they found something else they were passionate about.  All of that is ok and it should also be seen as a reality. The fundamentals taken away will still last throughout a career. The second is that people do really well and leave too soon. I say too soon because statistics say that even though turnover is around 18 months, peak performance isn’t achieved until a sales reps second or third year. If you find yourself in a B2B role you will also find yourself getting recruited by other companies almost immediately. Reps leave because some of those offers are too good to turn down. If you ever interviewed with me, I’d tell you to stay for two years. The job offers you get at that second year or going into your third year are WAY better and your experience and patience will pay incredible dividends! The third reason for that turnover is promotion. Promoted in 18 months? Yup, that happens, and a lot more than you’d think. I worked for a massive 2 billion dollar organization and within 4 months of starting my outside sales role I was promoted to sales manager. It can happen to you too. Some more reasons for that turnover could be lack of promotion opportunities, bad bosses, and layoffs. You should know what you’re getting into so you can prepare yourself, navigate your environment and set the right expectations for yourself and your experience.

Turnover in sales is approximately + 30% and in some companies that number can be much higher. How could it be that high? According to Forrester.com the average quota attainment for B2B sales organisations is only 47%. One broader benchmark for 2024 reported by Spotio says that “up to 70% of B2B sales reps missed their sales quota.” You have to know what you’re doing in sales, and if you don’t know what you’re doing, you have to do everything you can to get up to speed as fast as possible. I’ve always thought that it was funny that 50% of college grads go into sales but only 4% of schools teach it. For your personal development, I recommend books (see my must read B2B sales book in the second to last chapter of this book) and finding a company with great training. Bonus points if you can find yourself a manager who really cares about the advancement of their team. That last one is the most difficult in this whole process and we’ll cover that in the last chapter.

Some of these numbers might scare you a little bit but don’t let them. Life has a 100% failure rate and you’re still living that. The point is, some people will fail at sales, but if you put the work in like you’re doing now reading this book, there’s a good chance you’ll make it!

What People Think Is A Good Fit For Sales

 You know what I’ve never been asked in an interview nor have I ever asked? “What’s your morning routine?” You know why? Because no one cares. There’s a big difference between what people think is a good fit for sales and what really is. Most people think about the cliche salesperson they see on TV or in the movies. The tall, good looking person with an incredible personality who talks well and is a naturally gifted communicator. They think of someone who can convince others to do what they want by just exuding confidence. The quick talking, fearless person who embraces people while simultaneously being a lone wolf who swoops in and gets all the credit for bringing in the big deal. 

You may think of Jim or Dwight from the television show The Office or you may think of Jordan Belfort from Wolf of Wallstreet fame. Either way you have a picture of someone other than you. Someone you feel like you need to be. It may even be a person close to you like your father or an uncle who’s done really well in sales. They dress in logo’d golf polos, khaki pants. They are tan and love the game of golf. Or it could be your mom or aunt who’s incredibly charismatic and likeable. You may see some of yourself in them and you may share some of the same qualities but you’re looking at all the superficial things. None of those things matter either. A lot of the disconnect I see with salespeople is they are trying to be what they think a sales rep should be and not what one really is. Most people look at the superficial things because they see the salesperson and not sales as an actual art form. And it is exactly that, an art. Pay attention to the art. You wouldn’t try to paint like Monet or play a guitar like Eric Clapton or shoot like Steph Curry by trying to look like them. No, not at all. What you would do if you wanted to be like them is put in the work like they did to become that person. 

What Actually Is A Good Fit For Sales?

Coachability

Athletes often get recruited in sales and most people believe it’s because they are competitive but I’ve come to learn that competition is only a piece of the puzzle. The bigger piece is that athletes love to be coached. Coachability is one of the most underrated but powerful predictors of sales success. It’s what separates the reps who plateau from the ones who keep evolving into top performers and future leaders. Coachability isn’t about “taking feedback” it’s about actively seeking it, processing it, and applying it. It’s the willingness to be uncomfortable for the sake of growth. A coachable salesperson listens to feedback without getting defensive. They experiment with new techniques even when they don’t come naturally. You can often find them reflecting on what went wrong and they own their outcomes. When it comes to their managers and other higher ups, they view them as mentors or partners and not critics. Sales environments are dynamic. Products evolve, markets shift, and buyer behavior changes constantly. A coachable rep adapts quickly and stays relevant to their prospects and customers. They tend to self-coach as well, identifying their own patterns before others point them out. Over time, that feedback loop compounds into mastery because every call becomes a learning opportunity. Showing yourself as a coachable person in an interview is a great way to break into sales. Managers love coachable people. 

The way you show coachability in an interview is to be curious. Curious people will even ask for feedback during the interview. They are humble and willing to admit past mistakes and how they’ve learned from them. Coachable people talk about personal development and habits they have that make them better. They read books, have mentors and take courses on their own. In an interview the opposite of coachability is defensiveness. Excuse when discussing failure is a sign of a lack of a willingness to be coachable. You may not be an introvert or have ADHD but all of us can be coachable. 

Curiosity

Some of the best salespeople I’ve met are the dumbest people on the first call with a prospect. Not because they are actually stupid but because they show up asking questions and assuming nothing. Curiosity in sales isn’t about asking questions, it’s about asking the right questions with genuine interest and intent. A curious salesperson doesn’t seek information just to move a deal forward; they seek to understand the “why” behind the “what”.  They are driven by a need to learn. They want to understand why the customer buys, why the current solution isn’t working and why the business operates the way that it does. Curiosity is the secret to building trust with prospects, reveals deeper problems with their current approach and helps tailor solutions that actually matter.

Curiosity turns sales (and interviews) from a transaction into a discovery process. It’s what makes a prospect feel seen and understood, not just being “sold to.” Curiosity when done authentically makes discovery feel natural. A candidate can show curiosity by how they engage with the interviewer. Curious candidates ask thoughtful specific questions about the company, the team, or the customers. They reference something they researched about the business. They say things like, “I was wondering why your customers choose you over your competitors.” And they show excitement when learning something new, even outside their comfort zone.

Emotional Intelligence

Emotional intelligence is the ability to understand and manage emotions both your own and others’. In sales, it’s not just about being “good with people.” It’s about reading the emotional landscape of every conversation and adapting your approach in real time. A high EQ salesperson doesn’t just listen to words, they pick up tone, hesitation, and energy. They know when to push, when to pause, and when to connect on a human level.

At its core, EQ combines four foundational abilities. Self-awareness, the recognition of your own emotions and how they impact your tone or behavior. Self-regulation, staying composed and professional even under stress or rejection. Empathy, understanding what the buyer (or interviewer) feels, not just what they say. Social skills, being able to build rapport, influence decisions, and maintaining trust.

Sales is emotional at every level, buying decisions are rarely logical; they are justified with logic. A salesperson with strong EQ can create safety and connection quickly and that makes buyers more comfortable and causes them to open up. EQ doesn’t mean soft, it’s strategic empathy. It allows reps to influence outcomes by understanding human behaviour better than anyone else. 

Here’s how EQ shows up in great salespeople. They are able to sense when a prospect is overwhelmed and they shift gears. They recognize their own frustration after a tough call and reset before the next one. They, and this is big, don’t take rejection personally; they see it as data, not judgement. They connect emotionally to a buyer’s motivation, like reducing stress, proving value to their boss, or regaining control of a chaotic process.

Resilience and Grit

Resilience and grit are the emotional engines that keep salespeople consistent, confident and coachable even when results, markets and buyers fluctuate. In sales, resilience and grit are not about “toughing it out”. They are about how quickly and how positively a salesperson bounces back from adversity. Resilience is short-term recovery, the ability to reset emotionally after a rejection, bad month, bad quarter or a lost deal. Grit is long-term endurance, the sustained drive to keep pursuing excellence through ups, downs, and plateaus. Together they create salespeople who can stay steady when others spiral, who learn from losses instead of being defined by them. Resilient salespeople don’t avoid failure, they metabolize it. They extract lessons, recalibrate, and re-engage with renewed focus. 

Sales is the only profession where rejection is a DAILY experience. Even the best reps hear “no” far more often than they hear “yes.” Without resilience, that repeated rejection chips away at confidence, effort and eventually performance. Grit takes that same mentality and stretches it over time. It’s what keeps a salesperson focused on their craft month after month, year after year when excitement fades and only discipline remains. Sales success is rarely a sprint. It’s hundreds of tiny recoveries strung together by grit.

High grit salespeople stand out in a few key ways. They separate emotion from outcome. They don’t let one bad call define their identity. They tend to find purpose in the process. They trust the activity even when the results lag behind. They self-regulate quickly. They take a walk, reset and come back sharp. They visualize progress. They track improvement metrics beyond quota, like better close rates or deeper discovery calls. They maintain a high level of optimism. This isn’t fake positivity but genuine belief that effort compounds. The mindset is actually a simple one. They see every loss as tuition paid against future success.

Great candidates demonstrate resilience and grit by telling stories of adversity and what they learned from it. They speak with a language that focuses on ownership rather than victimhood. They also show evidence of consistency and trajectory by hitting goals over long periods, not just short-term bursts. Grit doesn’t just help people survive, it compounds everything else. Curiosity, coachability, and EQ all improve when someone refuses to quit learning. That consistency creates momentum, and momentum builds confidence. In other words Grit makes good habits permanent and bad moments fleeting. Grit and resilience are the emotional durability behind every great salesperson. They turn pressure into focus, failure into feedback, and rejection into resilience. A salesperson with Grit doesn’t ride a wave, they build the surfboard.

Intrinsic Motivation

There are two types of motivation, extrinsic and intrinsic  . Extrinsic motivation is driven by external factors such as rewards, praise, recognition and promotions. That’s typically what we think when we think of salespeople. Driven by external rewards like paychecks and commissions. But the great salespeople usually have a higher degree of intrinsic motivation. It is the desire to do something because it’s personally rewarding, interesting or enjoyable. It’s driven by internal satisfaction and is often more sustainable in the long run. My dad used to tell me, “Don’t look for something you’re passionate about, bring your passion with you.” That’s really the difference between these two motivations. 

In sales that means the person is motivated by, mastering their craft, solving problems for customers, improving themselves and their performance and the feeling of pride in progress, not just payoff. They don’t need constant external validation. They’re self-fueled, curious, consistent, and focused on growth. They’re usually competitive but they compete with themselves. Intrinsic motivation really helps when you think in terms of building your career and seeing the bigger picture of career trajectory not just getting quota for the week or month. Take the time to zoom out on your career and see what you’re learning and how it’s going to help you move up. 

Sales is full of short-term highs and lows. Bonuses, leaderboards, lost deals, great months, bad months, big commission checks and no commission checks. Extrinsic motivation creates spikes in performance and is ruled by exterior variables. Intrinsic motivation sustains performance and keeps you moving forward regardless of what’s going on around you. When an intrinsically motivated rep hits quota they ask, “how can I do that smarter next quarter?” They stay engaged when the market pivots because they value mastery and consistency.  They self-reflect after losses instead of blaming pricing, product, or luck. They have a strong degree of ownership. When people care about how they perform, not just what they earn, their work quality and persistence towards their goals skyrocket. 

Top performing sales reps with intrinsic motivation set personal benchmarks aiming to beat their own performance. Usually the company has a quota but they know what they want to make! They prepare deeply, not to impress their boss, but because they value being ready when an opportunity presents itself. They study their craft, reading books, listening to podcasts, Youtube, watching call reviews, role plays, and seeking out mentorship. They reflect regularly by journaling, self assessing and refining their approach. They celebrate the process not the outcomes.

Great candidates light up when talking about personal growth or learning moments. They describe their wins in terms of the process rather than just reward. Their goals are their own, not just the company's standard metrics. They have several past examples of self-development with books they’ve read, coaching they received and mentors who’ve helped shape them. Intrinsic motivation is the difference between chasing quotas and chasing mastery. They don’t work for rewards, they work for purpose. In the end, purpose driven salespeople outlast, outperform, and outgrow everyone else. I said earlier that the best salespeople have a higher degree of intrinsic motivation but they still have both. They do enjoy the rewards like commissions, bonuses, and accolades but I hope you understand now that those are byproducts of deeper motivation, not the source. The balance produces maturity, longevity and self-driven accountability.

Integrity and Authenticity

Integrity and authenticity are essential and they are what makes everything else in sales work for the long-term. Without them, skills look manipulative and curiosity feels calculated. WIth them, even tough conversations build trust and deepen relationships. In sales, integrity is doing what’s right even when it costs you in the short term. Authenticity is being genuine, showing up as yourself, not a performance of who you think a salesperson should be. Together, they build trust, credibility, and loyalty. They are the difference between a transaction and a relationship. 

Authenticity means being transparent about limitations, pricing, or fit. Saying “no” when a product or service isn’t right for a customer and admitting when you don’t know something and following up with the answer. Integrity means keeping promises both big and small. Telling the truth in any and every situation and prioritizing the customer’s outcome over your own commission.

We’ve all dealt with “that” salesperson. The one who you could tell was just being sleazy. How could you tell? Most likely the reason lies in their insincerity and you could sense it. Buyers can sense insincerity instantly and when trust is broken, it’s nearly impossible to recover. However, when trust is built through honesty and consistency, you create a competitive advantage that money will never be able to buy you. It’s how a salesperson goes from seller to trusted advisor. Integrity wins because it eliminates buyer skepticism and customers stop feeling “sold.” It strengthens referrals and your reputation in your industry while creating a psychological open line of safe communication between buyer and seller turning them into two parties looking to solve a problem. Authenticity wins because people buy from people not personas. When you’re yourself, your tone, energy, and presence are more relaxed and believable. Authentic reps connect on a human level and humans make buying decisions emotionally first, logically second. Authenticity and integrity are the long game when it comes to sales. They compound trust with every conversation.

When a salesperson leads with integrity they have no problem telling a prospect “no” or that they can’t do what they are looking for or satisfy a specific need. They own mistakes publicly and fix them quickly. They’re consistent and their words and actions line up regardless of the hour, day, week or month. They don’t badmouth competitors, they differentiate with class. They stay honest in forecasting even when it’s uncomfortable. Authenticity shows up in subtler ways, their language sounds natural and not rehearsed. They share appropriate personal stories that create connections. They speak with empathy and confidence, not pressure and they don’t pretend to have all the answers instead they show curiosity not ego.  When authenticity and integrity meet, customers feel safe and safety is the fastest route to trust.

In an interview the interviewer will “feel” integrity early on in the process. They look for consistency between the candidates words and demeanor. They want to see if the candidate is honest in their personal assessment when discussing past roles and takes accountability for missteps or mistakes. The candidate will also have a genuine curiosity about the company's culture, story and problem they solve for their customers. 

Salespeople still need to close but the closes are easier because trust helps close for them. When buyers believe your word, pricing becomes less of an objection, and decisions speed up. That’s why the best closers are usually the most honest, not the slickest. They are believable because they are real. In a successful sales career you learn that authenticity builds trust, trust builds influence and influence builds revenue! Integrity and authenticity are the bedrock of professional sales. They don’t just earn deals, they earn respect. A salesperson with skill but no integrity can win once, but a salesperson with integrity and authenticity can win for their entire career. In the long run, truth sells best.


Storytelling Ability

Storytelling in sales isn’t about being entertaining, it’s about connecting meaning to information. We see charismatic speakers or sales trainers and associate that skill to being tied directly to a certain level of sales performance but there is a difference between what they do and what is done in sales. A great salesperson doesn’t just describe what they sell, they help the buyer see themselves in the story. They use a narrative to translate logic into emotion and emotion into action. True storytelling in sales is structured with a clear beginning, middle, and end. It’s relevant where every story has a purpose that ties back to the buyer's situation. It’s emotional and evokes a feeling of curiosity, trust, relief, or confidence. And lastly, their story is authentic and drawn from real experiences not canned scripts. Ever have someone tell you a story you can tell is fake? Don’t do that. Don’t do it in an interview or to a prospective buyer or customer. It doesn’t work and your credibility is shot after that. 

Storytelling, in short, turns data into meaning and turns products into possibilities. In his book, Building a Storied Brand, Donald Miller gives a great example of how Steve Jobs learned the meaning and power of storytelling. He talks about how the last computer that Job’s team built before he was fired as CEO was called Lisa. Jobs took out a nine page ad in the New York Times describing in detail all of the amazing features of the Lisa computer and it fell flat and was deemed a complete failure. Jobs went to work with Pixar and learned how to tell the story the right way. When he came back to Apple he released a new marketing campaign. Instead of telling what the computer could do for you he created a story of what you could do or who you could be with an Apple product. He took out a one page ad with two words “Think Different” and hard launched a brand into success and that story is still being told today.

People don’t buy the best product, they buy the product they believe in the most. And belief is built through story. Stories bypass skepticism creating a narrative that feels safe and doesn’t feel like a pitch. Stories are more memorable and emotions stick but facts and data fade. Stories also create trust. When you share something authentic people will relate to you. In a crowded marketplace, stories differentiate you and how you communicate those stories matter much more than what you sell. Every great salesperson becomes a storyteller because they understand that the story is the strategy.

Great salespeople use short, purposeful anecdotes in order for their prospects or customers to see themselves in them and their problems getting solved. They understand how to create emotional tension and resolution using vivid language and pacing to pull people in. They instinctfully personalize stories and shift them depending on the audience’s goals or pain points. And finally they end their stories with insight and a takeaway that drives next steps. Their conversations flow naturally and they aren’t scripted and don’t feel forced. They are able to make complex solutions sound simple because they make them human and relatable.

Storytelling is crucial in an interview. Candidates who can use stories instinctively when explaining past roles, wins or struggles stand out more, leave an impact and stay remembered by their interviewing manager. They have a natural rhythm when speaking and pause, emphasize and engage during their conversations. Great storytelling candidates use emotion and context helping them convey their data and metrics in past roles. There also tends to be revelations of self-awareness in their storytelling making their audience feel something whether that be curiosity, empathy, or belief. Candidates who are overly polished or robotic in their storied answers will send up red flags.

There’s neuroscience in storytelling. Stories activate more areas of the brain than data and facts do. When you tell a good story, sensory, emotional, and memory regions of the brain all light up together. The audience that hears a great story not only understands the message they feel it! When a story is well told, trust chemicals (like oxytocin) increase, attention and recall go up dramatically and decision making becomes easier and faster. That’s not sales magic, it’s human biology. Storytelling is the bridge between logic and emotion and between information and influence. It’s how you reveal truth, connect authenticity, and inspire belief. And in sales, belief is everything. A salesperson with great storytelling ability doesn’t just sell products, they sell possibilities and we all buy it.

Process Orientation

Process orientation is a powerhouse trait for consistent, predictable sales performance. While storytelling and curiosity drive emotional connection, process orientation is what keeps that energy focused and repeatable. It’s the discipline that separates professionals from performers. It’s also why most salespeople need managers. A lot of salespeople struggle with process orientation and often need reminders from business leaders, sales managers and other sales roles within their organizations. 

What does process orientation really mean? Process orientation is the ability to think, plan and execute systematically, not emotionally or reactively. Process-oriented sellers see selling as a science as much as an art. They follow a structured path for prospecting, discovery, follow-up, and closing but within that structure they leave room for creativity and human connection. This concept is the company’s selling process or method that they have found works best (usually a data driven decision) for selling their products or services. Most well established companies have a process for their inbound leads and outbound sales and they should be able to explain it in an interview. It’s a question most people don’t ask but it’s very telling when it comes to what’s expected of you and your role. What any company is trying to do with their sales process is creating consistency out of chaos. Every action has intention. Every call, email, and meeting fits within a framework that leads to measurable results. This is the foundation you are looking for in your sales career. In short, talent wins moments. Process wins careers.

Sales is full of constantly moving parts, from leads, calls, demos, follow-ups, renewals, objections, strategy and emotions. Without process, even skilled reps will burn out in the noise. Process orientation provides clarity on what to do next and why it matters. Predictability with pipelines staying healthy because activity metrics are measured and kept consistent. They are scalable so leaders can identify what’s working and replicate it or what’s not working and get rid of it. It also provides focus in order for everyone in the organization to spend time on high-impact activities, not distractions. Big note here, process doesn’t limit creativity, it enables it by freeing mental energy for listening, storytelling and strategy. Top performers often appear spontaneous, but underneath that confidence is a rock-solid routine that drives results.

The best salespeople I’ve ever met were process driven through and through. They operated in a way that almost seemed robotic. They blocked time for prospecting, follow-ups, and pipeline reviews. Their CRM was clean, accurate and always up to date. They prepared for calls with agendas, goals and desired if not defined next steps. In other words, they always knew what they were doing next. They knew their metrics better than their managers not because someone was making them but because they were always looking for an edge and they knew it lied in the data. They were always refining their scripts, templates, and workflows to save time and increase their conversion rates. They knew their process so well that they could flex it when needed because the structure ultimately gave them control.

Candidates who want to show that they prefer a system over chaos will describe routines, frameworks or cadences when talking about how they approach their day to day life or previous sales roles. I look for people who are a fan of the process, not the results. Showing clear examples of how they track progress or measure success is also a great indicator of being process driven. They will use language like “process,” “systems,” “cadence,” “discipline,” or “workflow” when describing how they are able to accomplish things. They can also explain how they troubleshoot performance issues by self diagnosing the process and where they may have strayed. My favorite is when they talk about improvement in repeatable terms, not luck or personality. The common misnomer in sales is that process robs you freedom but it actually provides you precision under pressure. Process is a foundation for flow creating order from chaos, scalable success and maintaining consistency across markets. You’ll often hear about working smarter not harder, working within a process allows you to work with intentionality and the best intention beats intensity every time.

Time Discipline

Time discipline isn’t just about punctuality or working hard, it’s about intentional focus. It’s the ability to plan, prioritize and protect your time so that your daily actions align with your long-term goals. I would teach reps to be “violently protective of your time” and used the term “violently” very intentionally. Oddly, in sales you’re pulled in every direction that isn’t sales related. A system or process will have you block territory into some sort of optimization plan but not every manager or co-worker will respect it. And customers' problems or emergencies don’t follow your plan either. So it’s up to you as a salesperson to manage the chaos of your everyday sales life. Where most sales reps go wrong with their time management is the line they dance with honesty and setting up reasonable expectations. Salespeople have a natural people pleasing mindset so saying no is difficult. Not to mention the thousands of other distractions that occur everyday whether it’s job related or family related. Everything is seemingly pulling you everywhere but those cold calls you need to make. The other side to allowing things to distract you is psychological. Most people are risk adverse, your brain works overtime to protect you from doing the activities that it feels could put you at risk. Since sales or more specifically cold calling is a statistically failure oriented activity your brain will prioritize any other activity over cold calling. Cold calling is a muscle that atrophies nightly and without proper discipline to manage your time, you will fail.

A time disciplined salesperson treats time like currency because in sales, it is. They know where every hour goes, why it matters and what it produces. Legendary sales coach Jim Rohn said, “Amateurs spend time. Professionals invest it.”

Without discipline, it’s easy to be busy all day and still accomplish nothing meaningful. Time discipline creates the separation between top performers and everyone else. Great sales reps manage their time with discipline. It ensures they are spending developing their pipeline. It prevents burnout through structure and focus. They are able to maximize their output without increasing the hours they work. And the output of a time disciplined sales rep is that it builds a reputation for reliability and follow-through. Salespeople who are time disciplined can almost be spotted instantly. They have an established calendar that they consult before they agree to do anything. Time is blocked and protected. They arrive on time and end them with clear next steps for a scheduled meeting. They batch tasks like calls, emails, and proposals to reduce mental switching. They rarely reschedule because they planned their schedule realistically and appropriately. They are well prepared, reviewing their week and day proactively. They don’t chase urgent things, they plan important things. And here’s the big takeaway, because they value and protect their time, customers and colleagues do too.

Time discipline is one of the easiest traits to convey in an interview. Great candidates don’t have to show a calendar, it comes out in the way they talk about their habits. When candidates talk about their time they express clear structure while describing their day or week. They say things like, “I block my mornings for, and afternoons for…” You can see time discipline in the way they have ownership of their time management and how they control their time and that their time doesn’t control them. They talk about balancing their time with competing priorities and how they manage it effectively. You can be a victim or a victor of your time as well. Saying things like, “I just try to stay on top of things,” doesn’t make you seem like you’re a multi-tasker, it makes you seem disorganized. 

Top sales reps don’t just manage their time, they leverage it. They know the value of preparation, timely communication and prioritized follow-up. Speed and structure create trust and momentum. Time discipline is what transforms potential into performance, and chaos into consistency. It’s the habit behind every other habit, the foundation of preparation, follow-up and professionalism. A salesperson with a strong sense of time discipline doesn’t chase the clock, they control it and that control is the quiet superpower of top producers.

The best part of the list you’ve just read through, all of them except for two can be learned, developed and perfected. If you have ADHD you’ve found a home with sales and a level of exception for who you are. Your differences are your super power and can be maximized if given the right environment. Introversion also works well in this new process driven sales landscape. Sales is a field that can work for anyone as long as they are willing to work on their skills, abilities and their craft. You can’t be lazy in sales and be successful!

ADHD

Believe it or not ADHD has a home in sales. There are estimates out there that 30% of salespeople have ADHD. I’ve even seen some numbers where 38% of marketers, 30% of salespeople and 33% of founders have ADHD. Those are big numbers given that in the U.S. only about 4-6% of adults report a current ADHD diagnosis. Why does ADHD seem to flourish in sales? There are a lot of positive traits associated with ADHD including creativity, hyperfocus, high energy, courage, and adaptability. These people tend to think outside the box, become deeply engrossed in interesting, to them, tasks and seemingly solve problems for fun. There are some other things that help as well. Cognitive dynamism which refers to a combination of curiosity, drive, and the ability to switch between fleeting ideas and focused thinking. Strong interpersonal intuition or Emotional Intelligence (i.e. EQ). They are usually highly attuned to tone, emotion, and micro-reactions. These are all crucial for reading a buyer’s intent or mode during a conversation. Their natural humor also often makes them memorable and likeable two key drivers of trust in selling. It can also be a point of frustration between salespeople with ADHD and managers or operations folks. People with ADHD aren’t the neatest when it comes to their car or desk nor are they always the greatest at details which can hinder things like payment and installation dates. Salespeople require an ability to handle rejection in mass and people with ADHD have a rapid recovery rate from rejection. ADHD individuals often bounce back quickly because they’ve been adopting to feedback loops and setbacks since early in life. They also may have an ability to spot patterns other miss, reading situations holistically rather than linearly. In sales, this allows them to quickly size up customers, connect dots between needs and solutions, and pivot mid-conversation when a pitch isn’t landing. 

Active Listening

Everyone thinks that salespeople are good “talkers” but the best salespeople are actually amazing listeners. It’s one of the most vital and under-taught sales skills. It’s often confused for politeness or attentiveness, but in reality, it’s a strategic skill that drives trust, discovery, and influence. Active listening is more than hearing words, it’s processing meaning, emotion, and intent behind those words. In sales, this means listening to understand, not to respond. Great salespeople don’t just wait for their turn to speak, they are fully engaged in decoding what’s being said and what’s not being said. 

*Author's Note: As someone with ADHD myself, active listening has proven to be one of the most difficult skills to master. It’s not because I’m not listening I’m actually listening very intently it’s just that what was said triggered another thought. Another difficulty dealing with ADHD is that I think I already know what the person I’m speaking with is going to say. My brain jumps to the end of the sentence even though the prospect is only halfway through. It comes across as interrupting but I think I already know what they are going to say. It’s frustrating in all aspects of life but it can really hurt you in an interview and even more so when you’re actually selling. People with ADHD like myself have a difficult time controlling impulse or we fear we are going to forget the thought we just had. There’s a lot of good information out there on how to control those impulses and anxieties and if you have ADHD, I’d recommend you dig into that information. ADHD can be a super power especially when you learn to deal with some of the difficulties of having it and learn to keep it under control.

When you break it down, active listening requires three layers of attention. Context, what’s being said (their facts), why it’s being said (the situation) and emotion, how it’s being said (the feeling behind it). When you combine these, you uncover what the buyer truly needs, not just what they ask for.

Most salespeople listen to reply, the top performers listen to reveal! Active listening does some powerful things. It builds trust, which causes people to feel genuinely heard and that translates into them opening up and sharing more. That sharing will uncover the truth. Prospects often hide real pain behind surface level objections so gaining trust wins deals. Truly listening to your customers differentiate you from most reps as most reps “pitch” instead of seeking to understand. Active listening is the foundation of every great sales conversation. It transforms sales reps from talkers into translators, turning customers words into insight, insight into empathy, and empathy into action. You don’t need the best pitches to sell, you need the best ears.
 
A candidate who wants to demonstrate that they are an active listener will pause before answering a question. They tend to repeat and or reframe questions to fit their story. They make good eye contact, pace their speech and show patience in a conversation. They also follow up with great questions that show engagement and not self promotion. Taking notes will never hurt you either.


Introverts

The best salesperson I ever hired was a massive introvert. When he became a sales manager he tended to hire other introverts. I remember sitting in on one of their one on ones and I swear no one talked for 5 minutes. Being an extrovert, that meeting drove me up a wall. They hardly said anything to each other. It was so weird for me I thought they were playing a joke on me. Both of those guys were multiple time President Club winners. Introverts make great salespeople. They listen more than they talk which is vital to sales success. Introverts tend to process information carefully and let others speak. That means they really hear what the customer or prospect is saying. They hear their pain points, their motivations, and what they truly value rather than waiting for their turn to pitch. They tend to build deeper relationships as they prefer quality over quantity in their interactions and conversations. They have a tendency to prepare and plan more because they think before they act which makes them more methodical and disciplined. Introverts like their ADHD colleagues tend to be very empathetic and pick up tone, hesitation, and body language well. They are fantastic at follow up due to their propensity for written communication which translates well to thoughtful emails, messages and meeting recaps. Introverts don’t rely on charm or pressure tactics, introverts often come across as consultants rather than salespeople. They earn credibility by offering value, not volume and that trust often shortens the buying cycle. In high-stakes or enterprise sales, buyers value intelligence, strategy and listening, not flashy personalities. Introverts shine in these settings where the goal is to diagnose, educate and guide, not entertain.

What’s Interesting About Sales Today?

Is it possible to get out of the first chapter of a sales book written after 2025 and it does not include a part about AI? Nope and not this one either. AI and automation are redefining sales workflows and rapidly becoming a core part of selling rather than being just a nice to have. It’s being positioned as a co-pilot to sales reps helping with lead prioritization, automating repetitive tasks, personalizing outreach, and freeing reps to focus more on relationship building. That means for everyone in sales the skillset is changing. You should have a good idea on how to leverage AI tools to your advantage. Buyers, managers and organizations are constantly evaluating what “sales talent” looks like in the age of AI. You need to be confident and AI empowered and not worry about being tech replaced or tech averse. You can only control what you can control!

You’ll want to position yourself as tech fluent. Everyone knows that you're applying to be a salesperson and not a data scientist but you should have a good understanding of AI and more importantly what it can do for you. You might even want to include a positioning statement in your cover letter that infers that you’ve learned to leverage AI by saying “I combine technology driven efficiency with human centered selling.” You’ll also want to anticipate new interview questions around technology, automation of a sales process and any tools you use. At the end of any sales interview managers will hire the person who makes customers feel understood. Mark Zuckerberg said it best when he said, “AI is your leverage. Humanity is your advantage.”


Take The Tests

When someone asks me if they think they would be good as sales I give them a simple test to find the answer themselves. It’s probably best if you take this test now to see if sales is something that is right for you or if it's worth getting ready for a sales interview at all. Here’s the test. The next time you’re at a restaurant, store, or coffee shop, confidently and politely ask for 10% off of your total. Then, say nothing. Don’t explain it, justify yourself, or joke it away. Just smile and wait. And don’t just do it once, do it 5 to 10 times in a week. 

This single act tests several core sales muscles all at once. Comfort with discomfort is a must have when it comes to being in sales. Sales is full of awkward moments. You’re constantly putting yourself in situations where rejection is likely. If you can complete this 10% request without shrinking, apologizing, or laughing nervously, you’ve got the raw material for sales. It tests your confidence and presence by testing your ability to hold eye contact, stay calm, and project confidence. That same composure is what buyers respond to in a sales call. You’ll be able to test your rejection recovery. When someone says “no,” do you smile and move on, or do you shrink inside? Salespeople are expected not just to tolerate rejection, they metabolize it. It becomes data, not drama. If they do say yes, ask why. If they say no, ask what would make it possible. You start learning negotiation and empathy which are two big parts of selling. That curiosity and adaptability will do well for you and your sales career.

There’s a second test that I’d recommend you do and it involves interviewing people. Pick a product or service and go find a company that uses it. It could be a local mechanic shop that uses a payroll service, a small business that uses a credit card processing company, any restaurant using a uniform company, a realtor that uses a marketing agency, and so on with companies that use SEO firms, HVAC companies or security companies. It doesn’t have to be in a vertical that you’re applying for so look for something where you can just go have a conversation with a business that uses one of those services. Think of it like an interview. Ask why they selected them as a vendor. How they arrived at that decision. What did they say or do that earned them the business. What would you change? Was there a difference with what they sold you versus what they delivered? How was the onboarding process and changing vendors? What would make them switch to a different vendor?  Questions win deals in the field. Being able to walk into a business cold, knowing nothing, having a conversation and asking them business questions about current vendors is a fantastic way to jump in the deep end of the cold calling pool. 

You’ll learn a couple of things when it comes to these “tests.” The first is that the hardest door in sales to open is the car door. You’ll freak yourself out telling yourself that “this is dumb” and “why am I doing this” but that’s your brain trying to save you from rejection and keep you safe. Do it anyway. This is what sales is going to feel like, it’s good to have a dry run and build up your resilience. The second test will show you that people love to talk about their business and while everyone hates to be sold, they are more than happy to share their stories. People love to talk about their baby, and their business is that baby. Thirdly, being authentic and vulnerable go a long way into getting to the meeting and getting answers to your questions. You can even tell them that you’re thinking about sales and just trying to do an exercise. People love to help, they hate to be sold. Remember that in your sales career.

If you’re starting out in sales you need to know one thing. Nothing good is built off of a bad foundation. I’m always baffled at the responses I get to one of my favorite interview questions. The question is, “what do you do to get better at sales?” I usually have to expound on it a bit because it takes people back a step or two. They don’t know how to answer because they just realized that they should be doing something and they aren’t. So I ask, “do you read, watch YouTube videos, listen to any podcasts?” And still the answers aren’t great. Very rarely will I get someone who’s working on themselves to get better at sales. Fewer are reading books. People are waiting to be taught and not seeking out information. It’s tragic really. So what can you do to stand out? Read something related to sales, people or business. Find great podcasts and YouTube channels. In the second to last chapter of this book I’ll give you a list of resources I recommend to anyone who asks. Those resources will help you build an incredible foundation and start your sales journey off in the right direction.

The Job You Need Before The Job You Want

There’s a quote by Alex Morton that says, “Most people don’t want to be part of the process, they just want to be part of the outcome. But the process is where you figure out who’s worth being a part of the outcome.” That’s sales! The best advice I could give you as a salesperson is to fall in love with the process, not the result. Sales isn’t about what’s given to you, it's about what you can learn, what you can earn and leveraging both into new opportunities to do it all over again and again. But you have to start somewhere. Whether you’re starting out with no experience or starting over, sales will always provide you with an opportunity to provide for you and your family. You need to know that you are what's in process. You’re the work that’s being built. Learn to love consuming information. The fastest way from point A to point B is a good sales book, a podcast or a YouTube video that’s meant to get you better. Shortening the runway on your success is all about how hard you’re willing to work on getting you better. The education and experience you receive along the way is what’s going to get you where you want to go. If you’re starting out, understand you won’t start where at your destination you’ll most likely need to put in the time to get the job you want. Whether that’s management or with a high base pay you’ve got a lot to learn. Don’t be afraid to take the job you need and put in the time, you’re on your way to the job you want.

If you’ve gotten through this first chapter you’ve decided that sales is for you. I’m excited for you!  There’s one thing you need to do first if you haven’t already and you have to do it before you move to the next chapter.

Create your LinkedIn Profile

If you have a profile you want to make sure it’s working for you. When you look at someone else's LinkedIn profile it notifies them. I recommend that you toggle your settings in order to let them see that you’ve looked at their profile. This way they know who you are and you can see if they in turn look at your profile. Even if they have a “stealth” profile it should give some indication that they work for the company where the person’s profile says they work. 

Your LinkedIn profile is essentially your sales pitch to anyone who is looking ‘AT’ or just as importantly ‘FOR’ you. Your profile breaks down into five categories that you have to sell yourself.

The Banner at the top of your profile is your free billboard. It should be simple and focussed on you. That may look like your school, or what you’ve accomplished at school. AI or Canva is a great way to create something unique that’s tailored to your time and experience. Stick to a message that’s clear and you can even include a simple call to action. “I’m looking for a sales position that’s going to help me grow in my career. I’m a learner, competitive, and tenacious in my work ethic. Want to meet with me? Schedule a time to meet with me.” 
The Profile Picture should be professional and approachable. Use a high quality picture of your head and shoulders. A full body picture makes you invisible in the timeline and no one is going to click on your picture to see what you look like. Look at the camera and smile. Smiling makes you approachable. Keep the background simple and clear. 

The Headline that’s under your names should clearly explain who you are. If you don’t have a job, write what you want to do in this area. It should be keyword friendly and have things like, “Aspiring B2B, Outside Sales, or Sales and Marketing”

Your About section is where a lot of people get imposter syndrome and think that they need to ‘fluff’ up what they’ve done. The people looking for people like you know you haven’t done a lot in sales. They are looking for attitude, competitiveness and a willingness to learn. Highlight the roles you’ve had in a way that amplifies those things. 

The Featured section are your posts, likes and comments. Make sure you’re active on the platform. Follow companies you want to work for, sales influencers, and authors of books. Like their posts, comment on them and you should also be reposting those folks with your own thoughts. There’s a lot of content out there all you have to do is repost with what it means to you. Don’t worry about likes at this point in your career. You’re not trying to show your subject matter authority, you’re trying to show people looking to hire you are a student of the game.

The Experience section is about the results you’ve achieved not tasks you’ve completed. Highlight work, school groups, leadership positions or anything else that lends itself into communicating that you’re the right person for the job you’re looking for.

Your LinkedIn profile looks different after you get the job but at this point you’re selling yourself and it helps to have a consistent message that merchandises who you are and why you’re the right person to hire.

Now let’s get you ready to apply for that job! 














Chapter 2

Getting the Interview


I grew up in Pennsylvania in the 80’s and 90’s and as a family we never flew anywhere. When it came to traveling, if we were going anywhere, we were driving there. That included the time we took my brother to college in Texas. My step-dad was the type of person that if we were going on a trip, we were going to stop at the cool (what he thought was cool) places along the way. Looking at the map from Hershey, Pennsylvania to Longview Texas you won’t see a tiny town called Branson Missouri. That’s because it was about 200 miles completely out of the way. But there was absolutely no way my dad was going to miss this redneck paradise on earth. Certainly a measly 200 miles wasn’t going to stop him. 

I was 12 at the time and had no idea I was going to hear a life altering phrase uttered at a dinner concert by a genius of a man by the name of Lecil Travis Martin but sure enough I did. Lecil had a stage name and was known as Boxcar Willie in the country music world and was famous for singing in an “old-time hobo” music style complete with overalls and a floppy hat. He could also make sounds exactly like a train whistle and it was amazing! For the life of me I can’t remember any song he sang on that stage but as we waited in line to meet Mr Boxcar, a woman, a couple of people in front of us wanted her husband's picture with Boxcar. As the wife fiddled with the camera (a 90’s disposable one) Boxcar was getting a little impatient. After a little more of a delay, he pointed at her and said something I’ve never forgotten. He said, “lady, nothing happens until you push the button”. It was funny, he said it in good spirits and jokingly reminded her that there was a line of people behind her but those words hit me right between the eyes. It was a call to action and I think about it when I catch myself thinking too much and not moving in a direction of action. When it comes to applying for your job, nothing happens until you move and move you must. Throughout this application process and for that matter, your career, you will have to remind yourself to keep moving because that first call or email might not happen the way you wanted. It takes persistence on your part to stay after it. Push that button! You have to move! Action wins every time!

Let’s start with your cover letter and what’s important when you are writing one. A great cover letter is a sales pitch, NOT a formality! Most candidates treat it as a polite introduction; the candidates who get hired treat it as their first close. Here’s what separates a strong cover letter from a forgettable one. Think of your cover letter as your first cold email to the hiring manager, your goal is to get them to respond. 

You have to know your buyer. That’s the person hiring you and the company you want to work for. Before writing a word, research the company like you would a sales prospect. Start with the job posting, then to the company website. After that you’ll want to check them out on LinkedIn to see if you can find the hiring manager. Finally move to Glassdoor or Repvue to see what people are saying about working there. Keep in mind that not every rep is successful and their reviews may be more of a reflection of them and not the company. We’ll deep dive into the research later in this chapter. 

After you get an idea of who you’re writing to, take a look at the job description. Are there any clues as to what challenges they are facing as a sales organization? What are they looking for in a potential sales rep. When you understand what they are looking for and why, your cover letter shifts from “I want this job” to “I understand what you need.”

One thing I see on LinkedIn a lot are conversations or posts about Applicant Tracking Systems or ATS scores. Let’s address that real quick before you start your work on your cover letter and resume. Applicant Tracking Systems have taken on an almost mythical status in the job search world, especially online where candidates are told their resume is being “scored by AI” and rejected before a human ever sees it. The truth is a lot less dramatic. Most ATS platforms are not judging your worth as a candidate or assigning a universal score that determines your fate. Their primary job is administrative. The ATS collects applications, stores resumes, enables keyword searches, filters basic requirements (such as location, work authorization, or years of experience), and helps recruiters manage large volumes of applicants. In many companies, especially for entry level sales roles, a recruiter or hiring manager still reviews a significant portion of applications manually. The ATS is a filing cabinet with search functionality, not an autonomous hiring decision maker. 

However, ATS software can eliminate candidates, but usually only on a clear, objective basis. If a role requires a bachelor’s degree, a specific certification, or legal eligibility to work in the country, those filters may automatically screen out applicants who don’t meet them. Some systems allow recruiters to search by keywords, which means your resume needs to reflect the language of the job description. The idea that there is a single “ATS score” universally calculated across companies is largely a misconception. A few ATS platforms or add-on tools may rank candidates relative to a specific posting, but those rankings are internal, customizable and often only one input among many. More importantly, recruiters frequently override or ignore automated rankings when a candidate appears promising.

For candidates, the practical takeaway is simple. Don’t try to “game the algorithm,” focus on clarity, relevance and credibility. Use standard formatting so the system can easily sort through your information, mirror key terms from the job posting where they truthfully apply, and clearly demonstrate that you meet the basic requirements. Once you pass the initial gate, the process becomes very human driven. My reason for saying all of this about ATS’s is that a lot of folks looking to break into roles get sold a service that will help them “increase their ATS score.” Those are scams, please don’t fall for them.

Your Cover Letter

As you start your cover letter, don’t lead with a header, lead with a hook. Your first line must sell attention. Avoid, “I’m writing to apply for the Sales Representative position.” Instead, start with a results-driven or curiosity provoking line. Your first line isn’t meant to announce yourself, it’s to demonstrate relevance and fit. Hiring managers and recruiters look at hundreds of resumes in a week or month and like a viral video you only have a short time to grab their attention. 

Here are some examples:

With sales experience:

“Last quarter my team drove $3.5 million in new business by focusing on customer storytelling, the same approach that drives your company's mission.”

Without sales experience:

Show that you’ve naturally acted like a salesperson in other parts of your life.

“When I convinced my college to fund a student event that doubled attendance year over year, I realized how much I loved presenting ideas and getting buy-in, which is why I’m drawn to sales.”

That line tells a hiring manager that you’re able to persuade, communicate convincingly and follow through by executing. That statement is full of sales DNA.

Sales leaders love curiosity, competitiveness and drive. You can hook a reader by showing both upfront.

“I’ve spent the last six months reading books like Fanatical Prospecting, Selling from the Heart and New Sales Simplified along with listening to podcasts and practicing discovery calls with friends because I’m serious about breaking into sales.”

In the body of your cover letter you’ll want to demonstrate a proof of coachability and self-motivation. Those two things are like catnip for sales managers. Sales managers hate having to break bad habits or preconceived notions of what sales is. But they love someone who is humble enough to know they have to learn how to excel in their new role. I always tell my sales managers, "Look for someone hungry and willing to learn, it’s our job to teach them the rest!” 

Authenticity shows empathy and emotional intelligence.

“I’ve always been drawn to the challenge of earning trust quickly and winning through communication. After years of watching great salespeople at work, I’m ready to be one.”

A sincere and well written first sentence immediately connects your story with your reader. Most hiring managers appreciate honesty and enthusiasm over fluff.


Don’t just say you’re “driven,” “motivated,” or a “team player.” You have to show it. Replace adjectives that everyone else is using with specific metrics or examples. Hiring managers looking for their next superstars don’t want personality, they want proof.

With sales experience: 

“I increased client retention by 22%” is infinitely more powerful than “I’m great with customers.”

Without sales experience:

Show evidence of grit. Sales is a grind and every hiring manager knows it.

“I worked full time while finishing my degree in four years. Balancing deadlines, customer interactions, and 5 a.m. shifts taught me how to stay disciplined under pressure.”

It’s fine to show warmth, humor, or confidence, but anchor it in substance.

“I love connecting with people but what I really enjoy is understanding what drives them and helping them solve problems. That’s what sales is to me.”

“I volunteered to lead our school’s sponsorship drive, raised $5000 and learned that persistence beats persuasion every time.”

You’ll want to mirror the tone and words in the company’s language that they use on their website, job description, and any other company driven content you can find. Giving the impression that you’re a fit in your cover letter will give the hiring manager all of the warm and fuzzies they are looking for in a candidate they want to hire. If their culture emphasizes “collaboration and humility” your language should reflect that as well. If it's “fast-paced and high-growth,” show excitement and speed in your tone. Mirroring their language and tone sends a subtle signal that you are a match. 

You’ll read a lot in this book about “going for the close” and the truth is, not enough salespeople do a great job of asking for the business. You need to demonstrate from the beginning that you know how to ask for the business. In this case it’s the interview and ultimately the job. End your cover letter with a call to action, not desperation. 

With sales experience:

“I’d love the opportunity to show how my experience building referral pipelines could help your team hit your next quarterly targets. When is a good time for a short call?”

Without sales experience:

“I’m eager to learn from experienced professionals and apply what I’ve studied about B2B prospecting, outreach, and discovery. I’d welcome the chance to prove how quickly I can ramp up and contribute to your sales team.”

“What excites me most about B2B sales is learning how businesses make decisions and helping them find real solutions. I’d love the chance to start that journey with your team.”

Keep your cover letter tight and scannable. A mentor of mine by the name of Roy Cella used to tell me, “Be Brief, Be Brilliant, and Be Gone.” It’s a great way of thinking about your cover letter and it applies well in cold calling. The point is still the same. Book the next meeting. Your cover letter should get them excited about looking at your resume and ultimately scheduling a phone screen or in-person interview.

Your Resume

In my career I’ve hired a lot of teachers to be salespeople. They have an amazing amount of transferable skills. They are able to plan their schedules, time and territory well (lesson plans), they are incredibly organized and they are used to using multiple apps to communicate (CRM’s, Hubspot, Slack), they understand the concept of teaching when it's time to teach and doing admin at a later time (Selling when people are buying and admin work when they are on their own) and they have no problem presenting to large groups. If you’re new to sales your resume needs to focus on transferable skills, mindset, and proof of performance rather than direct results, especially if you don’t have results yet. 

Here’s how your resume should break down. Start with your full name, phone number, professional email address (oh, could I tell you some stories about horrible email addresses), then your LinkedIn profile url, and optionally you could include your city and state. I’d suggest including this if the job is in the same city you’re applying to. Stick to the city as a whole. Some sales jobs are split into territories and hiring managers will skip over your resume if you’re on the north side of a larger city and the territory they have open is on the south side. We have a saying in Atlanta, if you’re in Atlanta you’re still an hour and a half away from Atlanta. If I had a southern territory open but the rep was in the northern part it might be enough to exclude them from the search. Your goal is to get in front of them and sell yourself then win the territory, despite the travel time. 

Following the name and email is the professional summary or objective. This should be 2-3 sentences tops. As a hiring manager I know that your objective is to get a job at my company. You want that because you want money. Don’t waste your time writing that. If you write, “my objective is to get a job at your company in order to help them grow and attain goals personally and professionally.” You lost me already. A professional summary takes the place of the objective statement. Here’s an example:

“Motivated business graduate passionate about cold calling, uncovering and solving customers problems. Looking to begin a career in B2B sales where I can apply strong communication skills, a competitive drive and a growth mindset. I want to learn, grow and execute.” 

An objective statement like that tells me you have a grasp on the position you’re applying for and what it takes to succeed. It shows me you’re humble and have a ton of drive. Every sales manager wants someone who wants to learn how to win. Entry level sales doesn’t need someone who knows everything. A firm foundation is a great start. In entry level sales positions hungry and ready to learn could be all you need to get an interview. 

This is probably a good time to tell you too that you should nix the idea of including a picture. It doesn’t help and it takes up valuable real estate on your resume. There’s not enough ROI on a picture and it doesn’t make sense to include it. It should be present on your LinkedIn profile and that’s all you need.

Now you’re going to list your education. If you’re a recent graduate or student, this goes above the experience section. It should include the University name, city and state. The degree you obtained (Bachelor of Business Administration, Marketing, etc.), and graduation date or expected graduation date. Relevant coursework or honors are optional. Clubs and extracurriculars are a lot like numbers or KPI’s (Key Performance Indicators), they only matter if they matter to the situation. It should look like the following:

University of Your State - College Town, State
Bachelor of Science in Marketing | Expected 2026
Relevant Coursework: Professional Selling, Consumer Behavior, Business Communication

Then comes your work experience. Let me tell you, I always appreciate when I see work experience through college, whether that's on campus or off. It says a lot about who you are and what you’re willing to do to accomplish your goals. I respect it. Don’t omit your experience, be proud of it but be realistic too. 

I’m sure you have heard of the social media vs reality paradigm. The idea that highlights the contrast between the curated, often idealized versions of life presented online and the full, unedited reality of daily life. In the professional world it’s called “LinkedIn vs Reality” I’m sure you’ve seen it as “Instagram vs Reality" or insert any other social media platform and reality. 

Candidates tend to do this a lot with their resume. It’s not needed and comes off fake to the hiring manager. Worse case scenario when evaluating your resume, I have to figure out if you buy your own BS or if you're just trying to appear more relevant then you actually are. If you don’t have any experience that’s ok but when you say you are a “Client facing operations” and not someone who was waiting tables, I can’t tell if you’re lying to me or you’re lying to yourself. Once I see “Client facing operations” and then see the place you were employed and see Chili’s, it causes an immediate disconnect. It doesn’t work. After I see that and finish rolling my eyes I’m reading the rest of your resume with a certain degree of contempt. In Donald Miller's book, Building a Story Brand, he talks about marketing in a way of requiring your customer to spend too many mental calories on a website or marketing material. Putting too much fluff in your resume makes the reader spend too many calories trying to figure out who you really are.

There’s a line in describing what you do and you should try to be realistic with it and not cross it. Highlight the real stuff. Tell me why you had that job instead. “I worked in a restaurant because it was a job where I could make the most amount of money, that worked with my schedule, so I could continue to achieve my goal of graduating early. I started as a busser, moved to server in three months and then trained new servers in my last year.” It’s real, it’s authentic and tells me why you did what you did. There’s no shame in being a server, most of us have done it. We understand what it takes to be a good server, what you have to juggle and we also know how hard it is to work and go to school at the same time. When done right, you seem “normal” and hard working. Remember, you’re trying to be a fit in their organization. People don’t want to work with someone who pretends to be more than they are. 

When it comes to skills you should list a brief, concise bulleted list of hard and soft skills relevant to sales. Keep it to 6 items max. You should list, if true, “communication and active listening,” “time management,” “negotiating and problem solving,” and if you have any CRM experience with tools like Hubspot and or Salesforce. 

After skills, you should list any certifications and professional development. This is optional but if it applies to sales you should list it. Things like Hubspot Inbound Sales Certification, LinkedIn Learning modules, or other certifications show initiative and readiness to learn. If it’s applicable to the role, something is always better than nothing. If you don’t have any certifications, complete some while you are applying to openings. It will be time well spent.

Honors and Activities are optional but if you’re coming out of college this group of achievements shows that you were more than “just a student”. Sports and leadership positions even if they are intramural will help. Memberships in fraternities or sororities and leadership positions you held in those organizations are helpful as well. Showing participation is one thing but if you can show a pattern of gravitating to leadership positions it says a lot about who you are and how you apply yourself. GPA’s and dean list recognition are good but you have to be careful how you state it. You’re thinking about standing out from the crowd but if the hiring manager has a preconceived notion of what a person with a 4.0 GPA and Dean's List recognition is you may be putting up obstacles unintentionally. It is something you can talk about later during an interview if they ask. In 15+ years of interviewing salespeople…I’ve never asked. 

Baseline formatting should follow standard guidelines. One page only, especially for entry level candidates. A clean, modern font like Calibri, Arial and Helvetica at a 10pt to 12pt are all acceptable. Margins should be 0.5 to 1 inch and it should be emailed or uploaded as a PDF to ensure your format is preserved. Keep the design minimal with clean lines, no pictures or graphics and avoid templates with columns. Eliminate the noise. Noise doesn’t get a second look.

Opportunities And Places to Apply

If you’re a recent college graduate with little to no sales experience, there are several reliable places you can look to find real entry level sales opportunities. Creating a hot list of places to apply too is fairly easy these days. AI is the quickest way to crowdsource information. Here are two great prompts to start your search. There are a lot of AI engines you can use, for example, I used ChatGPT when creating these prompts and got fantastic results.

“Can you tell me what B2B sales organizations are great to work for with great training for salespeople with no experience?”

“If you were to recommend a “top 10 Guaranteed training grounds” for someone looking for a B2B sales job with no experience what would they be?”

“Can you expand that top 10 list to a “top 20 or 30” list?” 

After you create a list you’ll want to go to job boards that specialize in these sales or early career roles. Look for opportunities in your area within that list of companies. Keep in mind from that list you’ll also want to check out any of their competitors.  

Outside of the AI prompts there are some other ways to cultivate a list of potential employers.

The best time to start a LinkedIn profile is when you’re in high school, the second best time is today. You should have that LinkedIn account not just for your career and future opportunities to be recruited but because LinkedIn has a very robust job board. Log onto LinkedIn and click on their “Jobs” tab and then refine your search by clicking “Entry Level” + “Sales”.  You can also use LinkedIn to look at each company created by your AI prompt. 

Repvue is another fantastic site and I’d recommend following their CEO Ryan Walsh on LinkedIn. He has his finger on the pulse and offers fantastic insight into the sales world. Repvue was launched in early 2020 and it’s specifically for sales professionals and covers sales organizations and pay. They also have a large job board that’s easy to use. 

Indeed and Glassdoor are owned by a parent company called Recruit Holdings. The two operate as sister companies within the same parent organization. Indeed is great for high volume hiring companies like telecom, insurance, logistics and staffing. Glassdoor is helpful for reviews and compensation but also has a job board. Glassdoor and Indeed allow you to not only apply but also upload your resume for employers to look at.

Each sales opportunity you find should be vetted through all of these sites. As a recent college graduate I wouldn’t recommend a sales opportunity that doesn’t have a base pay. Please be careful when looking into these jobs. If it smells fishy, it probably is.

Secondly look for companies that are constantly hiring entry level employees. Some industries are more than willing to hire people with zero to minimal experience. Most of these companies offer training, high hiring volume, beginner friendly compensation and fast promotion paths. Tech / Sales (SDR/BDR roles) may not be the easiest to come by; it doesn't hurt to apply. Insurance and Telecom companies are always looking for agents and representatives. Make sure the positions you’re applying for are outside B2B sales. Retail sales in these industries provide fantastic careers but B2B is the focus of this book. Staffing and Recruiting firms as well as Logistics and Freight companies provide a lot of opportunities for employment and fantastic places to work. Merchant services, uniforms and home services like pest control and roofing round out that list.

Thirdly, is the commercial service businesses that need salespeople but they don’t advertise loudly. With the increase of Private Equity investment into home service businesses the commercial service industry is a perfect place to start your career. These can be heavy cold calling sales jobs that will get you recruited quickly for other roles. It’s companies like these you’ll get a solid foundation for your sales career. Heavy cold calling isn’t easy but you’ll shorten your runway by getting a lot of experience quickly. You’d want to look at commercial service companies like janitorial services, plumbing, HVAC, uniforms and waste services. Franchise businesses like restoration, cleaning and food service will have a smaller family feel but often still offer a rather robust training program. Larger manufacturers also offer opportunities to start in their inside sales departments and allow you to work your way into large account management roles. 

Most grads skip their college career centers however that tide seems to be turning as schools increase their engagement with students entering the workforce and measure student placement into careers. Schools have partnerships with employers actively seeking new grads and there are always alumni with open sales roles. Career fairs are also an overlooked opportunity for entry level salespeople. If you think about it, you have employers who know exactly what they are getting, college grads! If they know what they are getting, you should be there! I’d even recommend attending job fairs your sophomore and junior year. It’s an experience and you don’t want the time that you really need to land that job be the first time you’re walking into that room. Let me let you in on a little secret that I shared with my kids. Go to every career fair you can, even if it’s for accountants or engineers. Each of those companies has a sales department and most of them are hiring. Career fairs are a “nothing happens till you push the button” moment. You won’t know unless you go. Having a candidate go to a career fair that’s not for them and cold call a recruiter for a different role is someone I’d hire in a heartbeat. It shows me you have guts and ambition and I like that. 

Connect on LinkedIn with as many recruiters at these job fairs as you can and communicate with them. Ask for advice.Respect their time but most people love to help someone with the right attitude. Recruiters earn commission when they place people in roles, they want to place people. You reaching out to them and communicating with them keeps you top of mind (personal marketing) and potentially placed even sooner. Plus, you’re building your network. Salespeople should treat recruiters like they treat servers in a restaurant. With respect and appreciation. You never mess with people who bring you your food or could potentially affect your career. Not every opportunity is the right one for you but you want them thinking about you not hating you. These recruiters will move up in their roles too and keeping that relationship alive over years will only benefit your career opportunities in the future. 

Networking events at your school have to be one of the best opportunities for any college student to jump the line when it comes to getting hired. Any group that you can join or be a part of that has guest speakers, alumni mentors and or donors for the school is an opportunity that can create a relationship that could last a lifetime. These meetings aren’t meant just for the best of the best students and regardless of GPA you need to leverage these opportunities to increase your standing. The connection opportunities give you, the student, the opportunity but more so it gives the guest, mentor or donor a chance to be a part of your story. They love that! They love being able to go back and help and missing those meetings is missing out. 

The Deep Dive Research

For every job opportunity that you are going to apply to you should do a deep dive researching them. This is the research that will actually get you the job. The deep dive should be conducted on the sales jobs that you WANT to get. There should be about fifteen to twenty jobs that are on your “I want this job” list. Each of these opportunities should be vetted for things like culture and an incredible training program. 

That list of fifteen to twenty jobs should have some “safe jobs” that are in the reasonable category and attainable. SaaS and Pharmaceutical companies are great targets but may require some experience. Some of the “safer jobs” are companies that sell payroll, HR, recruiting and uniforms. In sales, we think of these “safer jobs” as the job you need before the job you want. These jobs are going to lay the firm foundation every sales rep needs to succeed in their career. These “safer” businesses are always in customer acquisition mode, which means they are constantly hiring, have incredible training in order to get new sales reps up and running as fast as possible and they are looked at as hotbeds for talent when it comes to recruiters and talent acquisition. 

You should know that companies in this “safer jobs” category, do have a higher than normal churn rate but that’s not all bad. Some people fail in these sales roles and that’s a part of the turnover but these companies also promote people fast internally and they get recruited from all the time. In the end, it’s about the training, the opportunity for promotion and getting recruited by a company willing to offer you more and after all isn’t that what you want? 

After you have generated a list of fifteen to twenty companies you should create a “I Want This Job” spreadsheet and then create individual tabs labeled with each company you want to apply for. There is a fully designed spreadsheet in the companion book Now Hiring Salespeople - The Field Manual. If you want to create your own spreadsheet you should create one where each tab should be labeled with the company name you’re applying to. On it you will want to include the hyper links to their LinkedIn page and their home page of their website. You’ll also want to list the local address and phone number of the location nearest you. You’re going to create this list of opportunities much like you would a target account info sheet. You’ll want to also create space for any recent company news, Glassdoor score and decision maker information. That decision maker information should have space for “recruiter.” “hiring manager,” and “general manager.” 

Glassdoor is a fantastic place for research. You’ll want to go to Glassdoor.com and click on “Companies.” Then go to the middle of the page and in the “Have an employer in mind?” field, enter the name of the company and click “search”. You may have to click on one of several listings returned from the company search. Usually the one with the most number of reviews is the one you want to look at. Once you select the company, copy the hyperlink onto your research tab. Do this for each company on your list.

There are several places to search on Glassdoor with the company you are researching on the company tab. The “Overview” is more marketing from them telling you who they are. Keep in mind that not every company properly owns their Glassdoor page nor communicates well on it but a well established company should have a pretty good grip on their communication. Most people go to Glassdoor for their employee “reviews” but these can be so drastically different from one another. What really matters in big companies is the local manager and you won’t be able to tell that from Glassdoor. Reviews can change drastically and large companies will even make efforts to increase their score by having loyal longer tenured employees give reviews. It’s a marketing effort on their part and a concentrated and tactical effort on the part of their talent acquisition team. 

The “salaries” tab is also good to review. Not only to get an idea of base pay and upside or on-target earnings (OTE) potential of the position you are applying for, but I also like the salaries tab so you can correlate career progression with earning potential at the same company. Seeing what you would make as a salesperson, manager and upper management gives you a great picture of what you’re working so hard for.

Glassdoor is a great resource for information but I think RepVue is even better. They position themselves as a sales professionals best friend.  When it comes to information about the actual job, the company forecast, company ratings and opportunities in the sales field it really doesn’t have a competitor. It also rates on target earnings (OTE) and percentage of reps attaining that number. Like Glassdoor, RepVue does require a company rating before it will share much information. In order to maintain access to RepVue’s data, it does require you to review your sales company every 120 days in order to keep its data fresh. While it might be tedious for users it does keep data fresh and fresh data is the best data.

RepVue doesn’t seem to have a way to access data if you haven’t had a previous sales job. It’s definitely an improvement they should make to get early adoption to their platform (email sent to their CEO). However a great workable solution to this in the meantime is to find a friend of yours who is in sales and do a review for them. I’d hate for anyone to submit a fake or bad review and mess up the data so finding a friend or relative to walk through a review should be ok until they figure things out on their end. 

After you fill out your “I want this job” spreadsheet and each company's designated tab with all of the hyperlinks from LinkedIn. Read through the company’s “About Us” page and get an idea of the vernacular they use when talking about their company. There should also be a link on their LinkedIn page to the company’s website and you should check that out as well. There’s three or four places on their website that you should review. First is the “Careers” page.

Most companies that are constantly hiring will have a pretty robust “Careers” page and those pages are designed to get you to want to work for them. Read through this page and copy the address and create a hyperlink on your spreadsheet. The “Careers” page is the messaging to you, the candidate. It’s designed with information to get you to want to work there. You should see a lot of the same information that you saw on their Glassdoor page.

Then navigate to the “About Us” page. Approximately 50% to 52% of website visitors go to the  “About Us” page of every website that’s visited. That means their “About Us” page is the message they want to send to their customers. There’s a lot of great information on this page and you’ll want to look at the messaging they are trying to send to their customers. You’ll most likely see the company history (their story) and an idea of their size in regard to the number of employees and customers. Your spreadsheet should have a hyperlink to this page as well.

The third page to review is the “Newsroom,” “Press,” or “Company News” page. This is the messaging they publish themselves or that is published about them to the press. This page will include company initiatives, shareholder news, charitable causes and drives, and any awards that they have earned. You should look through the last three months of information just to have an idea of who the company is and any initiatives that might be relevant in the interview. Most hiring managers are impressed if you just go to their website, reading through the company's “Press” page will blow them away!

The last page to review can be very informative and give you some assurance as to the financial viability of the company. The “Investor Relations” page is the message they want their shareholders and investors to see.  You should be able to see their outlook for the future, vision and what they tell their investors is important to them. For public companies you will see financial disclosures, cash flow, balance sheets and dividend information. You may even see quarterly call recordings that have information about future plans. This information isn’t going to move the needle too much for you but knowing that the company is going in the right direction is comforting. 

After you have looked at the company's website you’ll want to go back to LinkedIn. Look to see if there is a hiring manager or internal recruiter assigned to the job post. LinkedIn is the best way to find out who the decision maker for the role is. You have to understand that much like sales, there’s usually more than one person who’s going to make the decision.

In larger companies there is most likely an internal talent acquisition team. It’s a toss up on who is managing their Applicant Tracking System (ATS). It could be the sales manager or an internal team. 

Finding the front line hiring manager is your best bet. Go to the company’s LinkedIn page, click on “People” then click on “Where they live,” and click on “ + Add” and type in the city that you’re applying to. You can also add “What they do,” but I’d suggest you keep the initial search broader. That should give you some idea for who the sales manager is in that territory. If you’re going to click on their profile you can decide at that time if you want to do it publicly so they can see who you are or in private. I liked it when someone I was interviewing looked me up on LinkedIn. It showed they were doing their research but that’s up to you to decide. 

I would make a note on my worksheet of each person that is designated to the location in my area. They could be in Administration, Sales or Management roles. Obviously the Sales Manager and Location Manager are important to you being hired and the positions you want to make an impact on but there are a lot of other folks in that building who could play a big role in helping you get a job there. Every administrator that has ever worked for me had an incredible “gut” feel towards people. They could read people like crazy! I trusted their opinion. 

Looking at individuals' LinkedIn is one thing. It will definitely give you an idea of their professional journey and experience but that’s not where you’ll make the connection to them as an individual. Tenure at their current position will give you an idea of what you’re walking into. Are they new in their role? Have they moved up in the company? Those are the things you can glean from a quick look.

But the meat and potatoes of your LinkedIn research, when it comes to finding out more about the actual person who may be interviewing you, is their activity. You have to make sure that you look at their actual activity on the platform. What are they using LinkedIn for? Look at what they are posting, liking and engaging with. Are they sharing success stories of their team? Do they engage with other company employees? Are their pictures of the team training or doing any offsite activities? It’s a great glimpse into who they really are, what they value and how they engage with their team. You can see what posts they are liking and what seems to matter to them. Do you see any engagement with other companies, other managers, books they like or recommend. Imagine being in an interview and bringing up a book they posted about on LinkedIn. What would that do for your connection with that person? That’s a real opportunity for you to be able to connect with them. You may have heard the saying that people buy you when it comes to sales, the same is true for hiring!

LinkedIn activity provides a real look into who these managers are, what they value and how plugged in they are as a manager. It’s also a good thing if you see them liking and commenting on some of their past employees and celebrating their new roles with other companies. Great managers are great leaders and the good ones merchandise their work and cheer on their people. Most sales managers and salespeople are using LinkedIn. It’s where they prospect and it’s also where they merchandise their work and show others what type of manager as well as where they merchandise themselves to recruiters in order to progress their own career. Be sure to make notes on your spreadsheet with any takeaways from their activity.

Connecting and messaging people who work where you want to work is where research meets action. Introducing yourself to that admin person via LinkedIn and asking for their help in navigating the process could be the start of what gets you hired. A casual “Hey this person reached out to me about working in sales” conversation between Admin and Sales Manager isn’t the secret to getting you hired but it could keep you top of mind especially when you have looked at them both on their LinkedIn and they recognize your name. Your name is piquing interest at this point if anything by just being outside the box and persistent. And every sales manager knows outside the box thinking and persistence are what wins in sales. If you’re willing to do it to get the job, you’ll be even better at it when they pay you to do it! 
Here’s what that email would look like to that Admin person:

“To (Administrative person),

I was wondering if you could help me. I’m looking at applying to the sales position your location currently has open. Is there anything I could do as a candidate to stand out? (A bonus question would be) Maybe you could tell me what great sales reps do to succeed at your company?

Thanks so much for your time, your help is greatly appreciated.” 

Admins may not always be on LinkedIn but it can’t hurt to try. Asking only two questions allows you to start a conversation. Reaching out to individuals like this is a lot like reaching out to prospects when you’re selling. You don’t want to verbally vomit on them. You want your initial outreach to allow them to respond. You’re not pleading your case, you’re starting a conversation. 

It’s important to know where you are in the process. You don’t want to be presenting when you’re still in discovery mode. And discovery mode is right where you are. Start the conversation and have a couple of additional questions pertinent to the role when and if they respond. Keep any contact you make updated on your spreadsheet. It’s one thing to start a conversation, it's an entirely different thing to sustain a relationship. 

Make a note on your spreadsheet with any people you contact and have a conversation with, the date and any follow up needed to keep the conversation going. Even if you accept a job later on, follow up with these individuals. They took the time to connect with you and help out. You never know in the future how you can help them or they can help you. Relationships will always be the foundation of your success, nurture them.

I learned a long time ago that if I’m struggling to make any headway in an organization, talk to someone in sales. Salespeople don’t mind taking some of their time (especially if they should be prospecting or making dials) to help others. Salespeople appreciate good sales mojo and helping you could deliver that for them. There’s a couple of ways to go about this. First, reach out on LinkedIn. On LinkedIn it will tell you if they are in their “current role” or if it was a past role. Both of these folks could have good information for you.  Here’s what an email to them should look like;



“To (Current Sales Employee), 

I was wondering if you could help me out. I am looking at applying to an open role there at XYZ Company and I had a couple of questions I was hoping you could answer. Would you be open to a 5 minute phone call or would it be best to keep emailing?

Thanks so much for taking the time. I appreciate the help.”

“To (Past Sales Employee),

I was wondering if you could help me. I am looking at applying to XYZ Company and in my research I saw you were once employed there. Would you be willing to answer a couple of questions for me? I’d love to get your opinion on some things.

Thanks so much for taking the time. I appreciate the help.”

A couple of things here, first be brief, be brilliant and be gone plays a huge part in emails. No one cares about some massive personal story and the less mental calories someone has to spend getting through your questions the better. Make it easy for them to read your email quickly and answer you quicker. Keep it simple and to the point. Secondly you’ll want some good questions to ask if and when they answer your LinkedIn email. 

Your second email to either past or current employed should be even shorter.

“Would you prefer to keep these questions to email or would you prefer a phone call?”

You want to give people the opportunity to dictate how they communicate with you and some of those you contact may not want to put things in writing. 

I was being recruited for another company and as I was researching it I found someone who used to work at the same company I work for in the past. My email to him was four sentences long. I said, “We are both XYZ Company alums so I thought I would reach out. Can you tell me a little bit about ABC Company? I was contacted by their PE regarding their open position but I’d love to get some background from someone who’s been there. Any info you can share with me would be great.” I got a response from him in less than four hours and we had an hour long phone call talking about the company. 

Obviously this is a bit different than a cold outreach email. However, it does lend itself to the validity and power of reaching out. The information from that person was priceless. By the end of the call, I was able to speak the same language that the company did and reference problems they had that made me appear insightful and understanding. It also gave me enough information to tell me it wasn’t a great bet and probably not the right fit for me. 

When reaching out via LinkedIn doesn’t gain you any ground. Turn to the phone. That’s right, the phone. And as a sales leader, the sooner you get rid of your fear of picking up the phone the faster you will be on your way to succeeding in sales. You can’t be afraid to pick up the phone to get a job that’s going to have you on the phone all the time. Embrace the phone! Use it! Thank me later…

The phone is known for causing a lot of fear and trepidation in cold callers and I get that. But don’t let fear get in the way of accomplishing your goals. Despite what all the experts say, the phone isn’t dead. People still pick up cold calls and meetings are still booked quite successfully over the phone. As a salesperson, I still pick up the phone and take cold calls. My wife hates it but I love hearing people's sales pitches. Some have been so good, I’ve tried to hire the sales rep making the call. And if the majority of people believe the phone is dead it makes it the mode of communication that people are doing the least and therefore the one with the least traffic. Pick up the phone and make the call!

I would reach out to one role specifically when it comes to making a phone call, SALES. Call the main phone number on the local google maps page and ask to be transferred to the sales department. It’s important not to lie or misrepresent yourself but ask to speak to a salesperson. Introduce yourself to the salesperson and let them know that you are applying to the open role. These folks have a lot of information that can be very important to you, after all, they got the job you want.

Here’s how my call would go with them:

“Hi, my name is Steve Richey and I’m thinking about applying to the open sales role at your location. I was wondering if you could answer a couple of questions for me? Do you have a couple of minutes? 

Is the same manager there that hired you? What did the process look like? Can you tell me what the interview was like? Were there any outside the box questions you weren’t prepared for? What would be the best way to nail the interview? Is there anything that I could say or do to stand out?

What is it you like about working at XYZ company? How was the training? How long was it? Do they do continuous training? What do people do to be successful there? What are some of the things you wish you knew about the position?” 

You should walk away from that phone call with a lot of information. Salespeople are usually the ones in any organization willing to be blunt and brutally honest about how that particular company treats their salespeople. 

Most people avoid the phone because they are scared. Not from the call itself but the repercussions that could occur from saying the wrong thing. The fight or flight response triggers, the blood rushes out of your brain and it feels like you’re talking in slow motion. It’s not because you’re bad at the phone, it’s because you’re not comfortable. And let me help you, the first call is going to suck. Who’s great at anything without practicing? It’s ok. The brutal truth is it’s ok. You have to realize that the only person emotionally invested into that phone call is you. The person on the other side isn’t even going to remember you? Don’t believe me? Tell me the last five people who have cold called you and what their names were and what companies they were calling for. See, you aren’t emotionally invested either, and you don’t care. Get on the phone, and get comfortable being on it, it will set you apart in your career against people afraid to use the phone!

One more thing you could do to research or set yourself apart from others applying. Cold call them…I said it. Cold call the business you are applying to. It works! First and foremost, it shows your potential employer that you aren’t afraid to cold call anyone. I’ve known sales reps that specifically get hired because they cold called the business they were applying to. For hunter roles this works extremely well. 

And if you’re wondering, I’ve done it. I even did it when I was being recruited for a VP of Sales Role.  I cold called the local business, introduced myself to him, told him who I was, what position I was interviewing for and asked if he had anything to share. I wanted to see what the company was like, how the culture was, and what kind of relationship the franchisee had with the franchisor. I took a shot, cold called the company and ended up speaking with that franchisee for more than two hours. He told me everything! When I got home that day and told my wife what I did, she asked, “are you worried the people interviewing you will find out you did that?” I said, “If they care that I did that then I don’t want to work there.” 

Turns out, they found out. In the final interview I met with the CEO and the first thing he asked me was, “did you cold call one of our franchise locations?” and I said, “I sure did” with the confidence of someone who may have done something incredibly brilliant or stupid depending on their reaction. And he said, “Man, I love that!” A year or two after I worked there that same CEO was still telling people stories about how I cold called a location during my interview. He said, “someone who’s willing to do that will cold call anyone. I love that about him!” 

Don’t be afraid to cold call people who are going to ask you to cold call as a part of their job description. They will most likely love you for it and will immediately know that you can easily do what they are going to want you to do. 

You’ve got the framework for your customizable cover letter, your resume completed and now you have your spreadsheet and information to get you the interviews. You should be ready to connect to your intended employers and even cold called them to get on their radar. All you have to do now is what Box Car Willie would want you to do. Push the button! Get started!










































Chapter 3

Media Training

I confidently walked back to my seat and sat down after giving my first speech in my college Public Speaking class. I thought to myself, “I nailed it”, I had personality, charisma, made eye contact and even smiled at the right times. I hit every point in my outline and I thought I was better than most of the students in the class that had already presented. That is, until I made eye contact with my professor in the back of the class. As I started to sit down still looking at him, he quietly held up four fingers on each hand. I thought, “forty-four?” There's no way I got a forty-four on that. How could he be failing me after that speech? I just nailed it. Was he even watching? I sat there and fumed for the rest of the class and as soon as class was dismissed I headed right for his desk. He hadn’t even made it back to his desk yet but I was going to give this guy a piece of my mind. As he walked up to me I said, “There’s no way you’re giving me a forty-four on that speech.” He said, “I’m not, you got an eighty-three.” An eighty-three? I still didn’t think an eighty-three was fair. “So what’s with the forty-four?” as I held up the same four fingers on each hand mimicking the hand gesture like he had done to me. He pointed at my hands and said, “That’s the number of times you said the word “um” during your speech. FORTY-FOUR? I couldn’t believe it. “forty-four?” I said back to him questioning his ability to count. He was a speech professor after all, not a math professor. He said, “Yup, forty-four” I didn’t hear myself say it one time. How on earth did I get to forty-four? He could tell I was passionate about it so he asked me if I would like to watch my five minute speech with him. “You bet” I said defiantly wanting to prove him wrong. We watched it, and he was wrong. I said “um” forty-five times. Everytime I heard myself say “um” I cringed. It was painful to watch. At the end of watching myself I was saying way worse things in my head than “um”. I had the speech down but I didn’t account for the stress of the situation. I didn’t practice out loud, I didn’t understand that saying it in my head and saying it out loud or while standing in front of my peers would affect me so much. 

Media training is the process of preparing individuals to communicate effectively and confidently in high-stakes situations. You normally get this training when you have to deal with journalists, be on camera, or in public forums. You practice like crazy. You get put in situations where you are asked off the wall questions and then they are repeated or reframed and asked again. The practice is stressful and it's done in a way to make the practice way worse than the actual situation. That is what you should be doing. Want to know what makes professionals who they are? It’s practice. 

If you aren’t practicing with someone asking you questions then you aren’t training to get the job you want. It’s really hard to say that you want something and then not put the work in. If I told you I wanted to get skinny, you’d tell me to eat better and go to the gym or at least walk everyday. But when I didn’t eat better, or work out at all and still expected the weight to drop, you'd say I was crazy. Well, if you don’t put the work in to get better at interviewing, if you don’t take the time to get comfortable being uncomfortable, you’re the crazy one.

It’s chemical too…in interviews, just like cold calling, discovery and presentations, your fight or flight response is triggered until the activity is normalized in your brain. It’s a physiological response that occurs in response to perceived danger or stress. And trust me, you will feel this in an interview. Your heart rate increases, your breathing quickens, you start getting tense as your muscles tighten and worst of all, you start sweating. The blood rushes out of your brain because your body has decided that you’re done thinking and you’re either going to punch the person interviewing you or run out of the room. 

When that blood rushes to your muscles and leaves your brain it will feel like you’re talking in slow motion. I always refer to the movie Old School when Will Ferrell shoots himself with a tranquilizer dart. He starts talking in slow motion and can’t put his thoughts together. People who aren’t practiced go through the same experience. It’s tough to watch and tougher to go through and it’s one hundred percent avoidable.

It happens in interviews all the time. Fight or flight is your body’s natural, automatic response to stress or danger. Read that last sentence again. It’s natural for you to respond that way and the only way you can prevent it from happening is to practice. Everyone feels that fight or flight response, but the more you feel it and expose yourself to it the better your body will process the physiological response. It won’t feel like it has to run away because now it’s a normal thing to go through. That’s what you’re practicing for.

When you get that first or second sales job you’ll role play with your boss or a senior rep. They do this so you don’t freeze, punch someone or run out of the room when you’re cold calling or presenting demos. They know you need practice and they put you through it. That’s what you want to do. Why not be a pro when you walk in the door? And all it takes is practice.

Find someone to practice with. Put yourself in stressful situations. Get comfortable being uncomfortable. If any situation allows, dive in the deep end to something new. It can lead to quicker progress than a tentative approach. My favorite advice to give people who want to get really good at sales, join an improv group. The tenants of improv run in a direct parallel with sales. Try it!

Practice the art of being uncomfortable and also make sure to take the time to observe how you are feeling while doing it. You’ll need to be able to manage your emotional and physical responses so firewalking the uncomfortable experiences will only get you ready to be as relaxed as possible in an interview. 

You have to remember when you’re doing these things your brain is hardwired to keep you safe. Your brain, consciously or unconsciously, wants to protect you. In sales we say that the hardest door to open in sales is the car door. That’s because you will actively talk yourself out of doing the activities you need to do to succeed. That includes practicing. Your brain is trying to keep you comfortable and it’s activity to a minimum. 

Your brain creates routines for you to follow. Your morning routine is probably the same, just like your drive to class or work. The neural pathways are well worn and that’s all because your brain likes routine. Ever have that “how did I get here?” moment after driving for a couple of minutes? You suddenly alert yourself that you weren’t paying attention? That’s your brain in a routine environment. Conversely when you go to a new place or new area or sleep somewhere new you feel more alive and you take in everything around you. It’s energetic and fun, but your brain hates it because it’s constantly searching for homeostasis. 

And that’s what happens when you think about practicing interviewing or cold calling in your sales job and your brain hates the unknown. There’s only one way to make the unknown known. You have to normalize it, internalize it and you only get there by practice and familiarization. You have to find your own override switch.

Asking a friend, relative or even classmate is going to feel weird but you have to do it. You have to practice out loud and you have to practice with someone. There’s also a  good chance there is someone you know who’s already in sales, I’m sure they would love to help you practice. 

Here’s what I would do to practice with someone. Go to ChatGPT and ask specifically, “what questions does XYZ company,” from your spreadsheet of target companies. “ask in an interview.” Have your practice partner ask you those questions and any other questions they might want to ask. Record yourself with your phone and see how you look and how you present yourself. 

Videoing does two things for you. The first is it allows you to feel the pressure of being recorded and the second is that you can actually see what you are really like and how you present yourself. Watch the video. Have fun with it and don’t be afraid to laugh at yourself. Making it funny will normalize it and you’ll feel less pressure. And keep practicing.

 
Be Brief, Be Brilliant and Be Gone

Be Brief, Be Brilliant and Be Gone is my advice to sales reps when they are making cold calls. In phone calls, emails or meetings, don’t say twenty things when three will do. I say this to reps in training because I want them to remember the point of the cold call. It’s not to sell something, it’s to set an appointment. They have to sell the appointment and that’s all. The point of selling isn’t talking, it's listening. In this scenario you are selling yourself.

In an interview you should conduct yourself in the same way. Not to say you shouldn’t say what you need to say for the sake of trying to say less nor should you make the interview uncomfortable by being oddly quiet but you want to stay on point. When you’re asked a question just answer the question. 

For example, one of the questions I like to ask in interviews for salespeople is "Why are you choosing to get into sales?" What I want to hear is a candidate's motivation for the role. Things like, "I love it, it's challenging, it gives me a chance to be strategic, I can make a lot of money, it's changing all the time and seems to never get old, the upside is fantastic and completely dependent on the effort I put in." Instead I've heard things like, "I didn't really know what else to do and it seems like I can do it. I like people, I'm not sure what else to do with my degree, and I need something that can support my other goals." There's no purpose or real desire to get the job. 

I’ve interviewed thousands of people in my career and it’s incredible how many of them can’t stop talking. They will start to answer a question and then keep talking. They will launch into this massive monologue about things so far off topic that oftentimes I have to stop them and get them back to the question. It’s not conversational if you’re monologuing and it works against you when you appear that you can’t stay on task.

If you need to buy some time to put your thoughts together it helps to repeat the question they asked of you. Feel free to do that outloud. When you do that it seems like you process information well and then think before you speak. 

Remember the source material they are going to ask you questions from is yours. The interviewer is going off of your resume and cover letter. You should know each and every one of the KPI’s and metrics you have on your resume. The best indicator of someone lying about what they have accomplished is when the numbers they say and the numbers on their resume don’t match up. It’s also why you should bring multiple copies of your resume with you to an interview. Not just for the people interviewing you but you should also have a copy of your resume and cover letter in front of you just for reference. 

It’s important in any setting to be yourself, but it helps to be brief, be brilliant and be gone.

Answering the Question They are Asking You

Media training is really about confidence and the internalization of material. Well trained people have a confident answer to a question. The great ones have internalized a belief in that answer and you can feel the difference. Training with questions is great but what about the second question? This is where the verbal meandering comes in. If people have trained, they answer the first question well. If they have internalized their training they can answer the second and third questions after their first answer. Let me give you an example:

Question:

What do you dislike about sales?

Answers: (that I’ve actually gotten)

“In my last job I didn’t like how long it took to make a sale.” And then they launched into a story about their last boss that made it more difficult and handed sales off to reps he liked. Their answer took three minutes.

- As the interviewer I know that they have no idea how long our sales cycle is. And worse, I have no idea how long their last sales cycle was. An answer like this puts questions in my head not answers. Will you struggle with the length of our sales cycle? Why didn’t they like it? Were they not prospecting enough and putting enough opportunities into their pipeline so closes weren’t happening with enough regularity? Don’t make negative statements in an interview especially without knowing what the job you're interviewing for entails. 

“The phone. No one wants to be called anymore. I honestly think that the phone is dead and I end up always working on emails because I’ve found that to work much better.”

- Taking a stance like this in an interview is a sure fire way to kill an opportunity especially when you say something like “the phone is dead.” It shows that the candidate isn’t willing to try what we believe works and they are just going to do what they want to do. They are telling me they aren’t coachable. What the candidate didn’t know is that while we were hiring for an outside B2B rep we had two days of the week where we had 4 hour phone blocks to set appointments. How on earth do I hire someone for that when they say they hate the phone? 

“Updating the CRM. My boss made us update the CRM constantly and it felt like that was our job. I was hired to sell and I never made a commission for putting things in my CRM.” 

- What sales company doesn’t require their reps to update the CRM? Even if they hated it, why would you tell someone in a position in leadership that you hated it? As a manager, I will do anything I can not to have the CRM fight every week with a sales rep. There’s a million sales memes about sales managers asking for a CRM update, we hate that part of the job. This candidate told me they were going to be a problem without realizing it. Fun fact, that’s the reason we ask these questions. 

I will say, I’m a big fan of honesty and that’s what those answers were. The answers were honest but there was no follow up to those answers. No spin to make themselves look like they were open to learn or coachable. They are telling me they are already going to be a problem to manage. It’s obvious there’s no training on how to answer. Answering questions like this is a quick way to a short interview and no job offer. 

Your answer should really answer the question that isn’t being asked. Think about it for a minute. I ask, “What do you dislike about sales?” Is that really what I want to know? No. What I want to know is what you don’t like about it and what do you do to overcome it. Or how do you dig in when you come up against something you don’t like to do. It’s ok to say you don’t like something, even the CRM or Phone, but what you say is, “I didn’t like it at first because it was difficult to use or I had anxiety to pick up the phone but I saw that as a challenge. I know that if I want a career in sales, I have to learn to get over my dislike or fear.” 

The answer I want to hear:

“There are a lot of challenging things in sales. When I first started it was the phone and after that it was the CRM and then I struggled with how long it took to get some sales. But what I realized was that the more calls I made the better I got and when I stopped looking at the CRM as a thing I had to do but rather as a tool for getting more sales and keeping up with prospects I realized that sales isn’t something to dislike, it's something to dig into.”

- In that answer you’re acknowledging that there’s difficult aspects to sales but nothing that you dislike. You don’t have to answer the exact question that’s being asked of you. Instead you can highlight the lesson you learned and how you didn’t like them but once you understood the tools you learned to love them. It’s ok to acknowledge the difficult aspects of a job and then explain how you made them something you learned to make work for you. That’s the employee that sales managers want on their team. 

The answer I want to hear from someone with little to no experience:

“I don’t have a lot of experience in sales but I can tell you that when there is something I don’t like about any job that I’ve had I think about it in two ways. First, is it required of me as a part of my job and second, why am I being asked to do it. If it’s required of my job then I’m going to learn to love it. There’s no sense in not liking something that’s going to help me do what I need to do to succeed. And second, I’ll figure out how it helps me in my career and I’ll get really good at it so I learn to like it. For instance, I don’t like working out, but I know it’s in my best interest so I’ve learned to love the process and enjoy the outcome.”

- Acknowledging that you didn’t like something but the way you choose to learn to like it shows adaptability, a willingness to change, and an attitude of a winner. It shows you are coachable and you have a desire to learn. It also says that as your sales manager I’m not going to have to fight you on things to get them done. Managers are looking for people who they are going to get along with and not have to coach and reprimand. Sales managers also know that doing your job makes their job much easier. They love people who make their job easier!
 
Sales is tough and challenging. That manager knows that too. What they want to hear is that they are interviewing a candidate who’s in the fight with them. Does that manager love the phone, the CRM or even cold calling? Probably not, but it’s a part of their job and a part that they have learned is necessary for their success. They want you to know that too and you should be able to articulate you’re willing to do anything to be successful. That’s the goal right? 

What They Want You to Know and What They Want You To Say

I had a sales manager ask me, “What could a candidate do to make you want to hire them on the spot?” I answered, “Easy, tell me they are here to learn and they are willing to do what I ask of them.” I was joking a bit but that’s really what I was looking for. The reason is not because I’m not open to new ideas or wanted just a bunch of yes people but because I wanted the sales rep to win. I felt like our sales process and my track record could get a person who was willing to put the work into where they wanted to be. 

I was a new branch manager at a location sitting in a sales review with the General Manager, Regional Sales Manager, and Vice President of the entire region. I was there with my sales manager for our year end sales review. As somewhat of an unspoken rule a sales rep with a year of tenure and below fifty percent to quota should be terminated and not carried into the new year. But I was about to do what most people would consider career suicide.

I sat there in that sales review with my very new sales manager defending a sales rep who was nineteen percent to quota. That’s right, 19% to quota. And I was fighting for his job. Why? Because that sales rep literally did everything I asked him to do. When we met for his weekly one-on-ones, he rebutted every objection just like I or his sales manager would. He prospected like crazy, built his pipeline and worked harder than every other rep. He just couldn’t close. 

I wasn’t ready to admit failure with someone who was willing to work so hard. The problem wasn’t him, it was me. He was nineteen percent to quota because we couldn’t get it to click for him. He did everything we asked him to do which meant it couldn’t be his fault, I had to own the failure. In that meeting, I said to all of my higher ups what they wanted to hear, “He’s struggling and we know that but he’s worth keeping. He’s putting in the work and we will get him to be successful. At this point if we don’t keep him you’ll have to get rid of the entire location's sales team and me because we are all doing the same thing we just haven’t figured him out yet. Give us another quarter to figure it out.”

At this point in my career, with that company, I had built up a little bit of a reputation with how I managed and how hard I was willing to work to get people to be successful. We had done enough in a positive way to earn some goodwill and a little bit of some rope. As they say, just enough rope to hang myself with. 

Thankfully that year, with some more hard work and help, it did click for him and after 12 years he’s still with that company. He’s a branch manager now running an incredibly successful location with a phenomenal team that he’s built. 

That lesson taught me something I’ve taken with me my entire career. I now say it to every sales rep I extend an offer to. “If you do what I tell you to do and fail it’s on me but if you fail doing things on your own that don’t align with the company or our process, there’s no way I can save you. It’s up to you to put arrows in my quiver.” Imagine a conversation between your boss and theirs. “Are they following the system and doing what you ask? No. Then get rid of them.” See, if you don’t put arrows in your sales managers quiver they have nothing to defend you with. 

It’s one of the ways in interviews I get people to buy into the idea that I’m going to ask you to do things our way. So many salespeople die on islands they have built for themselves doing what they think is best. Don’t be that person. Great sales leaders work to get their people successful, but you as a potential sales person on their team have to show them that you’re willing to do the hard work.

Sales managers want someone on their team who’s willing to bye in. Someone who is motivated, eager, coachable, willing to do what it takes to win and goal oriented. Make sure you let them know that’s who you are. And most importantly, follow through and be that person. Rarely do managers quit on people who don’t quit on themselves.

Remember this is for entry level B2B sales. You don’t need experience to get hired in that type of job. In a lot of larger companies, they have a larger, more robust training program that they don’t need a salesperson to have any experience. A lot of times, in entry level B2B sales that manager wants someone to mold. Building new habits is so much easier than breaking more engrained bad habits. People who show up ready to learn and work are a much bigger asset to a sales team than someone who thinks they know what to do and isn’t willing to listen or do what this new position will ask of them. 

I worked for a company where I hired the whole team with no experience. All of them checked a majority of the criteria above and it was awesome to see people achieve things they never thought possible. We used to have quarterly call blocks contests where we would report numbers hourly to corporate on how our team was doing against the rest of the region. For the first two contests I wouldn’t share the results of the rest of the region with my team. Why? Because my team was setting six appointments an hour while the rest of the region averaged two to three. How? Was I that good of a manager? No, I just never told them what they couldn’t do or what the standard was. I let them drive it. There was no experience in that room to tell them what wasn’t possible.

The person interviewing you wants to hear you’re not going to be a problem. Trust me, they have enough, they aren’t looking to add to that pile. They also want to know that you’re willing to work your tail off because they know something you might not. Sales is hard! You are going to struggle. I’ve never met a sales rep that didn’t struggle at one point or another. If sales were easy, they wouldn’t need sales managers and if it was really easy they wouldn’t need salespeople. You win in sales by being consistent, disciplined and willing to show up and work even when things aren’t going your way.

That’s what you have to get across to the person interviewing you. When they ask you questions your answers have to show that you’re willing to do what it takes to win with them. 

When they ask, “Tell me about a time when you failed at something and what you did?” They want to know how you’re going to deal with failure when you encounter it. Think of your answer like a story. There has to be a start, the problem or failure and then the lesson you learned. It would go like this, “When I was in college I had the hardest time staying on top of assignments. I would just miss deadlines or due dates. I just couldn’t keep on top of it. It was the worst. I was constantly apologizing to professors and project team mates. I finally went to two professors and asked them who the best students were at turning things in. Not just on time but who was killing it. They gave me some names and I tracked down those students and got them to show me how they were executing so well. Ultimately I was able to find a way that worked for me and I never had to deal with it again. It felt like I cracked the code.”

The answer they are looking for is about getting a glimpse about how you might really handle failure. What are you going to do or how are you going to act when you start to fail at their company? How do you handle failure? Do you crawl in a hole and give up? Do you have any intestinal fortitude? Are you a cryer? Or when you are met with adversity, which is what sales is, do you dig in and get to work. So that’s the answer they want to hear.

The example given works to answer the question they are really asking. That when you face adversity, you assess it, figure it out, find a solution and execute on the plan to resolve it. That’s someone a manager wants on their team. The majority of sales managers need their salespeople to be successful so they can be successful. And if they aren’t you don’t want to work there. In the answer given it is clear that when you became aware of the problem you responded by asking for help and solving it. Every sales manager wants to know that you want to be successful and you’re willing to swallow your pride and ask for help. 

The flow chart you should follow is below. It’s the practiced story that you wrap around it that makes all the difference. 

Context / Story —-> Problem Encountered —-> Execution on Solution —-> Result

So many sales reps fail because they think they have all the answers. Someone like that is a PIP (Performance Improvement Plan) waiting to happen. The manager interviewing you knows that so what they want to hear from you is the truth while checking the boxes about what makes a good sales rep. Experience is the least of your worries. Managers in the entry level B2B space hire in some variation or another from the list above. 

In order to ease the pressure of your interview you should know that if you’re sitting in the interviewees chair, that manager wants to hire you. No one wants to waste their time, especially managers who have so many other things to do. If they are taking the time out of their day they are hoping you answer the questions the right way. They are looking for rock stars or candidates who are willing to become rock stars. 

Setting up your answers in the format above or using the popular STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) will help you create the appropriate stories. What you haven’t been told before is WHY you’re answering the questions that way. You’re doing the best you can to check off their list on being the right candidate.

Don’t Be Afraid to Make Your Skeletons Dance


Irish playwright George Bernard Shaw said, "If you cannot get rid of the family skeleton, you may as well make it dance". What he means by that is that you shouldn’t be afraid to own up to your failures. No one is batting 100% in their life and even the person interviewing you has made some colossal failures. I’ve got a bunch as well and you probably do too. What if you weren’t ashamed of your failures but you learned to create stories where you took the loss, learned from it and succeeded despite the failure. 

I started my first company at the age of 24. We did over a million dollars our first year of business. It shut down years later because I took my eyes off the ball. I failed because I didn’t see things through. I got distracted by success and money and the opportunity to do more. I was forced to start over in my thirties. 

What I didn’t know was how much that experience would help me later on down the road. I also developed a tenacity to see things through. My skeleton is an epic failure and it took some time to recover but now that baby is dancing. That skeleton doesn’t define me, it motivates me. It’s ok to make mistakes, it's what you do with them, how vulnerable you are in talking about them that makes you relatable both in the interview and in sales.

When you’re telling your stories the beginning of the story isn’t near as important as the ending. How did you learn? What did you learn? And what changes did you make to ensure it didn’t happen again. Results matter to sales managers and hearing about your desire to get results, solve problems, correct course and execute are music to a hiring manager's ears. Jeb Blount, founder of Sales Gravy and author of seventeen phenomenal sales books, wrote a book called People Buy You. Meaning people buy from people they like and in an interview you want to say things that the hiring manager is going to like. You want them to buy you!

You need to get good at telling your stories and you need to practice them. I honestly can’t say this enough. You want to know why? People rarely do it. I’ve done thousands of interviews for salespeople and you can tell they aren’t prepared. You have to look for people who are willing to help. Just asking someone is further than most people will go and actually practicing will help you lock down the job you’re looking for. Your time is an investment and if you’re not willing to invest in yourself, what on earth are you going to be willing to invest in? 

If you’re new to sales and looking for that first job to get your foot in the door, your narrative needs to be more about what you’re willing to do to win instead of what you’ve done. You need to learn how to tell the stories of your past and explain how what you did then represents what you’re willing to do in the future. 

Media training is about not being afraid to make your skeletons dance! It’s about redemption and results and a desire to overcome. Show a hiring manager that and you’ll get the job.

Please Don’t Say

I love working with salespeople about their mindset and mentality and I would prefer not to write this section at all simply because I want you to think about the right things to say, not the wrong ones. Most people are contrarian by nature. I tell salespeople all the time, if it’s raining you don’t want to cold call and if it’s bright and sunny and beautiful out you don’t want to cold call either because you’d rather be doing something else. Truth is, you don’t want to cold call. Or to reduce it even more, don’t think of the number eleven. Want to know what you’re thinking about…the number eleven. See how your brain works? Read this section but don’t dwell on it. Practice what you are going to say in the interview but be aware of what you shouldn’t say.

Unfortunately I have seen so many potential candidates throw away all the work they have done to get the interview because they have said the wrong things in the interview. You need to avoid language that places blame, except for blame you are willing to own. Don’t be defensive when answering questions. Don’t use indifferent language that would allude to you being lazy in your efforts. And remember that entitlement will get you nowhere. 
You may not know this but not everyone is a great interviewer. In fact they ask very predictable questions. We will get into that later but you should know they are going to ask you some pretty boiler plate questions.

One of them is “What do you know about our company?” The last thing they want to hear is, “I don’t really know much about your company.”  It’s why we created the spreadsheet. Not just to get the interview, it’s so you can get the job. That research is going to pay off huge when someone asks you this question. You have to understand that a person in a hiring position is emotionally connected to their job in one way or another. They know almost everything about that company and they would love to find someone who shares that passion. 

Saying you don’t know much about the company makes you look lazy and have little interest or intent to get the job. It tells the hiring manager that if you aren’t willing to prepare for the interview you’re likely to put little effort into the actual job. An old grizzled regional sales manager once said to me, “that interview is the best that candidate is going to get, if they aren’t impressing you in that interview they never will.” 

I ask sales candidates a series of questions that ultimately leads me to finally ask, “tell me about the worst boss that you’ve had.” Even if it’s true, I don’t want to hear, “my boss at XYZ Company was the worst!” or worse, “I can’t think of a great boss.” The answer to this question is best left to the old adage that if you don’t have anything nice to say don’t say anything at all.  What you can say is, “Having a bad boss isn’t something I’ve had to deal with. Even when they weren’t optimal I made sure I could work with them. Usually if there was any fault it laid at my feet. I’ve been very fortunate when it comes to leadership.” 

If you have little or no sales experience or not a lot of work history you can say, “I haven’t had any bad bosses and I think that’s why interviews like this are so important. I want to make sure the place I go to work has great people who are going to help me be successful if I put in the work to make it happen.” There’s a slight shift in the power dynamic with a statement like this which says, “I’m going to work my tail off and it’s up to you to be the boss I’m looking for.” 

A candidate looked me dead in the eyes and said, “I’m just looking for anything right now.” And in a way it broke my heart. They needed a job and I can understand that, but at that moment they just told me that they are here because they will take anything they can get. In hiring people desperation doesn’t equal motivation. What it tells me is that the candidate will leave the minute something better comes along. They didn’t want to work there because of our training, or the opportunity of being successful. They didn’t see it as an opportunity for career progression or even just the money. They just needed a job. What I heard the candidate say is that this role is a placeholder, not a commitment.

Interviewers hate to hear, “That wasn’t my responsibility.” Lack of ownership is a huge red flag in an interview. Nobody wants to hire the person in the group project who didn’t do any of the work. They are dying to have owners on their team, people willing to take ownership of results! 

“None that I can think of,” was the answer I got once when I asked, “can you tell me about your weaknesses?”. After a brief pause and fixing my face from complete shock, I looked at the candidate and said, “none?”. He corrected himself and gave me a couple of predictable answers but the damage was already done. I saw it as one of two things, either a huge ego or a complete lack of self-awareness. What I heard was that this sales rep can’t see any way that they need to get better. They were going to be uncoachable. 

When I’m asked the question, “what’s your biggest weakness?” Here's how I answer it. “One of the things I’ve always struggled with is organization. It’s difficult for me because I’m just not wired that way. What I’ve learned to do though is that I have to dive head first into systems whether they exist already or I have to build my own. Having a structure keeps me focused and makes sure I deliver what’s expected of me.”  An answer like this tells people that I know what my biggest weakness is and how I’ve learned to make it work for me. 

Some people in interviews will be overly agreeable and say things like, “I’ll do whatever you want.” This answer is given when you have someone eager to get the job but it also sounds like you lack backbone or values. It also sounds like they won’t push back and challenge a bad decision. It’s best to avoid an answer like this altogether. 

I saw a resume that one time and the numbers were just too good to be true. When I asked, “how is it you were able to always hit your number?” they said, “I just hit my number, I don’t really know how.” Salespeople always know how. Especially the ones who do it year over year. They love talking about it too. When a sales rep figures it out they love telling people how they cracked the code. Luck is not a strategy, leaders want to know you can follow a repeatable process. An answer like this also hurts your upward mobility. What you want to sound like a natural but what it really says is I can’t teach, replicate or scale what I know. 

And for the love of God, please don’t ever say, “I don’t have any questions”! Interviews should be a two-way evaluation for fit. If you don’t have any questions they are going to question your desire to get the job and if you’re thinking about working with that company for the long term. Bring questions, we’ll give you some later.

Right now, let’s get ready for the phone interview.



























Chapter 4

Nailing The Phone Screen

The phone is a funny thing. It’s the only object on earth that simultaneously weighs ounces and nine hundred pounds at the same time. It’s amazing how easy it is to talk to people over the phone but the minute we do it under stress we can’t put it down quick enough. I believe the difference is that one is a casual conversation versus the other being a purposeful conversation. We love casual conversations but the minute purpose is introduced, “The reason for my call,” everything changes. In actuality the phone doesn’t change, we do.

A long, long, long time ago when my now wife and I were dating we were sitting in her parents home as she was trying to figure out what to do about one of her college classes. She had missed some classes and assignments and perhaps didn’t do so well on some of the exams, finding herself staring down the barrel of failing the class. The only reason I think she was even having the conversation with me about what to do is because I had way more experience than she did in not performing well in classes. 

I offered her some options. She could visit the professor during his office hours and plead her case, which was my strongest recommendation as it had worked for me in the past. I’m also not afraid to cry and beg and that wasn’t a strategy she wanted to pursue. Unlike me my wife isn’t a big “peoplely” person and so she shot down that option pretty quick. Email was new at that point and not something people had widely adopted or using which meant she really only had just one option. She had to call him on the phone.

After coming to the conclusion that the phone was really her only option, she grabbed her syllabus that had her professor's contact information on it and made a beeline for the phone. She checked quickly to see that she was in the time frame of his office hours and so she picked up the phone and dialed. 

In her head, she had that conversation a million times with him over the last couple of weeks as she stressed about what to do. She had every reason why she wasn’t passing the class and she felt like she could articulate it. She thought she was ready for the conversation she had already had in her head. But that conversation was one she controlled. Unfortunately for her, that’s not what happened.

The phone rang and he didn’t pick up, his answering machine did. That was the first thing that she wasn’t prepared for. Instead of hanging up when it went to voicemail, she just started talking. What happened next was tough to watch. I’m talking about large-scale panic while she was still trying to plead her case. What followed in that message was worthy of comedy level writing. She was all over the place as panic built into an utter realization that she was toast. It ended thankfully but it ended in a way that only something like that three minute self-destruction could. She literally said when she was ending the call, “In Jesus name amen” like she was ending a prayer. She hung up the phone and her head collapsed into her hands defeated. 
Neither of us could do anything but laugh. We still laugh about it today. Not to laugh at her but just the fact that when anyone experiences that level of embarrassment one of us will look at the other and say, “In Jesus name amen.” Everything went wrong in that phone call and that’s because it went from casual to purposeful and massive variables entered into the equation. She wasn’t ready. 

You need to be ready for your phone screen. You need to be prepared. And you need to practice out loud. You have to practice with someone. Practicing with someone will also give you perspective outside of your own on how they might answer the questions. Your practice partner will also be able to provide content rich feedback on what you could say or what you shouldn’t say. Practicing is what makes you a pro. Pro’s get jobs and become successful. It’s the only way you don’t end up with a phone screen that ends in a Jesus name amen type of collapse filled with regret and realization that you just blew your opportunity to get the job you wanted.

Let’s get you ready!

First you have to understand a couple of things about these conversations. Context is crucial for mindset so you have to understand a couple of things. These are not casual conversations at all and they are very purpose driven from their side. If they feel casual, that’s a trap to try and figure out who you really are. A typical phone screen will last between fifteen and thirty minutes. Usually the longer they last the better but they aren’t afraid to cut them short if they don’t think it’s progressing or you're not the right fit. 

These people are very protective of their time and in some scenarios they are under a lot of pressure to fill their team and hire someone. They usually have a day of the week or two that they will do these phone screens and they have a list of people they have to interview that day. If they are a hiring manager filling out their own positions they are under even more time constraints trying to balance their work, their team and filling new positions. You’re not their only interview but the goal should be to be so good they stop doing phone interviews and schedule your in person interview because they think they found the right person!

Most likely this manager will reach out to you via email to schedule an interview. RESPOND IMMEDIATELY! This is your first chance to show the hiring manager your sense of urgency when it comes to getting something you want. This isn’t dating and you aren’t in a position to play hard to get. Respond and schedule that interview. Say yes to the time they offer and if they let you pick a time or schedule it yourself then pick the date closest to the day of the email. Give yourself at least a day to prepare and review your notes. 

What to Do Before The Call

If you followed and completed the spreadsheet in Chapter 2 a lot of the work is already done. You just need to review the information about the company. Make sure you get the person’s name who is conducting the interview and look them up on LinkedIn. Don’t search these people on LinkedIn with your profile set to private. You want them to know you are doing your research. You will also want to look up their boss and other people at the company in the local office where you are applying. If you’re applying to a large company with several locations around the country the chances are the local office is much more intimate and there’s a good chance they will notice. If you did happen to make contact with someone in the organization through your research this is a great time to reach out to them and let them know you got the interview. Getting your name circulating in the office before you even get there sets the stage for a successful interview. 

From a sales managers perspective they would love to have someone prospect an organization the way you would if you follow the steps laid out in this book. And if they don’t pick up on that, make sure you point it out if and when an opportunity presents itself. You’ll also do this again when you have the in person interview. 

You don’t have to reveal everything you know, the phone screen isn’t a time for small talk. But you should be prepared. This conversation is to see if their intuition is right about you and your resume and to see if they should bring you in for a formal interview. Small talk isn’t going to get you anywhere. This is business for them.

You should also know that you want to make it clear who is interviewing you for the phone screen. It could be the sales manager or it could be an internal recruiter. It matters. Developing a relationship with a recruiter at their corporate office and walking through your resume doesn’t mean the sales manager you will interview next will get your whole story. You’ll have to do it all again in the in-person interview. 

Here’s the framework for most phone screens:

Opening, Introduction and Agenda. (2 - 3 Minutes)

Just an FYI, they are most likely reading from a script and have a form to work through. They will be speaking with you and also taking notes of your answers.

What it sounds like: 

“Thanks for taking the time today. This will be a quick call to learn a little more about you and answer a few questions.”

They may also confirm that it’s still a good time for the call. That’s a habit and sign of a good salesperson or sales manager.  

What they are looking for:

Right off the bat they want to hear you being upbeat, friendly and happy. Positive energy and an upbeat tone in your voice go a long way when it comes to making a great first impression.

They want you to sound professional. Thanking them for taking their time is a great way to make them feel appreciated and appreciation always goes a long way. Saying something as simple as, “It’s really great to meet you, thank you so much for taking the time to speak with me today” with a natural upbeat demeanor will separate you from a lot of candidates. Smiling helps a lot when you’re saying this. Try it outloud. Did you read it? Did you hear how fast you read it? Don’t do that. It has to sound natural. Say it out loud and say it slowly. Try it again. Practice it until it feels natural and more importantly sincere. 

They also want to make sure you can carry a conversation without rambling. Remember, be brief, be brilliant and be gone. Answer their question and then stop talking. 

What they hate:

Bad First Impressions: 

You can kill an interview from the start with a poor attitude or a complete lack of energy. Tone matters and so do first impressions. Think about how fast you are to judge someone. People tend to project present feelings with how they will feel towards someone in the future. Get them to like you right away and all it takes is a smile.

Lack of Professionalism:

During a phone screen do your best to be in an environment where you normally get work done. Be in a place where you are used to being professional. Don’t take the call from your car. Don’t be around a lot of people or noises that are going to distract you or hinder the ability for them to hear you well. Distracting phone conversations are frustrating for everyone involved. Be where you have good cell service. Not setting the right environment is setting you up to fail. You are making the biggest sale of your life, YOU, position yourself for success and act accordingly.

Being Unprepared:

Never, ever should you not be prepared for something you bothered to schedule. Today it’s interviews, tomorrow it’s a million dollar deal. Being prepared will always matter. Being late, having a bad phone connection, low battery, and seemingly then a non-chalant to the conversation scream not being prepared for the moment. Think about it from their perspective, right from the start they think that you just wasted their time. If you can’t nail something as important as a paycheck, what effort will you put into prospecting, discovery or proposal when it comes time to selling the product or service they represent? Who you are on this call is who they believe you will be if you’re given the chance to work for them.

Question 1 “Walk me Through Your Background” (3 - 5 Minutes)

This is why practice is so important. The interviewer wants a short concise story of who you are and what you’ve done. Are you ready to tell that story? You need to be. 

They may ask, “Tell me about yourself.”

You may have a fantastic dog, they don’t want to hear about it. They don’t want your life story or a litany of life experiences. What they are really asking is, WHY you? That’s the question you have to answer. 

Here’s what they want to hear from a “Walk me through your background” question. 

Recent college graduate:

”I’ve just graduated college and so my background isn’t very extensive but I’d like to think that I made the most of my time in school. I was involved in several clubs and sports teams. Right now, I’m early in my career and looking for a role where effort, discipline, and learning matter. I’m drawn to B2B sales because it’s performance based and teaches real business skills. My thought is that if I want to be successful in my career, a strong sales foundation is going to be the most important thing I can do for myself.”

Salesperson with some sales experience:

The goal here is to frame your experience as proof you can handle early stage B2B sales,
not to impress the interviewer with a litany of experience. 

The winning structure is around 90 seconds max and should follow the pattern of: 

Past ? Present ? Purpose.

Your story should be framed in the context of where you’ve been, relevant experience in your past, what you’re doing now and why this role is the logical next step in your career. Going beyond 90 seconds puts too much noise when this is a conversation geared around signals. 

Experience specific answers:

Customer Facing or Service Experience:

       What to emphasize:
       
       Handling objections
       Volume of work
       Pressure
       Accountability
       Consistency
       
	What your answer could sound like:
“I started my career in a customer facing role where I handled high volume interactions daily. I learned quickly how to communicate clearly, manage objections, and stay professional under pressure. Over time I realized I enjoyed the challenge of influencing outcomes, not just servicing requests, which is what pulled me towards B2B sales.”

This answer works because you’re proving people skills and resilience.
       
Inside Sales / SDR / Appointment Setting Experience:

	What to emphasize:

	Prospecting
	Rejection
	Metrics
	Learning curve

	What your answer could sound like:

“I’ve spent time in an inside sales environment where my role was focused on outbound activity and qualifying opportunities. I learned quickly how much discipline and consistency matter. That experience confirmed for me that I want to build a long term career in B2B sales, ideally in a field based role where effort directly ties to results.”
	
This answer works because you’re demonstrating that you already know sales isn’t easy. That’s a huge green flag to a sales manager who may doubt you’re aware of what you’re getting into.

Internship Experience (Sales, Ops, Marketing, Finance):

	What to emphasize:

	Exposure to business fundamentals
	Cross-functional learning
	Curiosity

	What your answer could sound like:

“During my internship, I worked closely with both sales and operations teams. I saw how deals are generated, how accounts are serviced, and how performance is measured. That exposure helped me realize that I want a role where I’m directly responsible for growth, not just supporting it.”

This answer works because it shows business awareness not just ambition to get the role.
Blue Collar / Field / Route / Operations Work:

	What to emphasize:

	Work ethic
	Early mornings
	Physical and mental toughness
	Reliability

	What your answer could sound like:

“I’ve worked in a hands-on, field based role where showing up early, staying disciplined, and being accountable mattered. That experience taught me how to manage my time, deal with difficult situations, and earn trust. I’m now looking to apply that same work ethic in a sales role where effort and consistency drive results.”

This answer works because it shows you’re already accustomed to hard work and putting in the time to be successful. It also shows you know how to articulate your answers and makes a fantastic first impression.

Athletics / Military / Competitive Background:

	What to emphasize:

	Coachability
	Structure
	Performance feedback
	Resilience

	What your answer could sound like:

“I come from a competitive background where performance was measured and feedback was direct. I’m comfortable being coached, held accountable, and working towards measurable goals. That’s what attracted me to B2B sales and to a company like this.”

This answer works because companies hire for trainability, attitude and competitiveness. They know they can’t teach the intangibles and this answer says that’s exactly what you bring to the table.

Question 2: “Why Sales? Why This Company?” (4 - 6 Minutes)

What it sounds like:

What questions they could ask you.
“Why Sales?”

“Why B2B Sales?”

“Why XYZ company?”

What they are looking for:

What your answer could sound like to “Why Sales?”:

What they are really asking: “Do you understand what sales actually is, and are you choosing it intentionally?” 

“I’m drawn to sales because it’s one of the few careers where effort, discipline, and learning directly impact results. I like roles where performance is measurable and improvement is earned. I also like the idea of being accountable to a number and developing skills like communication, problem solving, and resilience. I know that skills like these are incredibly valuable for my career in the long term, regardless of where my career goes.”

This answer works because you’re not saying things like, “I like talking to people”. You’re signalling that you’re coachable and mature enough to look at your career in the long term. You also demonstrate, in this answer, that you realize that pressure equals growth!

What your answer could sound like to “Why B2B Sales?”:

What they are really asking: “Do you understand the difference between transactional selling and professional selling?”

“B2B sales appeals to me because it’s more consultative and process driven. You’re working with business owners or decision makers, diagnosing real problems, and earning trust over time. I like that it requires preparation, follow up, and consistency, and that success comes from building credibility, not just being likable. I’m also interested in learning how businesses operate, how decisions get made, how costs are evaluated, and how long term relationships are built.”

This answer works because even though you may not have the experience you understand the idea of longer sales cycles. It positions you as a business minded professional with a patience

What your answer could sound like to “Why XYZ Company?”

You can tailor this answer slightly for each company you're speaking with depending on your research but the structure should stay the same.

The core framework to use is:

Reputation and training
Essential services
A proven B2B sales model
Career foundation

“I’m interested in companies like XYZ because they sell essential, recurring services to businesses. Most companies need the services you provide which makes the sales process about solving realy operational problems, not selling a nice to have. I’m also drawn to the structured training and strong sales culture. I want to start my career somewhere that teaches fundamentals like prospecting, territory management, objection handling and holds people accountable to a process. I see XYZ as a place where I can build a strong sales foundation.”

If your answer includes learning, accountability and process as well as long term skill development you’ll put yourself in the top tier of applicants for the position.

Question 3: “Traits and Behavioral Questions?” (5 - 7 Minutes)

What it sounds like:

They could ask this question a couple of different ways.

“Tell me about a time you had to push through rejection.”

“Describe a goal you worked hard to achieve.”

“How do you stay motivated?”

“Tell me about a time you were held accountable.”

What they are looking for:

What your answer could sound like to “Tell me about a time you had to push through rejection.”

What they are really asking: “Can you handle hearing “no” without quitting or making excuses?”

“During my job search after graduation, I applied to a lot of roles and didn’t hear back from most of them. At first it was discouraging. Instead of stopping, I asked a few recruiters for feedback and realized my resume was too general. I rewrote it, started following up, and reached out directly to hiring managers when I could. It took persistence, but eventually I started getting interviews. That experience taught me that rejection usually means I need to adjust my approach and not just quit.”

This answer works because it shows persistence, self awareness and doesn’t require you to come up with some sort of fake sales story. You have to understand that creating a story means you need to be aware of your lessons. Once you start training yourself to see things from a thirty thousand foot view you see lessons not rejections. Being able to capture and describe that type of insight makes you an emotionally intelligent candidate and extremely hireable. 

If you have some experience, one to three years, your answer could sound like:

“Earlier in my career, I was responsible for outreach and heard “no” far more often than “yes.” At first, it was frustrating, but I started tracking why people were saying no. I adjusted how I opened conversations, asked better questions, and focused on the prospects that were a better fit. Over time, my response rate improved. That experience taught me that rejection is part of the process, and progress comes from learning and not taking “no” personally.”

This answer works because it shows you are familiar and comfortable with rejection. You already know it’s a part of the job. It shows your mindset is based on improvement and action rather than settling for failure. It shows you know what sales really is and that you already have a good understanding of what it takes to win.

What your answer could sound like to “Describe a goal you worked hard to achieve.”

What they are really asking: “Do you set goals and actually follow through?”

“One goal I set was to graduate on time while working part-time. I had to manage my schedule carefully, prioritize deadlines, and make sacrifices socially at times. There were weeks when it was overwhelming, but I stayed focused on the end goal and adjusted when things got tight. Hitting that goal showed me I can stay disciplined and consistent over a long period of time, even when things get difficult.”

This answer works because it shows your ability to manage your time, with both short and long term goals. It also shows that you're disciplined enough to stick to your process in order to achieve your goals. 

If you have some experience, one to three years, your answer could sound like:

“In my previous role, I set a personal goal to become one of the top performers on my team. I asked my manager what top performers were doing differently and tracked my activity closely. I focused on improving one area at a time instead of trying to fix everything at once. Within a few months, my results were more consistent. That goal taught me how to use structure and feedback to drive better performance.”

This answer works because it shows you are coachable and already understand the importance of being metric driven. It also clearly indicates that you know you are a work in progress and focused on improvement.

What your answer could sound like to “How do you stay motivated?”

What they are really asking: “Will you work just as hard when no one is watching?”

“I stay motivated by setting small goals and measuring progress. I like having something to work toward each day or week. Even when the task itself isn’t exciting, I focus on the skill I’m developing and how it helps me improve. Seeing progress keeps me motivated, especially when I know it’s building toward a bigger goal.”

This answer works because it shows an internal motivation driving you. Relating to being processed focused, which they are, makes your answer relatable. It also clearly shows you rely on discipline rather than motivation to set goals and achieve them.

What your answer could sound like to “Tell me about a time you were held accountable.”

What they are really asking: “How do you respond when expectations are clear and the pressure is real?”

“When I was working in school, my manager gave clear expectations around punctuality and performance. Early on, I missed a standard and was called out for it. I took responsibility, adjusted my routine, and made sure it didn’t happen again. After that, I became more reliable and earned more responsibility. That experience taught me that accountability helps you improve if you take it seriously.”

This answer works because it shows that you own your mistakes. It’s an early indicator that you respond well to structure and feedback and welcome coaching. 

If you have some experience, one to three years, your answer could sound like:

“In my previous role, my performance was tracked weekly. During one review my manager pointed out that my follow-up consistency wasn’t where it needed to be. I took the feedback, adjusted my workflow, and built a system to make sure nothing slipped through. My performance improved, and it showed in my results. That experience reinforced that accountability is a tool for growth, not a critique on me as a person.”

This answer works because it shows you understand the fundamentals of sales. You get that sales is data driven and you accept that information is a way to help you better your skills and results. Demonstrating that you have a grasp on KPI’s and insight into that data you’re proving that you’re not only open to feedback, you welcome it and also implement the necessary changes.



Question 4: “Job Fit and Reality Check” (3 - 5 Minutes)

What it sounds like:

First thing you need to know is these questions are designed to gut check you and see if you are really willing to put in the work to be successful.

Here’s the questions they could ask you:

“This role involves cold outreach or cold calls and that makes up a bulk of the work. Is that something you really want to do?”

“You’ll hear “no” a lot in this position. How will you handle that?”

“This job is a grind early on and requires a lot of front loaded effort. How will you handle that?”

What they are looking for: 

What your answer could sound like to “This role involves cold outreach or cold calls and that makes up a bulk of the work. Is that something you really want to do?”

What they are really asking: “Do you actually understand what this job is, or are you romanticizing ‘sales’? Are you choosing this role intentionally, or will you resent it in sixty days?” And “When the activity numbers matter more than results early on, will you lean in or shut down?”

“Yes, and I’ve been realistic about that going into it. I understand that cold outreach is the core of the role, especially starting out in a new territory. I see it as the fastest way to build confidence, discipline and communication skills. I’m not expecting it to be comfortable right away but I’m choosing it because I want to learn how to create opportunities and not wait for them.”

This answer works because it frames cold calling as skill building and not something that needs to be suffered through. It signals a desire for accountability and ownership of a successful outcome. 

If you have some experience, one to three years, your answer could sound like:

“Yes. Cold outreach has been a core part of my last role. I’ve learned that while it’s uncomfortable at first, it’s also where the most growth happens. I’m more effective now because I understand how to prepare, manage my energy, and stay consistent. I’m choosing a role like this because I know I can create opportunities because I’m always cold calling!”

This answer works because it references real experience, shows that you understand the role and positions cold calling as a learned skill and not a hurdle. If you’re able to make cold calling something you do you’ll set yourself up for an incredibly successful sales career.

What your answer could be to “You’ll hear ‘no’ a lot in this position. How will you handle that?”

What they are really asking:“Will rejection derail your confidence or motivation, do you take feedback personally and can you emotionally separate effort from outcome?”

“I expect to hear ‘no’ a lot more than ‘yes,’ and I don’t see that as failure, I see it as feedback. My focus is on doing the work consistently, learning from what doesn’t land, and improving my approach. As long as I’m controlling my effort and getting better, I won’t get discouraged by individual setbacks or outcomes.”

This answer works because it demonstrates a strong emotional maturity by seeing the bigger picture. It also shows coachability and a focus on process and not ego. Explaining that you understand that ‘no’ is a huge part of sales means you understand that getting all those ‘no’s’ will lead to the ‘yes’s’ it takes to win. 

If you have some experience, one to three years, your answer could sound like:

“Hearing ‘no’ is a part of the job, and I’ve learned not to internalize that. Earlier in my career I took rejection more personally, but now I focus on patterns instead of individual outcomes. If I’m hearing the same objections repeatedly, I adjust my approach. As long as I’m executing the process and improving, I stay confident and consistent.”

This answer works because it shows self-awareness and a personal evolution. It shows you as an active learner in the role that you’re doing. It demonstrates a problem solving approach and signals emotional control under the pressure of performance.

What your answer could be to “This job is a grind early on and requires a lot of front loaded effort. How will you handle that?”

What they are really asking: “Will you quit before you get good?” “Do you understand that results come after effort?” “Are you built for delayed gratification?”

“I expect the ramp-up to be the hardest part, and I’m ok with that. I see cold calling and increased effort early on as an investment into myself and my commission. I understand that the work I do everyday determines how effective I’ll be later. I’m comfortable working hard without immediate payoff because I know that I’m building skills, habits and momentum.”

This answer works because it shows you have a grasp on setting realistic expectations for the work and this role. It demonstrates an internalized grit while also showing patience and discipline to continue to execute without seeing immediate results. Long term thinking is key in longer sales cycles.

If you have some experience, one to three years, your answer could sound like:

“I’ve been through a ramp up before, so I understand the front loading effort and delayed results. I plan for that mentally and structurally. I block time, track activity and hold myself accountable to the activities that are going to produce results. I know that the grind doesn’t last forever, but the habits and structure you build during that ramp up will.”

This answer works because it shows you have an understanding of what it takes to succeed in a role like this. It demonstrates that you value planning and structure that are required for a role like this and that it leads to long term success.

Question 5: “Logistical and Commitment Questions” (3 - 4 Minutes)

What it sounds like:

“Are you comfortable with the territory this role covers?”

“The role typically runs early mornings and sometimes longer days. How do you feel about that?”

“This role has a base salary plus commission. How do you feel about variable compensation?”

“If selected, when would you be able to start?”

“This role requires being out in the field, knocking on doors, and meeting business owners face to face. How does that sound to you?

What they are looking for:

What your answer could sound like to “Are you comfortable with the territory this role covers?”

What they are really asking: “Are you and your transportation reliable and do you understand the job or are you going to complain later?”

“Yes. I understand the territory and that I will be required to drive while cold calling and meeting with prospective customers. I have reliable transportation, I’m comfortable being on the road, and I actually like the idea of owning a territory versus being in one place all day.”

This answer works because it shows you get what it takes to do the work and you’ve already made sure you’re set up for success.

What your answer could sound like to “This role typically runs early morning and sometimes longer days. How do you feel about that?”

What they are really asking: “Do you understand the expectations of the role, the work ethic it will take to succeed and do you have your entitlement in check.”

“I’m comfortable with that. I understand this is a performance based role and early mornings are part of building a pipeline. I’m not looking for a strict 9 to 5, I’m looking for a role where effort directly impacts results.”

What your answer could sound like to “This role has a base salary plus commission. How do you feel about variable compensation?”

What they are really asking: “What’s your motivation and risk tolerance for this role? Do you understand how you get paid and are you going to complain about the comp plan?”

“I’m comfortable with it. I see the base salary as the stability I need to live and the commission as a reward for performance. I like the idea of an uncapped commission as well as being able to control how much money I can actually make.”

What your answer could sound like to “If selected, when would you be able to start?”

What they are really asking: “What’s your urgency in obtaining this role? Do you have any competing priorities and what values do you hold when it comes to professionalism?”

“I can start within two weeks, sooner if needed. I’m motivated to get going and understand onboarding is important.”

If you’re not working full time it’s important you let them know that. You don’t want to appear that you’re willing to just drop any other commitments. 

What your answer could sound like to “This role requires being out in the field, knocking on doors and meeting business owners face to face. How does that sound to you?”

What they are really asking: “Do you have the grit required to do this job?”

“That’s actually what attracted me to the role. I like being active, meeting people in person and controlling my day. I know it won’t always be easy, but I’m comfortable with that.”

*Some hiring managers' interviewing styles are harsher and they may push back again with “I don’t think you can do it.” and your response should be, “If you give me the job, I will look forward to proving you wrong.” Again, they are testing you for Grit, don’t be afraid to show it to them!

What your answer could be to “This is a challenging role early on. What makes you confident you’ll stick with it?”

What they are actually saying: “This job isn’t easy and I don’t want you to quit as soon as you run up against your first obstacle. Are you sure you’re the right person?”

“I’m not looking for a short term job. I want a role where I can build skills, prove myself and grow. I understand that this job is challenging and I’m prepared for that.”

Question 5: “Do You Have Any Questions?” (2 - 3 Minutes)

THIS IS WHERE STRONG CANDIDATES SEPARATE THEMSELVES FROM EVERYBODY ELSE!

“No question” is the worst answer but right after that is making your only question about vacation. Those are called interview killers, don’t do that.

Great salespeople ask great questions, this is your chance to shine! Because of that fact it’s important to ask the right ones.

You can or should ask:

“What do top performers do differently in their first year?”

“Can you tell me what onboarding and training looks like?”

“How long have you been with the company? What would you say is the secret sauce to success?”

“Can you tell me what next steps look like in this interview process?”

“Do you see any reason why you wouldn’t move me forward to the next phase of the interview process?”

Sales managers love closers! Be a closer! The last two questions have to be asked! It shows them you understand how to ask for the business and you have no fear doing so.

Don’t just pick up the phone with these phone screens or you will find yourself in a “Jesus name amen” type of scenario. Your job is to make sure the person interviewing you understands that you are coachable, and that you understand that the job (and sales for that matter) aren’t easy. That you’re able to communicate clearly and show grit and accountability. And most importantly, in that final 10 seconds, you know and are willing to ask for the business. Do all that right and you’re ready for the next interview, as well as the next chapter; The In-Person Interview!
Chapter 5
The In-Person Interview - You Have No Idea What To Expect
You need to know something not a lot of people are going to tell you. Most people suck at interviewing. Sure, there are some great interviewers out there with some method to their madness, but you can’t go into these interviews thinking these people know what they are doing. The majority of hiring managers aren’t good at interviewing. That’s why they keep having to do it all the time. They aren’t good at finding the right people. They tend to look for what they think is going to work in the position they are trying to fill. You would assume that because hiring is so important these individuals would be really well versed in interviewing, but they aren’t. Most interviewers are still “going with their gut” or basing their hiring decisions off of how likeable a candidate is. I know, it’s crazy but understanding this concept is actually critical to landing you the job you're interviewing for.
I’ve been a part of hundreds of interviews where the hiring manager just talked about themselves, the company or the role for the first 15 to 20 minutes of the interview. It was painful watching the poor candidates just sit there and nod, agonizing and waiting for the interviewer to eventually ask about THEM! Interviewing like this is one of the reasons companies keep failing when it comes to talent acquisition and why sales reps keep missing quota. Wrong person, wrong fit. The manager is selling the job and they have no idea who they are selling to. They talk about the role as if to say, “do you think you could do that?” when they’ve finished their diatribe about the role. It makes no sense. They don’t think about the fact that every candidate will say yes because they want the job. Frankly, it’s why turnover is so high in some of these companies.
I’ve sat in other interviews where the hiring manager grinded the sales rep over everything. They pushed back on them and their resume, they debated them, and ultimately just made everyone either uncomfortable or angry. I sat there thinking that if I interviewed that hiring manager like they interviewed that candidate, they would have gotten up and left. When I pressed them on their interview style they dismissed it as if they were firewalking the candidate as to what “sales is really like” and they, “like to test their candidates to see if they could “hang” in a real sales type environment”. It was all wrong, but the candidate, and I, had to endure it. 
I’ve also watched some hiring managers who would go completely off track when they discovered that they had a couple of things in common with the candidate. They talked to the candidate like they were having coffee instead of interviewing them for a role that would cost the company a hundred thousand dollars if they turned over the position. One even asked about a bar and how good it was. It became an interview for a new friend, not one for the open position. I left the interview thinking, “We still don’t know anything about them in regards to their skillset. We just know where they like to hang out.”
Would you like to know why the interview and hiring process for most companies is so long and arduous? There’s two reasons, the cost of hiring the wrong person and the hiring manager's inability to interview people well so other opinions are needed. The company doesn’t invest in training their people on talent acquisition so they are forced to get more people involved to minimize the risk of hiring the wrong person. The cost of a bad hire for entry level sales positions can cost the company anywhere from 30% to 100% of that employee’s annual salary. Other expenses that companies incur from a bad hire are things like recruiting costs and advertising the open position, their ATS (Applicant tracking system), HR and admin, training materials and training costs.
Opportunity costs are the other costs the business incurs, things like lost revenue because no one is working the open territory, lost productivity, lost pipeline development and all of the managers time and energy training the new hire. People cost is a factor as well. The morale of the current sales teams morale also takes a hit with a failure. It creates friction with the sales team, leads to gossip and could leave to other reps leaving as well.
Most companies don’t want a long talent acquisition process but they are doing all they can to avoid the cost of making a mistake. Some smaller companies can’t afford the mistake at all. What they fail to realize is that it’s not the process or the number of layers of interviews that’s hindering them from selecting the right people who will succeed in their open role, it’s the skillset of interviewing that’s actually at the center of it all. They know how to ask questions but they don’t know people. Don’t believe me? Talk to a sales manager and ask them if they practice interviewing. They don’t…in most cases, they are practicing on you!
I’m not trying to talk bad about hiring managers, it’s just a reality that they don’t practice interviewing. I worked for a multi-billion dollar company for close to ten years and I had one training class on hiring salespeople during that time. Even when companies have a process it’s all still left up to interpretation when it comes time for the actual interview. People tend to default to standard interview questions, “Tell me about a time” or questions they have been asked in interviews that they really liked. Once you’ve been through a lot of interviews you’ll see what a broad range of skill sets there are out there in the hiring world. It’s scary how much they vary and sadly, both the candidate and companies suffer for it.
Again, none of this is bad news, it’s just a reality and you do need to be prepared for it. So what do you do?
Just like prospects in sales, most interviewers fit into a persona and it’s important that you understand who is interviewing you.. The core truth here is you are going to interview with a human and that human has bias, pressure, history and preferences . Each manager's identity, incentive and scars shape how they interview, what they listen for, and what answers “land” with them. If you study the following chapter you’ll be able to distinguish the interviewer style and flex your communication along with them. But before we jump into interviewers and their persona’s you need to understand the baselines 
Now that you understand the environment, the cost of getting a hire wrong, and how interviewers are trained — or more accurately, how they aren’t — let’s talk about what every single one of them is actually looking for. No matter who sits across from you, these are the things that move the needle. Think of this as the foundation you have to walk in with before you can tailor any . It’s also what you should be trying to communicate on your resume. 
With all of these different manager personas there are some things that are consistent with almost all of them. All sales manager personas are looking for the same foundations. Why? Because it’s their job to teach you the job. But there are certain things they can’t teach you. You have to demonstrate to them on the resume, in the phone interview and ultimately in the in-person interview so they ultimately choose you!
 Foundations That Every Manager Looks For:
Show up early to your interview. Don’t be afraid to wait in your car if you show up too early. Be in the office at least 10 minutes early. You may even want to make a dry run a day or two ahead of time so you know where you are going and know how to get there. At a minimum you should be using a traffic app to see what traffic will look like at the time of your interview. Trying to get hired is stressful enough, you don’t want to add unexpected traffic or an inability to find their office to that stress. 
Look professional and appropriately dressed for the role and the company. There’s a couple of ways to figure that out. You can ask but to a hiring manager that can seem like you’re asking, “how little effort do I have to put into this?” I suggest you look at their LinkedIn page and website to see if you can see what their people are dressed like. And dress like the most professional person in those pictures. You’ll never go wrong with being the best dressed person in the room. Buy a new suit or professional outfit. Make sure it fits great and that you try it on before the actual day of the interview. And iron what should be ironed. You’d think I wouldn’t have to say that, but trust me, I’ve seen more than enough unironed clothes that I have to say that.
You should bring a professional notebook or portfolio with several copies of your resume. You never know how many people are going to be in that face to face interview and you want to be prepared. Being prepared is impressive even in the small things and managers notice.
Keep your phone on silent and in your pocket. There’s nothing more distracting than a phone buzzing or notifications alerting in the middle of an interview. It’s distracting to you and agitates the person interviewing you. Shoutout to you ESPN for your oh so recognizable notification theme song that I've heard in way too many interviews.
There is no greater truth in life than FIRST IMPRESSIONS MATTER, especially in sales. And they matter in your interview. How you look, and more importantly how you carry yourself carries a lot of weight. How you treat the receptionist, how you sit down in the chair in the lobby. How you stand up when the interviewing manager comes to greet you in the lobby. Eye contact, a firm handshake and a smile all set the tone for getting that manager to like you. It will carry you incredibly far on the journey to get a job.
Quick tip: Try what’s known as the superhero pose either in the parking lot before you walk into the office. Believe it or not, the superhero pose (standing like superman with your hands on your hips and your chest out) will actually alter your body chemistry and perception. It makes you feel more assertive and less stressed by increasing your testosterone levels and decreasing your cortisol levels. Amy Cuddy’s work suggests power poses can increase your testosterone by 20% and decrease cortisol levels by 25%. Doing this pose signals to your brain that you are powerful and in control. It increases your confidence and hiring managers love confidence in their salespeople. I would actually have my new sales reps do this before they would make their first cold calls during training. The physical act of standing tall and open redirects your attention from stressors to the present moment and your body, and it gives you a sense of readiness. Try it now, tell me you don’t feel better.
Practice. Practice shaking hands, smiling and do it in the outfit you plan on wearing in your interview. You should firewalk the entire scenario so you feel comfortable in your own skin when you’re actually in the interview. Being comfortable is going to help you be more authentic.
One of the mental questions most managers think to themselves is, “would I buy from this person?” If you don’t present yourself as a confident, comfortable professional they won’t hire you. You’ll lose the opportunity before they even ask you a question.
 Coachability:
I tell people in interviews all the time, the secret to succeeding in any B2B sales role is to remember one thing. “Don’t die on an island.” I then go on to explain that it’s incredibly hard to excel if you’re solely relying on yourself. Managers want people who can be coached. They don’t want to have to wait for you to do things wrong and then start doing them right when you’re already failing. No matter what manager persona you are dealing with they can’t defend your results if you aren’t doing what they say. If you’re on an island trying and failing to do it your way no one is going to be able to save you. However if you do everything they say to do and you fail then the blame lies solely on you.
You have to portray yourself as someone who accepts feedback without being defensive. You’re new to this role, make sure they know you’ll act as a sponge and willing to do what needs to be done to succeed. Coaches love people who love to be coached. You will want to say things like, “I’m still learning, and I’m eager to be coached.” Talking about books you’re reading to get better at sales will make you appear to be a student of the sales game and they will love that. Most managers love managing people who are investing in themselves. They see it as you already buying in and willing to put in the extra work. In chapter 12 we provide a list of some of the sales books we recommend. Read at least one of them as soon as you can and talk about it in your interview. You’ll stand out from the other candidates just by being prepared.
Two of the worst things you can do in an interview are pretending you know something you don’t and cutting off someone interviewing you and finishing their thought. Salespeople should listen more than they talk and you should reflect that when you’re being interviewed. Be patient when they are speaking, pretending you know something that you don’t will expose you. You can’t get them to think you’re coachable and know everything at the same time. Don’t be afraid to tell them you don’t know something, it’s an opportunity to show you're coachable. Being overconfident with no experience is a huge red flag.
Work Ethic and Effort Signals:
Sales managers hire effort before results, especially at the entry level. Outside B2B sales managers can’t be with you every moment of the day. In these interviews they are trying to figure out if you have the drive and determination to get up every morning and go to work without them micro-managing you. It’s up to you to prove you’re the type of person that gets up and gets after it everyday.
Your work ethic and effort are going to be told in your stories. Working your way through college, playing sports, internships, service industry work and leadership roles all lend themselves to proving you’re willing to put in the work. It doesn’t necessarily matter where you did those things, what matters is that you did them, learned from them, and grew as a person. It shows you are willing to grind and get after it.
I like asking sales reps, “What is the earliest age you did something to make money?” Once a sales rep told me a story about how he and his brother would dive in a pond on a golf course and find golf balls they could resell to the golfers on the golf course. He said they did it over the summer to make money. When I asked him how much they actually made in a summer he said that their best summer they made $6,000. That’s a lot of money for two kids in high school. It was also a lot of work. It was impressive and what I really heard was that this was a candidate who’s money motivated and willing to do whatever it takes to make things happen. Being money motivated at an early age is something that doesn’t go away as you get older, it only amplifies. Hiring managers love money motivated candidates.
Hard work in hard working people is a default they resort back to when they encounter obstacles. They don’t run away from struggle, and when times are tough, they dig in and work harder. That’s the kind of drive sales managers want on their team. They want you to respect the game and understand that opportunity is earned not given. They want to know that repetition isn’t going to bother the candidate. That the repetition and unglamorous work of sales is seen as a means to an end and not something they are going to force them to do week in and week out. Show them you have learned to fall in love with the process and journey of getting better and not just the accolades or commissions.
Ownership and Accountability:
The worst thing you can do in an interview is blame someone else for a failure. They are going to expect you to own outcomes regardless of if they are good or bad. A bad manager, the wrong territory, limitations or restrictions to accomplishing the goal are all red flags in an interview. They want to know that you’re a winner and winners don’t play the blame game. Great sales managers know you’re not perfect. They want to know if you know that you’re not perfect. Being able to admit mistakes and faults shows a lot of maturity. 
Hiring managers are wildly suspicious of people that have never messed up. In sales, people don’t always hit their number. People fail. That happens. Having a resume that’s full of wins and no losses just makes you look suspicious, not impressive. You need to be able to own your mistakes and then share what you learned from them. Hiring managers love someone who shows an internal locus of control. Not sure what that is? A locus of control is a psychological concept that refers to whether someone believes their life outcomes are controlled by their own actions or by external factors like fate, luck or deity. Internal locus of control is a belief that you control your outcomes or you take your fate into your own hands. An external locus of control is someone whose life happens to them. 
The ability to own your outcomes and take accountability are foundational characteristics of a great sales rep and they make you easier to work with. They know that if you're willing to own up to making a mistake you won’t try to hide future mistakes from them when it matters. Ownership and accountability build trust with your manager because they know they can rely on you to tell the truth and that allows them to start fixing the problem right away.
Energy, Presence and Engagement:
There is a difference between hyper and energy and that’s presence. Intentional energy comes across in your posture and tone of voice. An interview is really all about selling yourself. Sitting in the chair confidently and professionally is helping you best present yourself and is indicative of how you will carry yourself when you are selling their product or service.

 Eye contact and speaking clearly will keep you present in the interview, so will listening carefully to their questions. Taking a breath or repeating their question back to them is a great way to make sure you understand what they are asking and give you some time to give you an answer. You want them to think that you’re patient and smart enough to answer their questions well.
You don’t have to wait for the end of the interview to ask questions either. If something interests you or sparks a thought and you want to dig deeper don’t hesitate to do it. Being interested is far better than being interesting. Showing that you're inquisitive in a role where that’s basically your job and that you’re willing to ask questions even in the midst of a stressful interview is impressive. It also gives you a lot of control in the conversation.
Being engaged and asking questions also confirm a strong desire to learn and be taught. Curiosity will get you a long way in sales so showing it in the interview will help your chances.
Integrity and Trustworthiness:
Near the end of interviewing a candidate I asked him, “what’s one thing I don’t know about you that I would know after we worked together for a year?” to which he responded, “99% of the time I’m honest.” And just like that, he lost his opportunity. I said, “Well I don’t think we can move forward” and when he asked ‘Why?’ I explained to him that I’ll never know when the 1% was going to be.
Sales managers need honesty from their people. They need to know that you’re going to tell them when something isn’t right, there’s a problem in an account, or a deal isn’t going to close. Those sales managers have bosses and if your lie causes them to lie to their boss you’re going to be in a tough situation that’s hard to dig out of.
Your answers have to be consistent all of the way through the interview and consistent with your resume. Don’t exaggerate your achievements to them either. They are what they are and making them seem bigger or better than they actually are makes them think you will do the same thing when it comes to deals in your pipeline.
Stay positive about past jobs or managers as well. Your integrity is reflected on how you speak of others and it’s important that you don’t put anyone down. If you’re willing to do it to your past managers, what will you say about them?  Managers are in the business of teaching sales skills, but they can’t teach integrity. You have to show up with that.
Commitment and Realism:
Most sales managers try to be very realistic with the job expectations. In fact if they are overselling the position you probably don’t want it. However, if they are being honest with the work that’s required to win, listen to them very carefully. They want to make sure you’re willing to put the time and effort into being successful because they are about to invest a lot into you.
Most people don’t think about it but these hiring managers need you to be successful. Their success depends on your success. How well you perform will affect their bonuses and how well you do will also affect their ability to get promoted. They are going to check to see if you’re willing to make that commitment to them and not just the company.
Sales isn’t a 9-5 job and it’s not easy. They will want you to have an understanding of the work and effort it’s going to take to succeed in the role. Sales cycles can be long as well and that means gratification isn’t instant and they will want you to know that winning doesn’t happen fast.
It’s one thing to get the job, it’s another to put your head down and go to work. That’s what they are going to want to know. Make sure you are the person you say you are.
 Respect for the Process:
Almost every manager, just like a customer, has a persona. And just like in sales, once you understand who you're dealing with, you can adjust your approach and dramatically improve your chances of walking out with what you came for.
Before we get into those profiles, there are a few things that hold true across almost all of them. Most managers will respect the interview process regardless of their style. They may be under pressure to fill their team quickly and skip steps to get someone onboarded fast, but they still expect you to follow the process and show up prepared. Do not be the reason the interview falls apart on the logistics side.
Keep an eye on the time as well. If you're bumping up against the time limit they set, be the one who calls attention to it. Say something like, "I want to be respectful of your time — you mentioned we'd go until 2:00. Are you okay to continue?" That's exactly what a great salesperson would say to a customer, and doing it in an interview shows them you're already thinking that way. It's a small moment but a memorable one.
Now let's talk about who's actually sitting across from you. Understanding that there are real people on the other side of the desk will help you know how to speak to them and increase your confidence in the interview. In the next chapter we're going to break down the five hiring manager personas you're most likely to encounter. Learn to spot them early and you'll know exactly what they need to hear to say yes.





















Chapter 6
Know Who's Interviewing You
 
Most candidates prepare for the interview. Very few prepare for the interviewer. That's the edge this chapter gives you. Just like you would research a prospect before a sales call, you need to read the person sitting across from you and flex your approach accordingly. The five personas below are the most common hiring manager types you'll run into. Learn to recognize them and you'll know exactly what they're looking for and how to give it to them.
Let's go over the Hiring Manager Persona. These are the five you'll encounter the most. I've ranked them best to worst.
The Developer: (Coach)
If you're new in your sales career you are looking to work for a Developer. They value training and growth, and they take pride in it. They are in the business of people and understand that growing and developing people lead to amazing results and tenure. People don't leave bad work environments; they leave bad managers. Developers are people that have low turnover and a spot on their team is valuable to them and the rest of the team.
Developers are strong people leaders and focused on any candidate working for them for as long as they can keep them. They value loyalty, hard work and effort.
Their interview style will be reflective in nature and often rely on behavioral situations and outcomes. They approach interviewing in a way where they act less like an interrogator and more like a mirror and guide. They will use listening, paraphrasing and thoughtful follow up questions to help the candidate reveal how they think, learn and make meaning from their experience. It's an especially effective technique when the goal is to assess self-awareness, coachability, accountability, values and alignment.
What wins with Developers is being able to quantify lessons you've learned. You don't need sales experience to have this conversation; you just have to associate your lessons with foundational skills in line with sales. Stories of your tenacity, competitiveness, learning, and loyalty all "land" with this type of interviewer.
They look for stories where you search for feedback from peers and leaders. Telling a Developer about a network of people who help you or an accountability group that keeps you on task and pushing forward will be music to their ears. They want to see that you understand and value the concept of a team or group pushing you forward. Self-development is also important to them. They appreciate people looking to better themselves so things like reading, podcasts, and certifications you were able to obtain outside of required school or work obligations make you a very appealing candidate to them. If they ask about your reading or development habits, it's a great opportunity to ask for their recommendations. It gives you a genuine reason to follow up after the interview.
It's hard to lie to or mislead a Developer. They will ask a lot of follow-up questions, especially if you tell a story that they are interested in or reference a book they have also read. When you speak like Developers do, they will be interested. Authenticity matters to them.
Developers don't like a candidate who is defensive. Defensiveness will put them on high alert and make them think you are hiding something from them. They don't like secrets. Blaming others will also put them on high alert. They want people who own their progress and their setbacks. Setbacks are where Developers have learned a lot of lessons and they enjoy teaching from them. Being willing to admit failure and explain lessons from that failure will really resonate with them. They look for self-reflection because they are introspective themselves.
Questions Developers could ask as well as some example answers:
Q: "Tell me about something new you had to learn quickly."
A: "One example was when I had to learn [new system / role / process] very quickly. I didn't have prior experience with it, but I knew I needed to ramp up fast. I started by breaking it into the basics of what I needed to know to be effective right away versus what could come later. I asked questions early, took notes and practiced outside of the actual task so I wouldn't slow anyone else down. Within a short time, I was able to help others. It showed me that I learn best by taking ownership and getting reps instead of waiting to feel ready. And that's something I'm comfortable doing again in a sales role."
Avoid saying something like "I'm a fast learner." Everyone says that and it makes you sound like all the other candidates. Interviewers want stories, not platitudes. You must show them how you've done something, not that you are something.
Q: "How do you typically respond to feedback or coaching?"
A: "I try to separate ego from execution. I listen first, ask clarifying questions, and then apply feedback immediately. Even if I don't fully agree at first, I test it before forming an opinion."
What Developers are really asking: Can I coach you without resistance and are you willing to change?
Q: "Tell me about a time you didn't hit a goal."
A: "I struggled early in a class where I didn't manage my time well. Once I adjusted my schedule and study habits, I was able to get back on track and my grades improved."
Q: "What did you learn from that experience?"
A: "That effort needs structure. Working hard isn't enough if I'm not organized and keeping my goals in front of me."
What Developers are really asking: Do you own your results or blame others?
Q: "Tell me about a mistake you've made that changed how you operate."
A: "I was on a team and assumed I understood the instructions for the project. Instead of asking questions I did the entire assignment incorrectly and handed it in. I learned to take my time in clarifying the goal and instructions of the project. Now I've just made it a habit."
Q: "How do you prioritize when everything feels urgent?"
A: "When everything feels urgent, I slow down and get organized instead of reacting. I write everything out, identify what's time sensitive or directly tied to results and start there. If I'm unsure about priorities, I ask for clarification. I've learned that being clear and communicative is better than trying to do everything at once."
What Developers are really asking: Can you follow a system even if it's uncomfortable?
Q: "What type of manager helps you perform at your best?"
A: "I'd like a manager who sets clear expectations, gives honest feedback, and is invested in developing my skillset and helps me learn from my mistakes."
What Developers are really asking: Will you thrive in a coaching heavy culture or fight it?
A Developer will look for things that show growth both personally and professionally. They are trying to see if you're self-aware and willing to be coached. They want to see examples of growth and a desire and willingness to grow on their team.
 
The Builder: (Player / Coach)
The Builder is usually a sales rep that's been promoted from the field to a managerial role. They take a lot of pride in earning the promotion and they are pretty certain they know how to succeed in the role they are interviewing for. If they are new in the role, they will look to hire people like them because they are the blueprint they follow. They have a strong belief that sales is earned and not taught. They love working shoulder to shoulder with salespeople and helping them close deals. Builders want to be involved and help drag deals across the finish line.
Their interview style is conversational but not necessarily probing. They are interested in your stories especially when you can tell them in a way that highlights grit, work ethic and tenacity. They like to hear how you've earned your scars but want you humble enough to admit you still have a lot to learn from them. Builders care less about polish and more about how you think, how you adapt in the field and how you respond when there's no playbook.
They really want to know that your tenacity to succeed means you'll try to out work everyone else on the team. That's who they see themselves as and that's what they value! They aren't looking for perfect people; they are looking for people who own their success as well as their failures. If you're talking about failures you need to talk about what you learned, how you bounced back and how it led you to be successful.
Builders like people who start from the bottom because they see their reflection in those people. As a result, they tend to like to give people a chance to be successful. They also expect you to work harder because that is their secret to success.
They appreciate humility and effort. Blue collar stories speak to a Builder's soul. Builders don't think they are better than anyone else. They think their hard work is what got them where they are. Telling them stories of real work, perseverance, and grit go miles with builders. They relate quickly with other builders and can't wait to work with them.
Builders don't like a candidate who comes across as entitled. They have a disdain for people who have been handed things or haven't had to work for what they've gotten. They like candidates who live where their feet are. They don't mind if you have dreams, instead they will hate it if you aren't working towards them. But they want you to be grounded. They want you to understand that work is what helps you achieve those dreams and earning it is everything. If you're too over polished, you could turn a Builder off. Saying things like I want work-life balance to a Builder too early in the process will stick in their brain forever.
Questions Builders could ask as well as some example answers:
Q: "When have you taken ownership of something that wasn't officially your responsibility?"
A: "At work, customers kept asking the same questions. I started writing down answers and sharing them with coworkers so we could respond faster. Later I brought it up to my manager and they were able to make clarifications with the process. The change we made answered the questions ahead of time. I felt like I owned the short term and long-term solution. I really enjoyed that."
Q: "Tell me about a problem you noticed before someone asked you to fix it."
A: "I worked in a restaurant while in school and noticed that when we hired a new person they all kept making the same mistakes. I started helping them and walking them through the processes informally. Once we figured out the disconnect, I was able to talk to my manager, and we worked together to fix the training."
What Builders are really asking: Do you create momentum or wait for instructions?
Q: "Walk me through how you'd approach your first 30 days here."
A: "I think it's important to focus on learning the role. I'd like to understand what the expectations are and what's required to exceed them. I'd talk to people who are exceeding expectations so I can figure out what they do and how. I think then it's all about executing the process and asking questions to make sure I set myself up for success."
What Builders are really asking: Do you process things logically or do you just use buzzwords?
Q: "Tell me about a time you struggled but didn't quit."
A: "I like to try new things and do things I wouldn't normally do because I like the experience of figuring things out. I joined a club for my major. Most people join their junior or senior year, but I joined my sophomore year. I was in way over my head, but I saw the value of getting started early. It was hard but I stayed with it and everyone was more than eager to help me catch up and I eventually became a leader in that group."
Q: "Tell me about a failure you learned from."
A: "I was in a leadership position in a club that I was participating in and completely underestimated how long it would take me to complete a piece of the project. I let the whole team down. It taught me to better understand the scope of work needed for projects and classes. Now I plan ahead, communicate early if it's a group project and think about getting things done early so I come through on time or ahead of schedule."
What Builders are really asking: Are you comfortable and able to handle discomfort or do you fold?
Q: "When you don't get immediate feedback, how do you decide whether to keep going or change direction?"
A: "I look at effort vs progress. If I'm putting in consistent effort but not seeing any improvement, that tells me I should try something different or ask for input. If I'm seeing small signs of progress, I stay consistent. I don't wait too long to make adjustments, but I'm really careful not to abandon something too early just because it doesn't work right away."
What Builders are really asking: Are you willing to build your future or are you looking for it to be handed to you?
A builder will look for things that you did, what you noticed and how you think things through. They aren't looking for perfect answers or corporate language. They are looking for candidates that take ownership, have curiosity and a strong bias towards action. They love people who are comfortable with being uncomfortable. They don't mind if you make mistakes as long as you make them fast and react.
 
Operator: (Process Manager)
Operators are exactly what you would expect. They are a system and process manager. They manage each KPI and metric the system provides. They thrive off of the rigidity and structure of the role. If you're a process driven person you will appreciate how they manage and coach. They will also value the training model and execute the entire training process. If you're a person who's getting into sales because of its freedoms, this manager will make you feel like you're being micro-managed. On the other hand, if you're new and a person who wants to know everything you can about the company and what it takes to succeed, an Operator will walk you through it.
Operators are very structured and look for it in candidates. Their interview process will appear repetitive in its nature and sometimes they may even ask the same question in a different way. They thrive on structure so the interview will be agenda and checklist driven. Expect them to take notes during the interview. They don't like to miss on anything that's measured, especially the success of new hires. They will most likely use the form provided by the company as a checklist for how the interview should go.
Operators want to hear stories of how you followed a process and how it led to your success. They like to take care of the little things because they believe that's how you achieve the big things. Operators want to know they can rely on you to do the job and that you like doing things the way the company does them. Stories of how you were there when someone needed you or a story where the team relied on you and you came through will do very well with them. Lastly they want to be assured that you'll put the work in and hit the activity metrics that the company says someone in your role should.
You succeed with an Operator by keeping your answers organized and to the point. Avoid tangents at all costs. The only experience you need with Operators is discipline to a routine. Showing them that you have routines in place and how they have helped you succeed will go a long way in getting the role. Your answers should highlight your ability to be coached and that you actively search for coaching.
Operators don't like to be pulled off track so trying to get around a topic or a particular question will leave a bad taste in their mouth about the whole interview. It will rub them the wrong way whether they are conscious of it or not. Stay on track, it will win you the next step or even the job.
Operators don't like a candidate who is scattered in their thinking and all over the place with their answers. A freelance mentality where you "want to see what sales is like" will frustrate them and they will dig in further to see if they really want to work with you. Independence and stories of how you did things your way will all but eliminate you from the interview process. It's not that they think it's wrong, they just don't understand how that type of thinking leads to their success. They are process driven, consistent, coachable, reliable and they show up everyday. They trust activity, habits and systems. That's how they win, and that's how they expect their people to win.
Questions Operators could ask as well as some sample answers:
Q: "When you're given a new responsibility, what's the first thing you usually do?"
A: "I make sure I fully understand what's expected, then I look for examples or guidelines so I can follow the process correctly from the start."
Q: "What do you do to avoid small mistakes?"
A: "I slow down, double check my work and use checklists so I don't rely on memory alone. I've found that sticking to the plan usually yields the best results."
What Operators are really asking: Can you think in steps, follow directions and avoid overcomplicating things?
Q: "What helps you push through days when you're not excited about the work?"
A: "I've learned that having a routine is the best way for me not to wait on being motivated. I rely on habits to keep me moving. Motivation will come and go but doing the things I need to do will actually get me where I want to be."
What Operators are really asking: Can you keep going when it's boring, uncomfortable or discouraging? Do you show up everyday ready to dive in and hit your numbers?
Q: "How do you prefer to receive direction from a manager?"
A: "I like direction that's clear and direct. I want to know exactly what's expected so I can execute correctly. I can execute on what to do and appreciate feedback on how I can do things better."
Q: "How do you handle responsibilities that others depend on you for?"
A: "In a team environment I know that I have to prioritize what's important and take it seriously. Being prepared and reliable is so important to me because I don't want to let others down."
What Operators are really asking: Can I trust you with responsibility when no one is watching?
Q: "We have very clear expectations and structure with this role. What attracts you to that?"
A: "I like expectations and structure. KPIs and metrics remove the guesswork of what needs to be done and allows me to focus on execution and improvement."
Q: "This type of sales role rewards consistency more than quick wins. How does that sit with you?"
A: "That fits exactly how I like to work. I'm comfortable building to success through steady effort. A quick win would feel more like a fortunate piece of luck."
What Operators are really asking: Are you clear eyed about the grind and still willing to do it?
An Operator is looking for low ego and high discipline. They want to make sure that you are comfortable with the structure of the position and the repetition that will be involved. Operators want to see examples or stories that reinforce your coachability and effort will beat talent every day. They are comfortable with building your skillset around your work ethic.
 
The Closer: (Results First)
Closers close and they pride themselves on it! This results oriented manager loves a competitor! They want competitors on their team. They manage and motivate like an athlete where hard work and practice are a sure fire way to win. They are extremely motivated and they were most likely the top performing rep on the team until they got promoted. They know how to win and are obsessed with it. They chase performance and love winning!
Closers are high energy and push themselves and their people hard to accomplish the goal. Working with them can be very fun and rewarding if you see yourself as a Closer too. Alignment with that personality profile is key.
Their interview style will be just like them. High energy and sometimes feel like it's fast paced. With Closers you could win or lose the opportunity just meeting them in the lobby. It's important you match their energy. They love winners, act like one. They push hard in interviews in order to test the confidence of the people sitting across from them. They are gut checking you to see if you flinch. Your responses to them can't be rude but they have to be courageous. Don't take prodding as a reflection of you or your skills, it's always a test of your toughness.
They are looking to see if you really want to win. They want to see if you hate losing as they see that as a driver in achieving goals. Telling them stories of being overly competitive and doing anything to win will go a long way in winning a spot on their team.
What wins with Closers is hard fought competition based stories. They are looking for your motor. They want to know what drives you and they are looking for that answer to be competition and a willingness to do anything to win. They are driven by numbers and anything that has to do with achievement. Any experience you can relate in your conversations to exceeding your goals will go miles with them. Closers want to know that you want to be the best!
Closers don't like passive people. If you're not fired up to be there they will already think about who they are going to interview next. Coming across as doing the bare minimum or a willingness to be average will cause them visible frustration. They value goals and they want to know that you have them and getting this job you're interviewing with them for is one of them.
Closers are tough to interview with and harder to hide from. Everything they do is a confidence test. They want to know if you can "hang" with them. They ask direct, pressure testing questions and push for commitment and clarity. A sense of urgency, confidence and accountability matter more to them than the polish of a seasoned sales vet. They believe hunger outworks talent.
Throughout the interview they will be trying to answer one question: "Can I trust you to ask for the business even when it's uncomfortable."
Questions Closers could ask as well as some example answers:
Q: "Do you want a job or do you want to win?"
A: "I want to win. I'm early in my career, but I'm not looking for something comfortable. I want to be measured and improve. I want a role where performance matters, where I can be measured, coached and pushed to improve. I'm motivated by progress and competition not stability and sales seems like a career where effort and skill directly translate into results."
What Closers are really asking: Can this person answer directly without rambling, hedging their answers or overexplaining?
Q: "Tell me something you're not good at."
A: "Organization hasn't always come naturally to me, especially when I have a lot of things happening at once. I've learned that if I don't build structure intentionally, it can slow me down. So I've developed systems: calendars, task lists, and preparing the night before so I make sure it doesn't become a weakness. It's something I'm actively improving, and the process has actually made me more disciplined overall."
What Closers are really asking: Will this person shrink, justify their loss, or stay steady when pushed?
Q: "What are you willing to give up to be good at this?"
A: "Comfort and short-term convenience. From what I've learned, success in this kind of role doesn't happen strictly between 9 and 5. It comes from preparation, repetition, and pushing through rejection. That might mean extra time practicing, learning, prospecting, or following up when it would be easier not to. I don't see that as a sacrifice so much as an investment in building real skills early in my career. A career in sales is a high reward opportunity, the effort should be high as well."
What Closers are really asking: Will this person actually do the job and not just talk about it?
Q: "If you miss your number, what's your explanation?"
A: "I wouldn't look for an excuse, I'd look for a diagnosis. I'd want to understand where I missed. Was it my pipeline, activity, conversion or execution? From there, I'd get feedback from my manager and adjust the plan so it doesn't happen again. Missing a target should trigger analysis and action, not justification."
What Closers are really asking: This is where I make my decision. Are you the person I want on this team?
Q: "Do you believe that asking for the business is pushy?"
A: "No. I think avoiding asking for the business is actually less professional. If you've taken the time to understand someone's situation and believe you can help, it's reasonable to ask for a decision. Being pushy is trying to force something that doesn't fit, but being respectful of everyone's time. I'd be comfortable hearing a yes or a no but not asking leaves things unresolved and doesn't serve either party."
What Closers are really asking: These are my non-negotiables, can you live with them?
A Closer doesn't mind asking tough questions because they feel like the sales environment you are applying for is just as harsh if not harsher. They look for equal stature in candidates, meaning how would I answer this question? They love confidence and your answers have to reflect that tone. You can't be afraid of your lack of experience here, you have to show them that your willingness to work and learn make up for any experience.
 
The Pressure Cooker: (Under the Gun)
There are two sides to the interview with the Pressure Cooker. The Pressure Cooker is struggling in their role. They have either lost the locker room meaning no one is listening to them anymore or they aren't hitting numbers. They are stressed and feel the pressure from people above them because they aren't doing well in their position. That's the downside. They aren't winning and they are backed in a corner. On the other hand, depending on the time frame, you may be able to prove yourself worthy of a promotion that they are going to vacate soon.
It should go without saying, but I'll say it just in case you need to hear it. You should never, at any point, undermine your manager. If they get a whiff of it they will get rid of you. And those higher ups putting pressure on them will see it and you'll never go anywhere in that company. Plus it's just flat out the wrong thing to do. Don't do it. You wouldn't want it done to you and even if you were able to get the job by sabotaging the current manager the whole team will know who you really are and they won't forget it. It's not worth it.
Their interview style will be quick. They are trying to put out a lot of fires and can't keep their attention on one thing. Be prepared to not have their full attention. It will be a tough interview because they are looking for someone who can help dig them out of their hole. It's even harder because with little to no experience you aren't the best candidate to help them save their job. But they could be under pressure because they don't have a full team and they may just hire the first person to walk in the door to keep headcount where they need it to be.
Don't give up on this person. They need someone who's going to be on their team and help them. If you can do that, they will never forget it. They may seem to be a little skeptical about how successful someone can be because they haven't figured it out yet. You'll feel like you're on your own. This is a real gut check time for you. You're going to need to build an internal network within the company and get as much advice as possible so you can succeed.
The Pressure Cooker really wants to know a couple of things about you at the end of this interview. Are you a person who has the potential to produce fast and deliver effective results? They want to know if things get tough, will you quit and they will soon find themselves right back in the same place with even more pressure in a couple of months. They are willing to do anything to avoid risk at this point. They can't afford to have anything go wrong especially when it comes to staffing. You have to prove you're the safest bet. You do that with alignment.
In this interview you will want to dig in with them with good questions to see how you can best help them get to where they want to go. If they sense that you are on their side and willing to dig in and help, that may be all you need to get the job. If you're doing well, this interview will feel much more like a conversation than an interview.
The Pressure Cooker is looking for candidates who are confident and have a high sense of urgency. They want to know that you are going to run at their problems with them. They like short, decisive answers to their questions. They are feeling all the pressure and owning it day in and day out. They want to make sure you are also willing to own your past failures.
The Pressure Cooker doesn't like long explanations or rambling answers or any type of excuses. They don't like candidates who lack confidence or someone who is going to need constant reassurance. Pressure Cookers don't need another person who is disloyal to them and if you allude to any lack of loyalty they will see it as a threat.
Questions Pressure Cookers ask as well as some example answers:
Q: "When was the last time you had to own a mistake?"
A: "I misunderstood instructions on an assignment and submitted it incorrectly. I owned it immediately, asked how to fix it and corrected it before the deadline. I learned to clarify expectations upfront."
Q: "How do you respond when someone tells you you're wrong?"
A: "I listen first. I want to understand their perspective before reacting. If they are right, I want to correct course quickly rather than defend a mistake. If I'm unsure, I ask questions so I fully understand what needs to change. I don't take feedback personally because improvement depends on it, especially in a performance driven role where small adjustments can make a big difference."
What Pressure Cookers are really asking: Do you take responsibility or make excuses?
Q: "This job involves rejection every day. Why won't that break you?"
A: "I expect rejection. I don't tie it to my self-worth. I focus on effort, activity and improvement instead of outcomes I can't control."
Q: "What motivates you when no one is watching?"
A: "I'm motivated by progress more than recognition. Seeing improvements in my activity, skills, or results give me a sense of momentum, even if no one else sees it yet. I like setting personal benchmarks and measuring against them because it keeps me focused on long-term growth rather than short-term validation. External praise is nice, but it's not reliable. Progress is. If I know I'm getting better, that's enough for me to stay engaged."
What Pressure Cookers are really asking: Can they handle rejection and discomfort?
Q: "What would frustrate a manager about you?"
A: "Early on, I may ask a lot of questions. My intention isn't to slow things down, it's to make sure I understand expectations clearly so I can execute correctly. Once I have that clarity, I move quickly and independently. I'm comfortable making mistakes as part of learning, but I try hard not to repeat the same one twice."
What Pressure Cookers are really asking: Can you be developed?
Q: "Why should we take a chance on you?"
A: "You'll get consistent effort, coachability and a strong desire to prove myself. I may not have a lot of experience yet, but I do have urgency and discipline and those are things I can control every day. My goal would be to absorb the training quickly, execute the process, and earn trust through results over time. I'm not expecting shortcuts, just an opportunity to demonstrate what I can do."
Q: "Why won't you quit when this gets hard?"
A: "I know this role isn't going to be easy. If it were easy, it wouldn't offer the same opportunity for growth and advancement. When things get hard, I try to focus on why I started and what I'm building toward long term. Quitting might be easier, but it won't get me where I want to go and I'm more interested in progress than comfort."
What Pressure Cookers are really asking: Do you understand the grind?
A Pressure Cooker will care about tone and composure. They will want you to be simplistic in your approach and clearly communicate. Taking ownership will impress them and so will honesty. They are looking for candidates who are calm, humble and hungry. They know ownership beats excuses and coachability beats confidence.
 
Now that you know who's interviewing you and how to speak their language, it's time to talk about the questions you're going to ask them. These are the questions you can ask at the end of an interview. This is where the strong candidates separate themselves from everyone else. That's what we cover next.






















Chapter 7
Asking Great Questions
 
Whatever you do, do not skip through this chapter, you need to read it! You getting hired could very well depend a lot on how you handle the last five to fifteen minutes of the interview. And those last few minutes are what is going to set you apart from all the other candidates. Because you're going to ask great questions!
Imagine for a minute that you just walked into your favorite watering hole and as you're making your way through the crowd you see your best friend sitting at the bar. To your surprise you realize they are in a cast from their hips to their shoulders. A full cast fresh from the doctor's office. Would you have any questions? You bet you would! Know why? Because you care. Because there has to be some sort of story behind what happened to them. Because you don't know what's going on and you have to hear the story!
Now imagine if you didn't ask any questions at all. What if you just saddled up to the bar next to them and asked the bartender for a drink. Or worse, you looked them dead in the eyes and said, "How's your day been?" How important would your friend feel? How would they think you felt about them?
Truth is, you wouldn't do that. You'd be going nuts trying to figure out what happened, how it happened and how you can help them. You'd probably even forget to order a drink. You'd be too wrapped up trying to figure out what happened to think or talk about anything else. The person interviewing you is your friend at the bar. They want you to ask questions. They have a story to tell you, you just have to ask.
The best candidates act like they are being recruited for the job, not desperate to have it. You're about to make one of the biggest decisions of your life. You don't think you should be trying to get all the details about what you'll be doing, who you'll be working with and what success looks like? Career trajectory is one of the most important things you can be thinking about at this point in your life. You should want to know exactly why you want this job and how to succeed at it.
Under the surface you can be excited just to be in the interview and excited at the chance to get the job, but you have to keep your cool. "I'm excited for the opportunity and I don't have any questions other than when can I start" is not winning you the job. That's a weak close and a blown opportunity. You'll be back to job searching faster than you can leave their building. There has to be something in you that shifts the dynamic from the interviewer trying to disqualify you to them knowing you're the perfect candidate.
This chapter is about how you do that. Quick secret: it's the questions you are going to ask them.
Strong candidates use the end of the interview to qualify the job to see if it's a fit for them. It's a chance to demonstrate to the person interviewing you that you are thinking beyond just getting the job. Asking the right questions can demonstrate long term thinking, business acumen and show your interviewer that you plan on owning the role and the outcomes from your effort. It's also very impressive when you ask thought provoking questions. Know why? Because how you approach the interview is a good glimpse into how you will approach cold calling, discovery and presentations to prospects. Especially when you're asking questions in a high stress environment like an interview. It demonstrates a maturity and confidence that not many candidates do.
You won't have time to ask all of these questions and you shouldn't try. With only five to fifteen minutes you'll have to figure out which ones matter most to you. Some of your questions will also get answered during the interview itself, so it's a good idea to bring this list with you, take notes and cross off what's already been covered. Just pulling out a written list of questions when you're asked will be impressive on its own.
When selecting which questions to ask, keep in mind the manager persona you're sitting across from. The first version of each question below is direct. The second is softer. Use the one that fits who you're interviewing with.
 
Developers
Developers care about coaching their people, learning, having a growth mindset and people development.
Questions:
Q: "How are new reps coached once the initial training ends?"
Alternatively you could phrase it as:
"I've heard a lot about your company's training and that's one of the things I'm excited about. Could you also share with me how you coach new sales reps to be successful after that training? My goal is to develop skills and habits to be successful at this and I know that doesn't just happen in regular training."
Q: "How often do managers ride along or review calls?"
Make sure to frame this question in a way that makes it clear you're hoping it happens, not trying to avoid it. You could say:
"One of the things I'm looking for as I interview with companies is to be developed because I think sales is something I want to do long term. Can you tell me how often you'll ride with me or go over calls with me? What does that look like, for you, here?"
Q: "What behaviors matter more than raw results early on?"
"I know that in sales, results matter. However, what are some other behaviors that you have seen early on in reps that have been successful?"
Q: "If I were to ask sales reps who've been here a couple of years what sales skill they have developed best while working here, what would they say?"
"What skills do you believe this role develops better than most entry level sales positions?"
Q: "I'm not looking to struggle at this job but I know sales is filled with ups and downs. Can you share with me what happens when someone is falling behind on quota?"
"What happens when someone is struggling?"
Developers love curiosity and humility in candidates because those people are easy to coach. Asking them questions that highlight your desire to win and a curiosity that shows you're interested in cracking the code of success will light a spark in them.
 
Builders
Builders care about growth, scalability and long term upside in a sales person. They want the right candidate to want to be a part of growing something meaningful with them.
Questions:
Q: "What separates your top performers from average performers here?"
"Every sales team has great sales reps and other reps that struggle. What would you say the great ones do that the average ones don't?"
Q: "What mistakes do new reps commonly make in their first year, and what advice would you give to avoid them?"
This is a straightforward question but folding in the second part does a lot of work. It shows you're already thinking about how to get ahead of the learning curve, not just survive it.
Q: "What does career progression typically look like for someone who performs?"
"In my research I've noticed a lot of people start in the position I'm applying for and move up. I know that has to be earned over time but if I'm performing well what would that opportunity look like?"
Q: "What excites you most about the future of this team and this company?"
"When you walk into work every day what gets you excited about the talent on this team and the future you have in this company?"
Q: "Let's say you've decided to hire me and we fast forward to a year from now. What would you say I did right?"
"If I were sitting in this seat a year from now doing well, what would I have done right?"
Builders want people who are thinking beyond getting hired and building a career. Showing them you have a desire to put in the time, earn a promotion and build a career at their company allows them to see you as an instant member of the team.
 
Operators
Operators care about the process their company follows and the consistency it takes to be successful. They want you to deliver on the execution of the metrics for the role and be disciplined enough to achieve them. They also want you to be reliable and predictable and show up ready to work every day.
Questions:
Q: "What does success look like in the first 30, 60, and 90 days for this role?"
"I like measuring and getting feedback. Do you have benchmarks for what success looks like in the first 30, 60 and 90 days of this role?"
Q: "After training and I'm on my own, can you walk me through what I can expect a typical day and week to look like in this position?"
"Can you walk me through a typical day or week in this role?"
Q: "From your experience, where do you see deals start to stall? And when is it a good idea to get you involved?"
"Where do most deals get stuck or fall apart in the sales process?"
Q: "What activities here do you think are non-negotiable?"
"With my research and reading it seems there are a lot of things that go into sales and being successful at it. What activities here do you think are non-negotiable?"
Q: "How transparent is performance data across the team? I want to benchmark myself against top performers and understand what's working."
"How transparent is performance data across the team?"
Operators respect candidates who want clarity in the position they are applying for. They respect the candidate who looks for structure and accountability. Operators don't want hype, they want you to look for realistic expectations and understand what it's going to take to succeed.
 
Closers
Closers care about results, accountability, competitiveness and standards. Their ideal candidate is sharp, prepared and willing to ask the tough questions and get straight answers.
Questions:
Q: "What percentage of reps are consistently hitting goal?"
"How many sales reps right now are hitting their goal and what would you say is stopping the others from succeeding?"
Q: "Going from training to actually closing deals, what does it look like to get off to a great start?"
"What does a great first customer win usually look like?"
Q: "What keeps your top performers motivated week in and week out?"
Q: "What kind of person struggles here, even if they are talented?"
"What are some indicators that a person is struggling and what advice do you give them to help them turn it around?"
Q: "Based on our conversation, do you see any concerns about my fit for this role?"
A Closer doesn't want a soft version of this question. They want you to close them. Asking this directly is an early indicator that you'll ask for the business in front of a prospect when it's time. Don't hedge it.
Closers respect candidates who aren't afraid of the truth and can handle pressure. Demonstrating that you can return that pressure with questions of your own will show them you can be a vital part of the team.
 
The Pressure Cooker
Pressure Cookers value a candidate who has composure under stress, demonstrates personal ownership and a willingness to be coached. They want a rep who stays calm and thinks clearly. They tend to test whether a candidate will fold, fight or learn. This is your opportunity to show them you lean in and learn.
Questions:
Q: "What does this job look like on its hardest days?"
"What usually causes people to fail in this role?"
Q: "In my interviews I've realized that most managers have non-negotiables on what it takes to be successful. What are yours?"
"What are your non-negotiables for someone on your team?"
Q: "When you are evaluating your team's performance, how do you decide who you invest more coaching into?"
"How do you decide who to invest more coaching into?"
Q: "What's the way to get off to a strong start in week one here?"
"What does a strong rep do in week one?"
Q: "Can you tell me why this role is open?"
This is a temperature check question and it's an important one. If you can tell you're sitting across from a Pressure Cooker, you need to know why they have open roles on their team. You need to know if people are leaving because of the environment. The answer will tell you a lot about what you're walking into.
Pressure Cookers are fast paced and usually trying to put out a lot of fires. Their interviewing style will cut right to the chase. They aren't afraid to push back on you and test your polish under pressure. They aren't trying to be rude, they are trying to survive. Show them you can help carry the load and they won't forget it.
 
Questions That Work With Any Persona
While managers have different interview styles they will tend to fit one of the five personas above. But because of those different styles there are rare occasions when it proves difficult to put your finger on which profile they fit. Because of that, it's important to have some questions that could work with any of them.
The important thing to remember is that there are foundational characteristics that sales managers are looking for in candidates regardless of their style. If you feel like you haven't been able to highlight those characteristics well enough, the questions at the end of the interview are a great way to bring those back out and reinforce them.
These questions are designed to show maturity, demonstrate the ability to think like a salesperson and help qualify the role without making you seem like you're trying too hard.
Questions:
Q: "What's something high performers here do that's not written in the playbook?"
This question signals an intelligent curiosity and a desire to crack the code to success with pattern recognition.
Q: "What tension exists in this role that someone needs to be comfortable with outside of just getting a lot of no's?"
This question demonstrates an emotional maturity and realism about what sales actually looks like day to day.
Q: "What does strong judgment look like in this position?"
This question moves beyond the effort it takes to do the job and into what it takes to think like a successful salesperson.
Q: "What assumptions would someone need to let go of to succeed here?"
This question shows your coachability and a willingness to let go of any preconceived notions about what it takes to be successful.
Q: "How does this team respond when results and effort don't line up?"
This question takes a failure you might encounter and rephrases it in a way that puts the team at the center. The answer will reflect how they will manage or coach you through it.
Q: "What would tell you, early on, that you made the right hiring decision?"
This question frames you as the right choice for the role and signals that you will continue to do what's necessary to confirm that decision.
Q: "What separates strong performers from average ones here?"
This question shows you're interested in what winning actually looks like, not just what the job description says.
Q: "How does this role change someone professionally if they fully lean into it?"
This question makes it clear you see the role as long term and as a real opportunity to launch your career.
It's worth saying again that common courtesy says you should only ask two to three of these questions at the end of the interview. But if the manager allows you to keep going, you could easily get away with two or three more. Some interviewers love to hear themselves talk so they may eat up all the time answering your first couple of questions. Great questions can stretch an interview past its set time so be prepared to dig if you're given the room.
One more thing: this is not the interview where you ask about work hours, vacation time, benefits or other logistics. Stay focused on showing this manager you want the job. Those details will come later and you'll want clarity on them before you sign the offer letter.
 
Going For The Close: Asking For The Job
There is one question you have to ask at the end of the interview. YOU MUST ASK FOR THE JOB!
In sales it's always about closing the deal and asking for the business. In a sales interview the job is the deal and asking for the job isn't frowned on, it's encouraged. It's a way you can reveal your killer instinct to close deals. Some managers won't hire people unless they go for the close at the end of the interview. Want to know the crazy part? While there is no actual statistic to reference there's a general consensus that less than 20% of sales candidates ask for the job. Some numbers suggest 1 out of 50 will try to close the interview by asking for the job. No one is ever going to rip the pen out of your hand and ask, "where do I sign." You have to learn to ask for the business and you MUST ask for the job!
As a hiring manager it's the most infuriating thing to have a great candidate do a fantastic interview and then not ask for the job. It brings your backbone into question. We are left wondering if you have the intestinal fortitude to actually do the job if you're not even willing to ask for it. It's the most incredible way to separate yourself from all of the other candidates that are interviewing for the position. It shows no fear! If you were in front of me right now I would grab you by the collar and shake you just so you understand how important this one question is. Please don't let me down. Write it on the top of your copy of the resume or place a note in your portfolio but do not forget to ask. If you do it, you'll see a smile flash on the interviewer's face looking at you like they just found their next rockstar. Ok, I've said it enough. Here are some great closing questions you can use at the end of your interview.
Closing Questions
Q: "What would make you hesitate to move me forward at this point?"
This sounds like a tough question but its point is to draw out any hang ups or objections the hiring manager would have with you. It gives you a chance to overcome those objections by putting them on the table and talking through them. If they give you an objection you can say, "I can understand why you would feel that way, and let me try to rephrase my answer if you'll allow me?" The hiring manager is looking for a salesperson and you're selling yourself right now better than 99% of any other candidate. It shows you're direct, composed and ready for the pressure.
Q: "What would you need to see from me to feel fully confident in this hire?"
This question is feedback friendly and shows you're open to coaching. It also shows an ownership of the effort you're willing to put into this role.
Q: "If I joined the team, what would earning credibility quickly look like?"
This is an assumptive close designed to shift the dynamic. It subconsciously gets the manager thinking about bringing you on rather than evaluating you. It also signals that you're already thinking about how to succeed, not just how to get hired.
Q: "Is there anything I should clarify or expand on before we wrap up?"
This is an isolating question to see if they will voice any remaining objections. You will most likely still want to follow it with a direct next steps question.
Your last question should always be:
Q: "Based on our conversation today, I'm very interested in this opportunity. Can you share with me the next steps in the process and when you'd expect to move forward with a decision?"
In sales you will hear the acronym BAFAM, which stands for Book A Meeting From A Meeting. You never want to leave a sales meeting without knowing the next steps. It's the same here. This question demonstrates you already know how to do that. It shows confidence without applying too much pressure.
My favorite closing question to ask, whether it's for a job or for the business, is: "I'm a salesperson so I have to ask for the business here. Can you see any reason we wouldn't move forward with the next steps?" It's a soft close while making it clear that everyone in that conversation knows why we are there. I'm a salesperson and I'm here for the job or the deal. No sense in beating around the bush and I don't succeed without leaving with my goal achieved. It lets the person interviewing me know that this is the part where we move forward. I'm trying to isolate objections and give myself a chance to continue the conversation towards winning the business. Soft wording but very upfront and intentional.
If you receive any feedback or concerns, do not under any circumstances get defensive. Ask clarifying questions and then look to overcome whatever objections they've given you. You can also agree with their assessment. "I can see how you would feel that way" and "that makes a lot of sense especially if I were sitting in your seat" will disarm them and open them up to you highlighting your willingness, tenacity, work ethic and coachability. Those kinds of statements let you reframe their objection and overcome it by sharing a little more about yourself and why you would still be a great fit. If their objection is, "I don't think you have enough experience," you can say, "You're right, I don't, but can I ask you a question? How good are you at developing people who will do everything they can to win in one of these roles?" Agreeing with objections is your best way to keep the conversation going, in interviews or in sales. People assume you will get defensive or make an argument. They aren't expecting you to agree with them. It's disarming and aligns you with them instead of against them. This rebuttal is a little strong but it will be respected and shows just how much you want the role.
Once you lock down this interview and get the hiring manager to move you to next steps, Chapter 8 covers exactly what those next conversations could look like and how to handle them.

































Chapter 8 

The Second Interview

I’m assuming for the purpose of this chapter that the person interviewing you in the first interview didn’t prepare you for what happens in the second interview with their boss. Some hiring managers aren’t allowed to let the candidate know what comes next; they just schedule the next interview. I know, it’s dumb but you have to keep in mind that every step of this interview is intended to weed you out. It’s all a test to see how you do, what you do, and how you react under pressure. Afterall, that’s what sales is all about. Nothing is predictable and you are constantly adjusting to feedback and more decision makers so it makes sense that they firewalk you through the same process by simulating the conditions in the interview. At large companies, the interview process is designed to see if you can thrive in a sales environment.

The best advice I can give you walking into this second interview is stay calm and let the circumstances come to you. If you don’t know what the format is going to be like, it's best not to make any assumptions. Since the interview could be very formal or casual; the only advice that works here is to be prepared. I will warn you that their goal is to get to know you and that may mean that the interview appears informal and casual but that could be very purposeful. They want you to relax so they can get to know the ‘real you’. A casual interview could be authentic or a trap. If you’re not sure, the best bet is to stay on guard the whole time. Remember, you’re in presentation mode so things like leaning back in your chair like it’s a lounge chair will only hurt you. You should also study Chapter 5 and Chapter 6 as you may get some similar questions in this interview. The hiring manager will look for consistency in your answers from the first interview.

There’s three common persona’s that will conduct your second interview. Since you don’t know what you’re going to get, it’s best if you walk in over prepared than hoping it’s one of the easier ones. When I was a new manager for an organization, they were all over me about making sure I had two or three interviews with the candidate and my higher ups. My managers didn’t know me well enough to trust me so they wanted to vet out the candidates themselves. After several good hires, the second interview with my managers got a whole lot easier. But that’s not always the case. In really good companies, they stay hard and disciplined about vetting candidates and make sure every person who gets hired is the right person. Let’s dig into the persona’s.

The Rubber Stamp:

A Rubber Stamps interview is going to feel very relaxed. Unfortunately this has nothing to do with you and everything to do with the trust this person has with the first person who interviewed you. The Rubber Stamp knows he can trust his manager and that you’d never make it to him unless you were going to get hired. This interview is going to feel very conversational and may even include some laughs. Stay on top of your game and keep your wits about you. 

If you walk into a Rubber Stamp interview you almost certainly have the job. Both managers are meeting with you again just to check off the boxes the company has laid out for them and they are required to do it as a part of their process. You are still very much being evaluated but you can mess it up. They will still evaluate your professionalism and your maturity. If you’re too relaxed, you can still set off some red flags and then the interview will get really serious. Speak when spoken to and politely smile when they are joking around. Stick to the right answers and let them enjoy the easy going conversation. Your job is still to confirm that you’re the right hire. There will be plenty of time to joke around with them once you’ve gotten the job.

The Auditor:

The Auditor will strictly adhere to the process. They check each and every box and ask every question they are supposed to ask. You may see your resume that the manager you first interviewed with took notes on. Or they may have a second page or corporate form where the first manager made their notes. They will work off of those notes to conduct this interview. They will ask the questions the company has laid out as well of some of their favorites.

With this persona, they are looking to verify the consistency of your stories and answers. When you’re asked about numbers, they have to be consistent with your answers from the first interview and what your resume says. Any sort of deviation from the first interview could disqualify you from the role. If you have a gap in your answers from your previous interview and this interview, you’re going to send up some serious red flags for them.

The Prosecutor:

Every Prosecutor is looking to disqualify you. They don’t like wasting their time on people who aren’t going to work out. And they assume most people aren’t going to work out. This interview is going to be even more difficult than the first one. They will apply pressure to see if you wobble at all. Stay on your toes and most importantly be honest and authentic. Prosecutors will pull the truth out of you. Any opening that you give them they will dig like crazy. You also have to take into consideration that the more they like you the harder the interview will get. Prosecutors are students of the game, they like people and they want to hire rockstars. Take pride if it’s difficult for you, you may just end up friends with them.

For the sake of this book, and your preparedness for the interview it’s best to assume that you are going to run into The Prosecutor. Someone who’s looking to disqualify you. The Prosecutor wants excellence and they are pretty sure regardless of who you are they are pretty sure you’re not the answer. This manager is tough but he’s doing that to test your competitiveness. They believe that the right candidate will see this interview as a challenge and one they have to win. Now that you have an idea of what types of personas are in this interview let’s dig into what it’s going to look like.

In all honesty, this was me. I never interviewed someone trying to hire them. I was always looking for a reason not to hire them. I know it sounds harsh but it was me that I didn’t trust, not the candidate. I tend to get along with people and like having fun so I had to adopt this persona because if I didn’t I’d end up liking the candidate and want to hire them based off of that. I once interviewed a candidate and kept digging into them so much that the other manager apologized to them because I was tough on them. That candidate has now been in his role for 5 years and we are very good friends. The truth is, I liked the candidate. He was a great person. The kind of person I would like eating dinner with. But that meant I had to check myself and make sure I wasn’t just hiring them because I liked them. 

The first interview is all about you trying to prove that you can do the job. In this second interview, the manager is deciding if they can trust you with the job. This tier of manager isn’t impressed by your enthusiasm. They don’t care about any of the fluff. The first interview was designed to screen you from the position, this interview is about validating if you are a great fit for the role. 

This second interviewer is looking for strong indications of your maturity, the wisdom of your judgement, the consistency of your answers from the first interview and whether or not you will tell the truth under pressure. They are also looking to protect their culture and trying to determine if you are a fit for that as well. It could be that the first manager who interviewed you is new and their boss is checking on their ability to judge talent. They could also be in a position that they know they are going to have turnover on the team. If that’s the case, they may be looking to see if they can build the new team around you. If they aren’t looking to maintain culture, they may be looking to create a new culture. There are a lot of scenarios most reps don’t think about.

Think about it from their perspective. They aren’t hiring a rep today, they’re hiring a future headline for their own career. As much as you care about getting this job, they are desperately trying to excel at theirs. They may also be in a position where they are trying to keep their job. The truth is you don’t know and you probably won’t. Some teams are winners looking for their next rockstar. Others may be lousy and trying to retool. Don’t worry about the quality of the team and where it is now, you have to have the mindset of what you do will determine your results. Right now your goal is to get hired, you can figure out the dynamics of the opportunity once you get hired. You need to know that this interview isn’t always about you, it’s about them managing up to their bosses.

As a manager, if they make a ‘great hire’ they earn trust and credibility with their colleagues and bosses. If they make a ‘bad hire’ then it becomes a story they have to explain later to their higher ups. People don’t like risks especially when it comes to their own career. Those layers that you’re going through in the interview process are often designed to mitigate risk both for the company and for the managers. If everyone agrees to a hire, then it’s everyone's fault if or when that rep fails. In sales, you will learn that if you’re only talking to one person in a business you really aren’t talking to anybody. That’s a deal that isn’t going to go anywhere. Even if you’re talking to a CEO of a company they likely won’t make a decision without the CFO and others buying into the cost and change to their business. In these interviews, your goal is to get everyone to buy into you as the right fit for the role.  

These interviews feel different because the stakes are higher for the people interviewing you. This manager isn’t concerned about who the person sitting in front of them is. They are trying to figure out who you are going to be when the training ends. They want to know who you will be when the honeymoon phase is over. A hundred other people have sat in the chair you’ll be sitting in and promised the moon about work ethic, drive and determination. And guess what? Those people aren’t there anymore. Some of them didn’t even show up on the first day. Some of them never passed the drug test. Others quit half way through training because they got another job. So forgive them for being a little skeptical and not caring about all the fluff. They want to know beyond a shadow of a doubt that when you miss quota and the pressure mounts you go all out to fix it. They want to know you’re not going to run away the first time you hit a losing streak. 

This manager doesn’t want to hear that you’re ‘good with people’ and you like talking so you think you’ll be good at sales. They know how hard B2B sales really is and they want you to understand that too. They want you to know that it takes a lot of work to be successful. That catalogs don’t sell products. They want you to do what they tell you to do. They are looking for those foundational elements of a sales rep because they know that’s what’s going to make you successful in that role.  

These managers typically apply their interview lens through six different filters.

1. Business literacy
	
Do you understand how the company actually makes money? This is a question that I personally used to ask candidates. I would ask, “Any idea how much money a place like this makes in a week?” I was testing their understanding of the business and also showing them that they shouldn’t judge a company's income, they should ask about it. 90% of the time, they were off with their number. But 100% of the time they didn’t ask any questions before they guessed.

2. Judgement under ambiguous circumstances

In the question above, I wasn’t looking for accuracy, I was looking for more questions. “How many customers do you have?” “How much is the average invoice?” Really anything that would demonstrate some sort of business acumen. Just because they ask you a question doesn’t mean you can’t ask clarifying ones to arrive at an answer. Keep your cool, breathe, think and work towards a solution.

3. Coachability without fragility

I was interviewing an incredible salesperson one time on Zoom and I asked her, “what do you think will make you successful here?” And she responded, “Isn’t that your job to tell me?” You may think that’s a rude answer but that was THE best answer she could have given me. The CEO was in the interview as well and I got a text immediately after she said that and it said, “She’s the one!” Most hiring managers are looking for moxy. If you don’t have the intestinal fortitude in the interview chances are you won’t get it. She took her shot and crushed it. She demonstrated her grit and that’s exactly how she would act with customers. She was hired.

4. Pattern recognition

These managers want to see if you have the ability to recognize patterns. Are you capable of seeing what works and what doesn’t? Be careful, this doesn’t mean you interrupt them because you think you know where the question or conversation is going. Remember, sales reps listen more than they talk. Pattern recognition is about learning not assuming. 

5. Cultural safety

Drama is a culture killer. Don’t be dramatic. They do not want someone on their team that they have to manage their drama. Be buttoned up. Early indications of drama in an interview process are, being late and being late with weak excuses. Overly apologizing and overreacting. Showing up frazzled with excuses. No organization with your portfolio as you look through a pile of 40 pages to find the resume you wrote. Complaining will also throw up some red flags. They want you to be drama free, humble and reliable. 

6. Trust with bad news
	
They want you to trust them and for them to be able to trust you. Their expectation is for you to trust them and they have earned that right with their title and tenure in their position. However it’s your job to earn their trust. Will you hide problems or surface them early? I loved reps who were upfront with any problems. Know why? Because I had the ability to fix them. Your manager wants to know what’s going on. Be honest and be honest fast. 

Regardless of what’s in front of them, every hiring manager has a mental checklist or silent scorecard that you need to be aware of. These are the real questions you have to answer in order to get hired. The problem for you is you can only answer them by how you answer all of the other questions they are going to ask.

First and foremost is your first impression. You should look sharp in this interview. Being overdressed will never hurt you but being underdressed will. The shirt or blouse should be ironed or dry cleaned. In B2B it’s all about impressions. If you’re coming right out of college you should know that you look like your twelve to a manager who’s over forty-five. Sorry but when we have kids your age or older it’s tough for us to see you differently. Most of us get over it and the only way for you to do it is to be incredibly professional. You need to be 23 going on 30 in regards to maturity and how you carry yourself.

Developing rapport isn’t what you think it is. This is a controversial take in sales but you need to know there is a massive difference between ‘rapport’ and ‘strategic rapport’. Most people in sales believe they connect with people by finding and talking about things in common. They talk about a picture on the wall or see that the decision maker likes something because of an item on their desk and they talk about it. That’s fluff, don’t do it. 

I learned that lesson the hard way early in my career. I was a new sales rep and I had just walked into a customer's office for a meeting to discuss some things regarding his account. Keep in mind this was in the south so at any time you could walk into an office and see some sort of wildlife mounted on the wall. In this case the customer had a large deer right over the chair I was supposed to sit in. I was looking for something in common with him just to get the conversation started and so I said, “Wow, that’s a great deer.” As I stood there looking at it before I sat down. To which he said, “thanks, it was the last deer my son shot before he passed away.” I was floored. There’s no coming back from that. I just sat down and apologized for his loss. What a terrible way to start a meeting. Trust me, I have more of those foot in mouth stories than I would like to admit.

Strategic rapport is about the company not what’s hanging on the wall or sitting on their desk. Asking questions about the business and what it’s important to them is the space you want to operate in when you’re in those meetings. Questions like, “Why do their customers choose them?” and other business related questions are what make you an advisor and not their friend. Friendships are made through the sale, not by talking about what you think they may have in common with you.

After the first impression question, one of the biggest questions that second tier manager will have in their silent scorecard is, “Would I put this person in front of my biggest customer or prospect?” A good first impression, with a smile and a strong handshake go a long way into answering that question. Speaking clearly and professionally reinforces the first impression and ultimately being able to answer questions and asking intelligent ones makes them feel safe in deciding on you.

Closely behind that first question for them is a very close second question and that is, “Would I bet my reputation on them?” Most salesreps don’t understand that the first step of trust in a relationship between a salesperson and their boss is actually done by the manager. That manager is putting their trust in the salesperson to deliver because their own reputation and career trajectory may just depend on it. I would ask my sales managers all the time after an interview that hadn’t quite sold me. “Are you willing to bet your job on that person?” Usually the answer was no. You need to understand that they are betting their personal career on you to win, their success may just depend on it.

That silent scorecard's third question is, “Would I want 10 of them?” That manager is looking for a tone setter. Someone who’s high energy, competitive, eager to learn and has the potential to work well with the candidates' direct superior. When you leave that interview you want them thinking that they would love to have ten of you. Telling stories based around your work ethic and drive will go a long way to answering that question.

The last question you have to answer on that silent scorecard is one of the most difficult questions to answer. You have to answer the question, “will they make my manager better or are they going to be difficult to manage?” Stories of teamwork and supporting superiors as well as being humble will go a long way with proving you’re the right choice.

You don’t need sales experience in an entry level sales position but you do have to learn to craft the experiences you do have into stories that fit the underlying foundational elements required to succeed in sales. Relating those attributes into stories to show you’re experienced without pretending will help this manager see you’re realistic about where you stand and what you have to learn. There’s nothing worse than hearing someone with no experience in sales tell you how it’s done and how successful they are going to be. Demonstrating you have something to learn and eager to do it will go a lot further than someone who can’t be honest with themself.

A simple structure for you to follow in this interview is:

	Situation ? Choice ? Result ? Lesson ? How it applies to this role

Now we can look at what questions they may ask, why they ask them, what they are listening for and a sample answer you can use.

Business Acumen and “Do you get how this works?”

Q: “What do you think drives success in this territory: activity, skill, timing or something else?”

This manager is looking to see how you reason, not what you know. They are listening for balanced thinking and to see if you’re naive to actual sales.

A: “I think activity is the baseline, without that, nothing happens. Skill improves conversations and timing definitely matters but activity gives you chances to learn faster. My plan would be to control activity early, get coached on skill and use feedback to improve conversion.”

Managers love salespeople whose default is action! Saying that your plan is to increase activity as the best way to shorten your runway to success is almost never said in an interview. Most sales reps will say that their first step is to master the product or service and learning its features and benefits is what’s going to help them succeed. It’s not. When you talk about learning all the features and benefits all that manager is hearing is that it’s going to take you longer to “feel ready” before you try to sell it. Default to action!

Q: “If you were me, what would you be worried about with a new hire in this role?”

This is a check in to see how you think. It's a test of your maturity and empathy for leadership risk. This question is asking you, can you sell me on you?

A: “I’d worry about someone saying they want to do sales and then realize they don’t like the rejection or daily structure. I’d also worry about someone who doesn’t ask for help either. I’m comfortable being coached and I’d rather raise my hand than struggle silently.”

This answer is a reassurance as to who you are regarding your grit and signals that you have a strong understanding that sales isn’t easy. It shows you’re willing to put the work in and do everything you can to win, even when times are tough. 

Q: “What’s the difference between being busy and being effective in sales?”

Senior leaders care about productivity and focus. They want you to understand that administrative work is not prospecting for new business. There’s a massive difference in their eyes between doing work and doing the work that matters.

A: “Busy is doing a lot of things. Effective is doing the right things consistently and getting better at them. From what I understand it comes down to measuring effectiveness by activity quality, conversion rates and whether I’m improving week to week.”

There was a large picture of a quote from Stephen Covey that hung on the wall just outside of my office and it read, “Keep the Main Thing, The Main Thing.” It comes from his book, The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People. It was a reminder to me that when I walked back into my office, my priorities mattered. There are so many things in sales that pull you away from doing what’s necessary and the people conducting these second interviews understand that. They want to know that you have the discipline and discernment to know the difference between busy and effective.

Judgement Under Ambiguity:

Q: “Tell me about a time you had to make a decision without clarity.”

Anyone who’s spent anytime in sales knows that there’s almost more gray area than an actual roadmap for what to do. They are asking this question to see if you understand that concept and to try and get an idea if you have the wherewithal to figure out what to do in those moments.

A: “I was working a summer job and let’s just say the onboarding process wasn’t fully developed. When it started to get busy, I just asked my manager a few clarifying questions and then made the best call I could. Later I followed up with my supervisor to make sure I did the right thing. I learned that it’s better to decide, communicate quickly and adjust than wait and create more chaos.”

This answer or something similar with your experiences tells the manager that you don’t fold under pressure. It shows that you keep your composure and step up rather than melt when the going gets tough. Free thinking and a resolve to accomplish the task are amazing ingredients to successful salespeople. 

Q: “When you’re unsure, do you act first or ask first?”

This is usually a follow up from the question above. They want you to step up but they also want to know you understand when it’s the time to ask for forgiveness or permission. Managers are ok with reps testing the fences trying to win but they aren’t a fan of a salesperson breaking through the fence and going where they want.

A: “If it’s low risk, I’ll try and learn by doing. If it affects customers or the team, I ask first. Either way I want to move quickly. My goal is to make progress without creating avoidable mistakes.”

Making it clear that your default is action rather than being paralyzed is a message they will hear loud and clear. That’s exactly what they are looking for. They want judgement not ego and they want you asking questions as long as they are action oriented and geared towards learning their system.

Coachability Without Fragility:

Q: “What’s the best way to correct you when you’re wrong?”

Managers are always trying to figure out who you really are and not just who you say you are. Be honest with them and do it authentically. With little to no experience there’s no reason for you to have an ego and learning from your managers is the fastest way to shorten your runway to success.

A: “Directly and quickly. I’d rather get corrected in the moment than repeat a mistake. If I understand the “why” behind it, I can apply it faster.”

This is the, “I don’t have an ego, I just want to win answer.” They know you are going to make mistakes, they want to know how you’re going to react to them when they point them out. I would ask this question in interviews just in case they weren’t great at receiving feedback so I could refer back to the interview and say, “You told me you were open to feedback, is that not true?” Be coachable, no one on that management team wants you to lose.

Q: “Tell me about a time when you got feedback that stung.”

This question is straight to the point for a reason. They want a real answer here. Don’t be afraid to be vulnerable with your answer. They are testing your emotional control and your ego. 

A: “I was told my communication was too detailed and people lost the point. It bothered me because I was trying to be thorough. I had to really think about it and absorb it. I learned to lead with the headline first, then details. When I figured it out, it made me so much more effective.”

Showing a hiring manager that you're receptive and willing to reflect on your own behavior is a massive sign of maturity. Winners take coaching, reflect on it, and make changes. 

Trust, Integrity and Bad News:

Q: “Tell me about a time you had to admit you dropped the ball.”

Senior leaders rarely care about things going right. They put out fires, fix things, and get people back on track. That’s how they view things. They want to know you’re going to be honest with them when something's not right and they want to know that they are going to hear about it from you and not find out about it after you tried to bury it.

A: “I missed a milestone on a project and told the team immediately. I owned it, proposed a fix and asked what else I could do to recover. I learned that bad news doesn’t get better with time.” 

Telling them you’ve already learned the hard lesson that hiding your problems doesn’t pay is reassuring to any manager. They don’t want you to be a problem and people who hide things from them are a problem. Putting the team in front of a mistake makes it clear that you’re humble enough to admit your mistakes.

Q: “If you miss quota early on, what’s your first move?”

This is an ownership question! So own it. They understand people fail, they understand that you might fail. What they want to know is will you own the failure or give them excuses.

A: “I think in this role it’s all about activity. I’d audit that first. Am I doing enough of the right actions? Then I’d ask for coaching on where I’m losing. Is it opening, discovery, handling pushback? I’d want to diagnose it fast and then work like hell to turn it around.”

Managers hate candidates who blame everyone else. But when you own your failures and leave your success in the hands of their people to coach you it’s hard to lose. I’ve said it a thousand times in interviews; if you fail doing what I tell you to do, I can defend you. If you fail doing it your way there’s no hope. Let them know you’re on board to do things their way and seek help fast.

Culture and Team Safety:

Q: “What kind of teammate are you when you’re losing?”

	What they are saying is, “I’ve seen a lot of reps get toxic, is that who you are?”

A: “I don’t withdraw or blame, I know that doesn’t help. I tend to get quieter, more focused and ask for feedback. I don’t want to drag the team down. I want to learn from people doing what’s working.”

Being coachable and willing to learn is what will buy you more time when things aren’t going your way. Toxic reps get little help and they get cut fast. No one likes a cancer in their culture and this is your chance to tell them, that isn’t who you are.

Q: “What behaviors do you think kill a sales culture?”

When sales reps start to fail they do one of two things. They either make the corrections and fix it or they start sabotaging the team, whether that’s consciously or subconsciously. If the whole team thinks it’s impossible to win, then it justifies the failure but if one or two reps are doing well and the rest of the team isn’t performing, then it’s clear who should be kept and who should be cut. So when reps start to fail they tend to drag everyone else's performance down. They are trying to figure out if you’re a fix it person or a future problem.

A: “Excuses, negativity, hiding low activity and gossip are what brings any team down and I have to imagine that’s the same here. The second people stop telling the truth, everyone’s performance collapses.”

The same things kill a culture regardless of what profession you’re in. People tend to get negative and start blaming everything and everyone because in their mind it can’t be them. Managers are looking for people who own solutions instead of people who can’t own their failures. Blaming poor performance on poor territory, lack of support or any other excuse only justifies things to yourself. Your boss is looking for you to fix it and change narratives.

Stamina, Commitment and Realism:

Q: “What do you think will be the hardest about this job six months in?”

Hiring managers want you to be thinking down the road, not just excited to get company swag on your first day. They want you to be realistic about the struggle, not unrealistic about an opportunity.

A: “I think consistency even when the work feels repetitive and results lag. That’s why routines matter to me. It helps me avoid relying on motivation and stay focused on my goals.”

Alluding to an understanding that sales isn’t easy and making it clear that your process focused will make any manager think they have the right candidate. They don’t want someone on their team who is oblivious or too optimistic for the opportunity to understand that sales is hard. They want someone who gets it. Demonstrating that you understand tells them that’s one less lesson they are going to have to pay you to learn.

Q: “What would make you quit?”

These hiring managers aren’t known for throwing softballs and they like answers that hit it out of the park. Pushing you in this interview is a test. 
       
A: “Lack of integrity or a situation where expectations aren’t honest. Hard work isn’t a reason to quit, that’s what I’m signing up for.”

Highlighting your ethics and pointing to your standards is a strong stance but they will like that. Remember, you’re here to hit home runs. Reassuring them that you know exactly what you’re signing up for is a bonus on the end of this answer. You told them you’re willing to put your ethics first and have no problem following that up with your work ethic.	

Sales Instinct Without Sales Experience: 

Q: “How do you earn trust fast with someone who doesn’t know you?”

This is a litmus test to see if you ‘think like a sales rep’ without requiring any experience to be on your resume. They are checking to see if you understand the importance of developing strategic rapport fast and what it can mean to connect with people because that’s what the job is.

A: “I’m consistent, prepared and I do what I say I’ll do. I ask good questions and I don’t pretend to know what I don’t know. Honesty goes a long way in winning anyone over.”

	Understanding that there is work that goes before a meeting even takes place is a good place to start. Managers love people who are prepared. Referencing back to your ethics being an actual part of your approach to sales is a strong indicator that they can trust you. Being willing to admit to not knowing what you don’t know is refreshing and makes you appear comfortable in your own skin. 

Q: “What do you do when someone is skeptical of you?”

	In almost all sales interactions your prospect won’t trust you. You’re starting at a negative and it’s important that you win them over. Think about it, how many salespeople would you say that you trust? You’re always wondering about their angle or what they are trying to get out of you. Well your prospect will think that way about you. This is a ‘what’s your plan’ question.

A: “I acknowledge it, stay calm and ask what they’ve seen that makes them feel that way. I try to see it from their perspective and understand it before I try to persuade them.”

	Being comfortable with being uncomfortable is not a trait that many salespeople have. They usually want to chatter during the quiet parts and that is known to kill deals. Being someone who already understands that it’s better first to seek to understand than to be understood is showing this manager that you have a strong foundation regardless of your experience level.

In Chapter 6 you saw a lot of closing questions that you could also use for this interview. If you’re given the time, you will want to have questions in this interview as well. You should ask at least three questions so you can show that you’ve thought through the job. If there was one particular question you asked in the first interview that seemed to really resonate with the first manager you should probably ask it again to this manager. There’s a chance that what you asked resonated and that’s because it’s a core value of theirs. Just reframe it so the first manager knows you’re asking the same question on purpose. You should say something to the effect of, “I asked this question in my first interview and I really liked his/her answer and I’d like to get your take on it.” From there, you can ask the question. There is however one question you should ask this manager that will help them understand your desire, tenacity and ability to think like an executive. 

Q: “If I’m offered this role, what would you want me to prove in the first 60 days that would make you feel like you made the right decision hiring me?”

This isn’t a ‘do you have concerns’ question. Asking someone in that position, especially a Prosecutor, a ‘do you have concerns’ question could put you in a bad predicament. They may actually tell you everything they don’t like about you and it would put you in a bad spot. You don’t want to risk ending the interview on a negative note. You want them thinking about how they are making the right decision because you think like a winner. Answering with something like the sample question helps you frame the question like an executive minded salesperson. It’s risk reduction, measurable and puts it progress on a timeline. 

Much like sales, you never really know what you’re walking into until you walk into it. The secret to succeeding in sales and interviews is to do the best you can to prepare for everything. As the interview starts your objective is to try and diagnose which persona is really interviewing you. The secret is knowing what they are looking for. The last thing you want to do is practice interviewing by interviewing. You will need to practice with someone before you actually get into the interview. If you want to really simulate the interviewing process, practice with a friend to simulate the first interview and then practice with someone who has some authority over you. Whether that’s a professor, someone in career services, a manager at your work but the goal is for you to put yourself in an uncomfortable situation. That’s the best way to simulate how you will feel in a real interview. 

Becoming a pro at interviewing will benefit you for the rest of your life. Great salespeople aren’t just great at sales, they are great at merchandising what they have accomplished and then nailing an interview for the next opportunity. The practice you put into your interviewing will gain you the confidence you need to progress your career.

Chapter 9
The Panel Interview
Panel interviews are all about People Company Fit. They want to know that you really want to do the job you're applying for, that you understand what that role is going to require of you and that you're a fit for the team. The panel interview is designed to figure all of that out. Companies conduct panel interviews to be efficient with their time, reduce the possibility of bias, and assess a candidate's ability to handle pressure and communicate with different stakeholders.
Keep in mind, a panel interview isn't meant to overwhelm you but from your perspective it will feel that way. This interview will have three to five participants from their side and the questions will be rapid fire while moving through the stakeholders in progression. Most of the time these questions are already spelled out for each of them so they don't step on each other and they are most likely oriented toward the department they represent.
Sales leaders know that resumes don't predict success. Resumes get you the interview. You are responsible for closing the deal. Body language is incredibly important in this interview. While you're answering the questions of one panel member, the rest are watching to see how you respond. It's stressful because you become hyper aware of how you're sitting and what you're saying. Relax, breathe and be the best polished version of yourself. This is exactly why practice matters so much. You don't have the opportunity to fold in this interview.
In a setting like this, these panel participants aren't necessarily looking for a polished salesperson. They are looking for the real you. They are trying to see the signal through the noise. This is a test of consistency, coachability and composure in real time. Authenticity is going to win the day so be yourself, put your shoulders back, put a smile on your face, walk into the room and shake everyone's hand. That's the first impression you want to make. Now let's talk about who might be in the room and why.
Common panel personas:
The Sales Leader (Closer / Operator)
The Day-to-Day Manager (Developer)
The Culture and Values Guardian (Builder / HR / GM)
The Cross-Functional Partner (Operations / Service / Finance)
The Wild Card (This could be an Executive or Tenured Rep)
 
Let's break down each one.
 
 
 
The Sales Leader
 
The Sales Leader is going to be the VP of Sales, Director level or Regional Sales Manager. This person is most likely a final approval on hiring the salesperson. They have the ability to make the final call but will usually defer to the sales manager and hiring manager unless they see something they don't like. Think of them as a yellow light in a stoplight. They are usually cautious but tend to defer to their managers while airing any concerns or areas where the candidate will need development or coaching. They come from a point of caution and they are mainly looking for any inconsistencies in your story that don't line up with your resume.
They have most likely been prepped by the hiring manager as to their thoughts from the first interview and they may have uncovered some information they want to dig into. These people like people who know their numbers. If you have a number on your resume, they are going to want to know how you did it and what you did to accomplish your goal. Most people at this level are looking to disqualify candidates, not qualify them. The questions from this person on the panel will feel a little less conversational with very pointed questions.
The question they are asking themselves throughout the entire interview is:
"Can I put a quota on this person and sleep at night?"
When they ask questions they will be looking for personality traits they typically see in candidates who perform well. The Sales Leader is hiring you to go get revenue. Until you prove you can do that, they don't want to talk about what comes next. Career trajectory for this person is based on your ability to prove yourself in the role you are being paid to do.
The Sales Leader is looking for foundational competencies. They are trying to figure out how hungry and competitive you are. They tend to like people who are driven internally and don't need an outside push for motivation. Ownership is a big deal for the Sales Leader, and that doesn't mean owning your future effort. It means being authentic about your past failures. A Sales Leader has seen failure more often than they have seen success so they are ok with it. What they aren't ok with is when you don't own it. Coachability is a must. They know you don't know what you're doing yet and they are ok with that. What they aren't ok with is someone who portrays themselves as a know-it-all.
Questions a Sales Leader may ask:
Q: "Why sales?"
What they are really asking: Are you running toward sales or away from something else?
A: "I like being measured. I want a role where effort matters, feedback is constant, and results are visible. Sales feels like a place where I can control my outcomes if I'm willing to put the work in."
You're telling this panel that you're driven by the rewards of your effort. It says you don't fit well in a role where you can't earn more and affect change. It says you're coachable, welcome input and need to perform.
Q: "How do you handle rejection?"
What they are really asking: Will you emotionally survive cold outreach?
A: "I've learned not to personalize no. I look at it as information, not failure. If I'm doing the right activities consistently, the results usually follow."
If you don't have any sales experience, share a story with how you came to a conclusion like this. Even a simple story of selling something door to door when you were a kid will translate well. It shows the Sales Leader and the rest of the panel that you know who you are and you're able to self-reflect on how you arrived at your purpose.
Q: "What motivates you?"
What they are really asking: Where does your drive come from?
A: "Progress. I like improving at something measurable. Money matters, but growth and mastery are what keep me engaged."
Say money. If you don't, a lot of Sales Leaders won't want you on their team. People shy away from it and talk about the company culture or the brand instead. The problem with that answer is all the Sales Leader hears is that you're only out for yourself. You want the training or the name on your resume. The example answer makes it clear your motivation is still money while showing you want to learn and grow. Sales Leaders like reps who are money motivated. Own it.
The Day-to-Day Manager
The Day-to-Day Manager is the sales manager or front line leader who most likely conducted the first interview with you. Something you should be aware of is that this manager needs you to do a good job in this interview. They took the time to interview you in the first place, which means they saw something in you worth bringing back. They also took the time to schedule everyone else's time around this interview. The bottom line is this: they are vouching for you. Their reputation for bringing in good talent is on the line and they need you to show the rest of the room why they believed in you.
They might use their questions to steer you back to answers you gave in the first interview because they saw something there that everyone else on the panel would appreciate. As if to say, "see, that's why I liked them." They might also ask questions that their boss surfaced after reviewing your resume. Either way, know that this manager is in your corner and they want you to win the room.
 
Questions a Day to Day Manager may ask:
 
Q: "Tell me about a time you were coached and received feedback."
This is often a "tell them what you told me" question. They liked your answer from the first interview, and they want the panel to hear it.
A: "Whether it was class projects or my internship, I learned quickly that feedback helps me get to where I want to go as fast as possible. I may not always get it right the first time, but I apply feedback right away and adjust in order to get things done."
People find change hard because in order to change you have to be humble. That's what you're saying here. You're not so overconfident that you can't admit your mistakes and you're willing to pivot because you're results oriented.
Q: "How do you organize your day?"
The Day-to-Day Manager is held accountable for your schedule and their own. They want to know and they want everyone else to hear that you know how to prioritize.
A: "I prioritize what is urgent first. If urgency isn't an issue, then I like to tackle the hardest tasks early. I like having a plan before the day starts, even if I adjust things as they come up."
Being aware that the real accomplishment of the day is having a plan of attack will set you apart from almost all other candidates.
Q: "What would you do if you were struggling in your first 60 days?"
No one is more on the spot for this answer than your Day-to-Day Manager. People struggle in sales and everyone in that room knows it. What they are asking is whether you will reach out when you do. Will you allow them to help you?
A: "I'd increase activity, ask for help early, and get specific feedback on what to adjust. I do not believe in struggling quietly."
"I do not believe in struggling quietly" is a big statement for the people in that room to hear. It says when things get tough you start looking for help early. Most struggling reps retreat, get quiet, panic and start making excuses. This answer tells them you seek help instead. It says you have a default to action.
The Culture and Values Guardian
The Culture and Values Guardian can have different roles in the organization but usually they come from human resources. At one company I worked for, this person was my office administrator. She was our local HR person at the location, and she could smell a bad sales candidate a mile away. She was an adamant defender of our culture and while she protected it fiercely, she also welcomed the right people into it. She was awesome and I trusted her opinion explicitly because she just had a nose for the right people.
At that same company, in different roles, the Culture and Values Guardian was different people. At some locations it was the General Manager, but they all had something in common. They were Builders at heart. They loved their teams, their work and they wanted to work with people who worked as hard as they did. The Culture and Values Guardian is usually the warmest heart in the room. They love their people and they stand guard over them.
They tend to be humble about the importance of their role. They like to see that humility in candidates. That's not to say they don't appreciate confidence. They have a ton of it themselves. But they know real confidence carries itself. It doesn't have to tell anyone about it. The Culture and Values Guardian knows almost everything about everyone in the building. If they are indifferent to you as a candidate, that may be enough for you not to get the job. Treat this person with the utmost respect. Everyone else in the room will.
The Culture and Values Guardian values emotional intelligence and self-awareness. They want to know who you are as much as what you've done. You might be able to spot this person when something happens that's not according to plan. If someone forgets a pen, people will look to them first for an answer. They lead with a servant's heart.
What they are asking in each of their questions really is: "Will this person elevate or drain the team?"
 
Questions the Culture and Values Guardian may ask:
 
Q: "Describe a time you were part of a team."
To them, this question is all about how you fit. They are looking for core value alignment.
A: "I learned that showing up prepared and supporting others matters. Even when roles are different, effort and attitude affect everyone."
Let me say this clearly: you are the only one who will own your quota. Being a great teammate is fantastic, but it doesn't keep you around if you're missing quota. Hear that early and come to terms with it. However, it pays incredible dividends in your job and your career when you're a good salesperson and a great teammate. Answering the question this way shows respect for the people around you and lets them know you show up for others.
Q: "What does accountability mean to you?"
This is an ownership question. They are looking for your willingness to own your success and most importantly your failures. They want to know if you'll blame others and become a problem for the culture.
A: "To me, accountability means owning your results, good or bad, and being willing to adjust without excuses."
Leaders understand that credit is shared and blame is best left at their feet. An answer like this shows everyone in the room that you're willing to own your results and pivot when feedback says change is necessary.
Q: "What kind of manager do you work best for?"
In a panel interview there are a lot of different personalities in the room and candidates tend to struggle trying to please all of them with this answer.
A: "Someone who is direct, consistent and invested in the development of their people. I respond well to clear expectations and coaching."
Answering this way positions you as someone who appreciates honesty, consistency and feedback meant to help you improve. It's a way to make everyone in the room comfortable with your desire to be coached and your humility in understanding the role you will play.
 
The Cross Functional Partner
 
The Cross Functional Partner is typically someone who represents the operations or service departments of the business. This person could be as high up as a VP of Operations or might be a more local operations person like a Service Manager. If it's a higher-up, they will be looking for humility and a sense of teamwork that fits well with the service side. If it's a Service Manager there are a couple of things you need to understand about how important they are to your success and what they are looking for in a great sales representative.
This Service Manager is what I call professional blue collar. They have probably come up through the service or operations departments, and they are in that room because they have earned the right to be there. They've worked hard to learn the system, get promoted and they don't hand out respect. If you want their respect you will have to earn it. They are also used to clashing with the sales department because salespeople are the ones who put them in a position of having to apologize to a customer when sales over-promises something.
The Service Manager, if they have some tenure, has seen a lot of sales reps come and go. Most of those candidates sat where you will be sitting and most of them thought they were going to kill it. What these people rarely hear from a salesperson is humility, a willingness to work hard, and a desire to learn. These Service Managers have an amazing amount of legacy knowledge about what works and what doesn't. They don't want someone who is ignorant to the business to tell them it's easy. They want you to respect that it's not. Your goal shouldn't be to oversell yourself to them either. They've seen that before and it gets you nowhere. What they want is for you to put your head down, sell things the right way and communicate well with them.
What they are asking in each of their questions really is: "Will this person overpromise and create more headaches for me?"
Questions the Cross Functional Partner may ask:
Q: "How would you handle a situation where a customer is upset after the sale?"
This question is about whether you are going to dump issues at their feet or own any problems you may have caused.
A: "Ultimately it always comes down to ownership. It's up to me to take responsibility for the relationship, communicate clearly, and work to resolve the issue. I don't want to set anyone else up to fail because of something I caused."
This answer is all about what the Service Manager is looking for: ownership and humility. Knowing you're willing to own problems you create will go a long way with them.
Q: "What do you think the sales role is beyond closing deals?"
They want to know if you understand their role. Do you see them? Do you value them? Are you aware that it's not all about you?
A: "Sales is the first priority but it's also very important that within that sales process things are communicated clearly, and the right questions are asked both of the prospect and here internally. Can we deliver on what they want and are expectations clear."
The first part of this answer is for the Day-to-Day Manager and the Sales Leader. You're letting them know sales is still what matters. The second part is for the Service Manager. It says you will include them in the process and make sure you don't overcommit to something you can't deliver. It tells them you'll vet out expectations and communicate them to all the stakeholders.
 
The Wild Card
 
The Wild Card could be anyone else that local leadership allows to sit in on the interview. This person could be an executive visiting the location who wants to participate, a tenured sales rep being groomed for management, or a vendor or partner who helps the organization with sales and is brought in as a guardian of the hiring process.
 
The Wild Card Executive
 
The Wild Card Executive is mostly there to evaluate the interview process and make sure it's being done the way the company wants it done. They will rarely ask a question unless one that should be asked isn't. There is a chance they may have a couple of questions they like to ask in an interview setting, especially if they believe you have potential beyond the role. They are looking for a strong sense of judgment, self-awareness and possible long-term upside.
When they ask a question it will seem relatively simple, but their questions are usually psychologically layered. They are designed to test authenticity, whether you default to rehearsed answers and your level of emotional intelligence. These questions will be a trap for candidates who only repeat resume bullet points, overshare something inappropriate or freeze because the question wasn't scripted.
 
Questions a Wild Card Executive may ask:
 
Q: "Tell me something about yourself that isn't on your resume."
Most people are already nervous walking into a panel interview and seeing someone at this level has a way of making that worse. But the Executive, out of everyone in that room, is the one most looking for you to be authentic. So just be yourself.
A: "One thing that isn't on my resume is that I've always been someone who studies people. I pay attention to tone, body language and how conversations shift. I've realized I enjoy understanding what motivates someone. That's part of why I'm drawn to sales. It feels like applied psychology."
An answer like this assures them that you know exactly who you are. It shows reflection, it connects back to sales and it's mature without being overconfident. Executives love candidates who are students of the game. Executives love answers that reveal how you think, not just what you've done.
Q: "If we hire you, what do you think will be your biggest blind spot?"
This is a test of your humility, coachability and ability to self-diagnose. Weak candidates say things like "I'm a perfectionist" or "I work too hard." Those answers signal low self-awareness.
A: "I don't have direct sales experience yet, so my biggest blind spot will probably be knowing when to push and when to pause in a conversation. I expect I'll need coaching early on to calibrate that. But I'm comfortable adjusting quickly once I understand expectations."
There's so much confidence and authenticity in being able to clearly articulate where you stand. Executives know your blind spots. People in their roles don't usually ask questions they don't already know the answers to. They are testing to see if you know where you stand. When a Wild Card Executive speaks up, the room shifts. They aren't asking "Can this person do the job?" They're asking, "Is this someone we'd want representing the company long-term?" Your edge isn't your skill. It's your potential, your self-awareness, your coachability and your maturity. Those are the traits that compound and that's what they're investing in.
 
The Wild Card Tenured Rep
 
The Wild Card Tenured Rep is there for a couple of different reasons. It could be that the other people in that room want this rep's opinion on whether the candidate can do the job, or they believe the rep is in a position to be promoted and they are trying to give them interviewing experience. The only way you will know why they are there is by the questions they ask.
If the Tenured Rep is asking questions centered around expectations, grit, team mentality and what it takes to succeed, you're probably looking at your future teammate, not your future boss. However, if their participation starts to feel like they are status signaling then they are simultaneously interviewing you and selling themselves to the other people in the room. They are trying to portray themselves as promotable.
The Tenured Rep looking for a promotion is not a neutral observer. They are evaluating you, representing the sales team, signaling their own value to leadership and protecting their internal status. A veteran salesperson doesn't stop being a salesperson just because they are in an interview. Status signaling reps are trying to convey "I'm not just a rep, I'm a leader." You may hear questions that sound more managerial than tactical.
 
Questions a Wild Card Tenured Rep may ask:
 
Q: "How do you respond to feedback when it conflicts with your own approach?"
This question signals that they understand coaching dynamics, think like a leader and value adaptability.
A: "I try to understand the reasoning before reacting. Even if I don't fully agree, I assume there's something useful in the feedback. I'd apply it, test it, and then adjust with more context. Early on, I think learning matters more than being right."
This answer shows maturity, signals low ego and demonstrates coachability. It also makes the rep feel comfortable advocating for your hire because you won't dismiss their feedback or challenge their political position. You're telling them you know exactly where they are in the pecking order and very much understand where you will be as well.
Q: "What would you do if you were working hard but still not getting the results?"
This question signals they know that effort alone isn't enough. They may be the only person in the room who truly understands that performance plateaus with almost all salespeople.
A: "I'd step back and look at whether I'm working on the right things, not just working hard. I'd ask for specific feedback, observe top performers and adjust my approach. Effort matters, but direction matters too."
This answer shows an ownership mindset while positioning yourself as someone who wants to learn. It respects their experience and signals that you would look to them for direction on how to win.
Q: "What kind of team environment helps you perform best?"
This is all about culture fit. They know every personality in that sales organization and they're trying to figure out if you'll mesh with the rest of the group.
A: "A team that is supportive but also accountable. I like environments where people share knowledge but still push each other to improve. I perform best when expectations are clear and effort is recognized."
This answer signals that you understand sales is still competitive but can also be collaborative. It doesn't sound needy and shows you won't be high maintenance.
Occasionally some reps will push harder, even seeming adversarial. For them, that serves two purposes. They are stress testing you because they know sales involves pressure and they want to see if you stay composed. They are also demonstrating tough standards and signaling to leadership that they don't let weak hires slip through. A tenured rep doesn't want to outshine leadership. They want to show alignment with leadership while demonstrating value. The ideal message they send the rest of the panel is: "I'm on your side, and I can help you make good hiring decisions."
When everyone is done with their questions someone will wrap it up. They will ask if you have any more questions and you should have some ready.
Questions to Ask the Panel
Most candidates waste this opportunity. They ask safe, generic questions or worse they say something like "No, I think we covered everything." From a sales leader's perspective that is a massive red flag, and you could throw away a perfectly good interview because you're just glad it's all over and want to get out of the room. Staying to ask good questions demonstrates business maturity and shows genuine interest in the role. Almost everyone on that panel knows curious people win and passive people struggle.
Strong candidates ask questions that signal ownership, long-term thinking, coachability, cultural awareness and a desire to succeed, not just get hired. You don't need many. Two to four thoughtful questions is ideal.
Questions:
Q: "If someone were sitting in this chair one year from now and you said they were a great hire, what would they have accomplished?"
This is a future-oriented, results-focused question. It shows a desire to exceed expectations, and it allows anyone in the room to answer.
Q: "What would you want a new person in this role to focus on during their first sixty to ninety days?"
This question implies a readiness to get started and execute without seeming needy. It shows you are serious about ramping up.
Q: "What tends to be the biggest obstacle for new hires in this role?"
Salespeople and sales leaders like it when a candidate demonstrates an understanding of the realities of a sales role. It shows you respect sales as a skill and that you know you'll have to develop it.
Q: "How do top performers here typically use coaching or feedback to improve?"
This question tells everyone in the room that you're coachable and that you want to grow. It will appeal strongly to the sales leadership in the room.
Q: "What keeps your best people here long term?"
This question is for anyone on the panel but if there is a tenured rep in the room, they will default to them to answer. They will be happy to share their thoughts and appreciate you for putting them in a position to shine. The Culture and Values Guardian will love this question too.
One more thing. Do not ask about salary, commission, promotions, vacation or remote flexibility in a panel interview. Those are negotiation topics, not closing topics.
 
Going For the Close
 
The last question you ask should be your closing question and yes, you need to ask for the job. It doesn't have to be awkward or aggressive, but it does have to be clear that you want the role. Asking for the job demonstrates the core skill of sales: asking for the decision.
Close Option 1: Direct and Personal
Q: "Based on what we've discussed, I'm very interested in this opportunity. If selected, I would be committed to learning quickly and contributing to the team. What would be the next steps in the process?"
This is considered a soft close because of the phrase "if selected." It's not a killer close where you assume the sale, but it shows clear interest in the position and keeps a professional tone with everyone in the room.
Close Option 2: Value Focused
Q: "I appreciate the time you've all taken today. The role sounds challenging in a good way and I would welcome the opportunity to prove myself here. I'd love to move forward if you feel like I could be a strong fit."
This is a more confident close but remains humble enough that you stay likeable. It's a window into how you would close a sale in the field. This question offers a tone of resilience and would appeal to the veteran sales leadership in the room.
Close Option 3: The Sales Close
Q: "From my perspective, this role aligns with what I'm looking for and I'm excited about the possibility of joining the team. Is there anything you would need from me to feel confident moving forward?"
Now this is a salesperson's close. If you find yourself in an interview with a lot of sales leaders, they will respect this type of close. Asking for objections in your close while still asking for the role puts you in the top tier of candidates they want to hire. Remember how you close them is how they think you will close a potential customer. This demonstrates that while you may not have a lot of experience, you have a killer sales instinct. Tone and tonality are big in sales.
Moments like this in an interview matter so much when it comes to actually getting the job. Panelists are ultimately watching for three final signals and how you leave them will be what sets the tone for the conversation that comes after you walk out the door.
Do you actually want this job?
Do you understand what it involves?
Can you communicate confidently under pressure?
 
Many hiring decisions are formed in the questions you ask before you walk out the door. The last question they ask you is not a formality. It's your final demonstration of how you think, how you communicate and how badly you want the opportunity.
Practice with someone in answering these questions and then make sure you practice going for the close.
Real quick. If they gave you a bottle of water or a drink of some sort, make sure you pick it up and take it with you or ask, "Thank you very much for the drink. Is there a place I can throw this away?" That simple gesture goes a long way in delivering the message that you take care of your mess and you're aware that if you don't, someone else will have to. That's a great way to leave an interview. Here's why.
What Happens When You Leave the Room
For a few seconds after a candidate leaves the room, there's usually a bit of silence. As is with any hierarchy of people, the lower-level people are hesitant to go first with their opinion. They will want someone at the top to talk first. If you ask for the job with a solid closing question, usually one of the sales leaders will reference that first. "Well, they asked for the job, that's great!" If you don't ask for the job, they will look at each other, someone will exhale and then usually someone who holds a higher position will break the silence. "So, what do we think?" From that moment on, the panel shifts from performance mode to evaluation mode.
First impressions get anchored immediately and if it comes from leadership it will stick. That's why it's so important to ask for the job and clean up after yourself. That will be what gets talked about first. That's the best anchor statement you can make, and it will increase your chances of getting the job. The type of things that get said after you leave the room are, "I liked them," "I'm not convinced," "Strong energy," or "I don't see it." Those first statements become the anchor opinion that will shape the rest of the discussion. The psychology behind it is called anchor bias plus authority influence. If a VP says, "I'm unsure," others will scrutinize more critically. If they say, "I think they could work," the rest of the panel will search for supporting evidence. It's imperative that you leave that room on a high note so that the first anchor statement is a good one.
Each person will weigh in from their lens within the business. Sales Leaders will talk about your ability to carry a quota. The Day-to-Day Manager will talk about what it will take to coach you. The Culture Guardian will talk about how they think you'll fit within the culture. The Tenured Rep will talk about how they think you'll fit within the team. And the Executive will usually talk last while they debate internally whether or not you're a good long-term investment. You're not judged by one standard in a panel interview but rather five simultaneously.
The Tenured Rep's opinion is often requested. Leadership will frequently ask the tenured veteran: "What do you think?" A positive comment from a respected rep can tip the scale. If positive it will sound like, "They seem coachable. I think they'll grind." A negative comment can quietly kill momentum in the room. Some bad answers and no closing questions could produce, "I'm not sure they understand how tough the job is."
Panelists will compare notes. "Did anyone else notice...?" Honestly, statements like this can lead to some very petty conversations. I've heard panelists talk about the way someone sat in a chair or that the candidate kept twirling their pen. Depending on the seriousness of the culture, this is when it gets real. I had a candidate one time who wore a logo'd t-shirt under his white button-down shirt. You could actually see the logo through it. It was a no from two people in the room just because of that shirt. I hired them anyway. Day one I pulled him aside and said, "Hey man, lose the logo shirts under the button-downs. If you need some money to go buy new ones let me know." He never wore another logo shirt again. The fix was that easy. But we almost missed out on a future Presidents Club rep and manager just because of a logo t-shirt. He's a VP of Sales now and I still give him a hard time about it. But he would have been passed on by most. Make sure you look and act the part, or they will talk about it and it could disqualify you.
Inconsistencies get surfaced in these conversations. They will talk about different answers to the same question. Things like "they seemed overconfident for someone with no experience" will come up. If anyone sees any nervous ticks or body language cues it will be brought up. They will talk about how prepared you were or any lack of it. If you were defensive with any of your answers that will be talked about as well. They will review any responses they felt were soft or generic instead of authentic and definitive. This is why consistency in your answers matters so much.
Then comes the risk discussion. Hiring is fundamentally risk management. Everyone in that room has something to gain or lose by taking a hiring risk. The conversation often becomes, "What could go wrong if we hire them?" Their common concerns will be: "Will they quit early?" "Can they handle the rejection that comes with this role?" "Are their expectations realistic?" "How much handholding will they need?" And finally, "Are they culturally compatible?" It's your job to minimize the risk of them taking a chance on you.
Strengths versus concerns are weighted. They will literally frame it that way. One of them will ask, "What did you see as strengths and what are your concerns?" Each of them will have a list based on your answers. Your lack of experience will be on the list. Don't worry too much about this because strong fundamentals will easily overcome the experience objection. They will look for a good attitude, coachability, preparation and competitiveness. Those are the fundamentals they want. The concerns they will look for are unknown resilience, unsure confidence, a quiet personality and oddities. The decision becomes one based on trade-offs and which list outweighs the other.
One strong advocate can carry a candidate forward. One strong detractor can stop them. Don't give anyone a reason to be a no. You don't need everyone to be impressed, you really just need someone who's willing to say, "I believe this candidate can do it." That has to come from the Day-to-Day leader or the Sales Leader. Those are the only two people who can override other detractors and they are risking quite a bit of political capital when they do.
These panelists are imagining training you, working alongside you, handling problems with you and if you don't work out, defending the decision to hire you. It's up to you to put them at ease throughout this interview. Be authentic with your answers, be prepared and look the part. Practice the panel interview if one gets scheduled for you. And for the love of all things holy, go for the close and throw out your trash or take it with you.
The interview itself is only part of the process. What you do in the 24 hours after you leave that room matters just as much. That's what we cover in the next chapter.























Chapter 10
The Follow Up
 
You just finished the interview. You shook hands, asked for the job and walked out the door. Most candidates at this point do one of two things. They either go home and wait, or they do the bare minimum and send a generic thank you email that reads like it was written in thirty seconds. Neither of those are going to help you.
Following up after an interview is one of the simplest ways to separate yourself from the majority of candidates and almost no one does it well. Some don't do it at all. That's the opportunity. In sales, the follow up is where deals are won. Most sales don't close on the first call. Most relationships don't get built in one meeting. The follow up is where you demonstrate that you do what you say you're going to do, that you pay attention and that you actually want the role you just interviewed for.
Think about it from the hiring manager's side. They just spent an hour or more with you. They asked you questions, they shared information about their team, their goals and their challenges. They invested their time. How you follow up tells them exactly how you will follow up with a prospect after a first meeting. It's not lost on them. A great follow-up confirms the decision to move you forward. A poor follow-up or no follow-up at all plants a small seed of doubt.
There’s a concept in marketing called ‘Top of Mind Awareness.” It’s why a realtor sends calendars or why other sales reps give pens away. They want you to be thinking about them. It’s why you see the car that you drive everywhere. It’s top of mind. Following up with the person interviewing you is your way to stay at the top of their mind. Unless you have a pen with your name, email and phone number on it…I’ve never been given that before. That could work too! In leu of you having those pens, let’s see what your follow up should look like.
This chapter covers every stage of follow-up from the first interview all the way through the offer. Each stage is different and requires a different approach. Get good at this and it will serve you throughout your entire sales career, not just in the interview process.
After the First Interview
The first interview follow up is your chance to land one more impression before the hiring manager sits down to make a decision about who moves forward. You have a 24-hour window. Use it.
Send a brief thank you email within 24 hours of the interview. Keep it professional, on the short side and specific. Generic gratitude is invisible. "Thank you so much for your time, I really enjoyed learning about the company" tells the hiring manager nothing. It sounds like every other email they've received that day. What helps you stand out is personalization. Prove that you were listening, that you understood the challenges that were discussed and that you are already thinking like someone in the role.
Reference one or two specific things from the conversation. If the hiring manager mentioned a challenge the team is working through, acknowledge it and briefly connect it to something you said in the interview. If they told you something about the culture or the expectations that genuinely resonated with you, say so and be specific about why. If you asked a question that sparked a good exchange, reference it. The goal is to make the hiring manager read your email and think, "This person was paying attention."
This email is also the right place to reinforce your interest in the role. A lot of candidates assume the hiring manager already knows they want the job. They don't. Or at least they aren't certain. Coming out and saying clearly that you are interested and want to move forward removes any ambiguity and gives the hiring manager something concrete to anchor too when they are making their decision.
Keep the email short. Three to four sentences are enough. You are not writing a cover letter. You are not summarizing the entire interview. You are making one more professional impression before the door closes. Here is what a strong first interview follow up looks like:
Example:
"Hi [Name], thank you for the time today. Our conversation about [specific topic] really stuck with me, especially what you said about [specific detail]. It reinforced why I'm genuinely excited about this role and the opportunity to be part of what you're building. I'm very interested in moving forward and I look forward to hearing about next steps."
That's it. It's professional, it's specific and it closes with clear interest. No fluff, no over-explaining, no resume summary. Just a clean, confident note that reminds them who you are and where you stand.
One note on handwritten cards. A handwritten note can be memorable, and it does stand out. If you know the company's culture values that kind of personal touch, it's worth doing. The risk is timing. Handwritten notes take days to arrive and by then the decision may already be made. Email is the norm because it's timely and expected. If you want to do both, send the email first and follow it up with a handwritten note as a secondary touch. Never do only the handwritten note.
After the Second Interview
A second interview usually means you've cleared the first hurdle. That's something to feel good about. But don't let that confidence make you casual with your follow up. The second interview typically involves a higher-level leader, sometimes a regional manager, a VP or an executive. The stakes are higher and your follow-up needs to reflect that.
The same 24-hour rule applies. But where the first follow-up was brief and warm, the second should be slightly more substantive and strategic. Executives and senior leaders are usually evaluating judgment, cultural fit and long-term potential, not just skills. They are thinking bigger picture. Your follow-up should meet them there.
Reference the strategic themes that came up in the conversation. Things like growth plans, leadership philosophy, the direction of the team or market dynamics are all fair game. These are the kinds of topics a senior leader thinks about every day. When you reference them in your follow-up it signals that you were tracking the conversation at the right level and that you are capable of thinking beyond just doing the job.
This is also the right moment to reaffirm your enthusiasm and your commitment to the opportunity. Not in a way that sounds desperate but in a way that sounds decided. There is a difference between a candidate who says, "I hope to hear from you soon" and a candidate who says, "I'm very interested in this opportunity and I'm ready to move forward." One is waiting. One is closing.
Keep the tone confident but not arrogant. The goal is to demonstrate maturity and fit, not to oversell yourself. Here is what a strong second interview follow up looks like:
Example:
"Hi [Name], thank you for the time today. I appreciated your perspective on [strategic topic discussed] and found it helpful in understanding the bigger picture of where the team is headed. It reinforced my confidence that this is a role I'd be able to grow in and contribute to quickly. I'm very interested in the opportunity and I'm ready to move forward whenever you are."
Notice the difference in tone between this and the first interview follow up. The first one is warm and engaged. This one is confident and direct. That's intentional. You should be adjusting your follow up the same way you adjust your interview approach based on who you are talking to.
After the Panel Interview
The panel interview follow up is the most important one and it's also the one most candidates handle the worst. Most candidates either send one group email to everyone, which reads like a form letter, or they send nothing at all because the whole experience was exhausting and they just want to decompress.
Here is the rule: follow up with every person on the panel individually. Not in one group email. Individually. This is the move that almost no other candidate makes and it is the one that will get people talking about you after you leave. It’s customary for most managers participating in the interview to give you a business card. If they don’t, ask for one. It’s a great way to make sure you have their information readily available so you can follow up in a timely manner.
Think about what it communicates. You took the time to remember each person's name. You noted something specific from each of their questions or comments. You treated each of them as an individual contributor to the process, not just a face in the room. That is exactly the behavior that signals how you will treat a customer. Attentive. Thoughtful. Thorough.
This is why you bring a notepad to the panel interview. While the conversation is happening, jot down the name of each panelist and one thing that stood out from their participation. It doesn't have to be long. Just enough to jog your memory when you sit down to write the emails. One specific observation per person is all you need to make each note feel genuine.
Here is what that looks like in practice:
Example for the Sales Leader:
"Hi [Name], I wanted to thank you for your time today. Your point about [specific comment they made] stuck with me and it gave me a clearer picture of what success looks like in this role. I'm very interested in this opportunity and I'm looking forward to the next steps."
Example for the Culture and Values Guardian:
"Hi [Name], thank you for being part of the conversation today. I appreciated your question about [topic they asked about]. It made me think about [brief reflection]. The culture here feels like a place I'd be motivated to contribute to and I'm excited about the possibility of being a part of it."
Example for the Cross Functional Partner:
"Hi [Name], thank you for the time today. Your perspective on [topic they raised] was something I hadn't considered before, and I thought it was a really valuable part of the conversation. I understand how important that side of the business is and I'd want to approach the role with that in mind."
Each of these emails is short, specific and personal. None of them are the same. That's the point. If you do not have everyone's email address, send a note to your main contact and ask them to share your thanks with the others. It's not as powerful as individual emails but it still signals the right intent.
The 24-hour window applies here too. The panel conversation is fresh, and your emails need to arrive while the memory of you is still clear. If you wait three days, the moment has passed.
When You Haven't Heard Back
You sent the thank you email. You did everything right. And then you heard nothing. A day goes by. Then two. Then a week. Now what?
First, take a breath. Hiring timelines are almost always longer than candidates expect them to be. Managers have full schedules. Decision makers travel. Other priorities come up. The silence is usually not about you. It's about them.
That said, you are not obligated to just sit and wait. Following up when you haven't heard back is completely appropriate when it's done correctly. The key word is “correctly.”
Wait five to seven business days after your thank you email before sending a check-in. If the hiring manager gave you a specific timeline during the interview, respect it. If they said, "we'll be in touch by Friday," don't email them on Thursday. If Friday comes and goes with no word, then Monday is fair game.
Your check-in email should be brief, professional and low pressure. You are not demanding an answer. You are demonstrating continued interest and showing that you follow through. Here is what a clean check-in looks like:
Example:
"Hi [Name], I wanted to follow up and reiterate my interest in the [role] opportunity. I understand hiring decisions take time and I don't want to be a burden, but I did want to make sure I stayed on your radar. I'm still very interested and happy to provide anything else that would be helpful in making your decision."
That's it. Short. Respectful. Clear. It does not beg; it does not pressure and it does not make you sound anxious. It makes you sound like someone who is still engaged and still interested, which is exactly what you want them to think.
If you send a check-in and still hear nothing after another five to seven days, one more follow-up is acceptable. After that, let it go. Continuing to reach out past two check-ins starts to feel like pressure and will work against you. If the role is still open and they want to move forward, they will come back to you. The best thing you can do at that point is to keep interviewing elsewhere and stay in motion. Great sales reps can hear “no” even if the prospect isn’t saying it directly. No communication or ghosting you is their way of saying “no.” Move on.
When They Give You Feedback
Sometimes the hiring manager will come back with feedback before making a final decision. This could be a concern they want to address, a question they forgot to ask or a comparison they are making between you and another candidate. How you handle this moment matters a lot.
The biggest mistake candidates make when they receive feedback is getting defensive. If a hiring manager says, "we felt you didn't have enough experience in X," the wrong response is to argue. The right response is to acknowledge it and address it.
Remember what we covered in Chapter 7: agreeing with an objection is the most disarming thing you can do. It stops the defensive pattern they are bracing for. "You're right, I don't have direct experience in X, and here's how I've been thinking about closing that gap" is a much stronger response than "actually I do have some experience because..."
When you receive feedback over email, take a moment before you respond. Don't react. Read it twice. Identify what the actual concern is. Then write a response that does three things: acknowledges the concern, addresses it with something specific and reaffirms your interest. Keep it concise. One paragraph is enough.
Example:
"Hi [Name], thank you for the feedback. I appreciate you sharing that directly. You're right that I don't have direct experience with [specific concern] yet, but here's how I've been approaching that: [brief specific example]. I'm confident I can close that gap quickly and I remain very interested in the opportunity. Happy to discuss further if it would be helpful."
Notice that this response doesn't grovel, it doesn't oversell and it doesn't panic. It owns the concern, addresses it and keeps the door open. That's what a good salesperson does when a prospect pushes back. The same instinct applies here.
Staying Top of Mind
Sometimes the hiring process moves slowly and there is a real gap between interviews or between rounds. The decision is taking longer than expected, a new role opened up, or the company hit a pause on hiring for a few weeks. These situations require a slightly different kind of follow-up. You still want to stay on their radar, but you don't want to do it by just sending more "checking in" emails. That gets old fast.
One of the most underrated follow-up moves a candidate can make is to send something of value. If you come across an article that connects directly to something the hiring manager mentioned in the interview, share it. If you read a book they referenced and have a genuine thought about it, mention it. If you attended an industry event or completed something relevant, bring it up. These touches keep you top of mind without feeling like you're chasing.
The bar for this is that it has to be genuine. Don't force it. If there is nothing relevant to share, don't invent something. A forced value-add is more transparent than silence. But when something real comes up, use it. It shows that you were paying attention during the interview, that you are continuing to develop and that you are someone who follows through on things they say they are interested in.
Here is what a natural value-add follow up can look like:
Example:
"Hi [Name], I came across this article on [relevant topic] and it immediately made me think of the conversation we had about [specific topic from interview]. Thought you might find it useful. Still very much interested in the role and looking forward to hearing about next steps when the timing is right."
Two sentences. A reason to be in their inbox that isn't just "any update?" and a clean reaffirmation of interest. That's all it takes.
When You Don't Get the Job
Not every interview ends with an offer. That's the reality of sales and the reality of job searching. How you handle a rejection from a company says a lot about your character and it also has more practical implications than most candidates realize.
When you receive a rejection, send a thank you response. This sounds counterintuitive but stay with me. The hiring process takes time and effort from the people on their side. Acknowledging that, even after you didn't get the job, is a sign of professionalism that almost no one does. It is also a smart long-term move.
Companies hire in cycles. The candidate who got the offer ahead of you might not work out. A new role might open up in six months. The hiring manager might move to a different company and take their candidate preferences with them. People remember who handled disappointment with grace. A short, gracious response to a rejection keeps the relationship alive and signals the kind of maturity that gets people reconsidered.
Example:
"Hi [Name], thank you for letting me know. While I'm disappointed, I genuinely appreciated the time everyone invested in the process, and I have a lot of respect for the team you've built. If circumstances change or other opportunities come up, I'd welcome the chance to reconnect. I wish you and the team continued success."
That's a one-paragraph email that takes two minutes to write and could pay dividends for years. The candidate who sends it is the one they remember when the next opening comes up.
If the rejection comes with specific feedback, treat it the same way you would treat any other coaching. Ask yourself honestly if there is something true in it. If there is, work on it. If you don't agree with it, that's fine too, but look for the grain of truth before you dismiss it. Every rejection is information. Candidates who treat it that way improve faster than those who don't.
 
When You Get the Offer
Getting the offer is the goal but it is also a moment that a lot of candidates mishandle. The excitement of receiving an offer can push people to respond too fast, too slow or with the wrong tone. Here is how to handle it well.
If you receive the offer verbally in a call or at the end of an interview, express genuine enthusiasm. You don't have to commit on the spot. It is completely acceptable to ask for the offer in writing and to take a reasonable amount of time to review it. A simple "I'm really excited about this, thank you. I'd love to review the written offer and get back to you within a day or two" is professional and appropriate.
Once you have the written offer, read everything. Compensation, commission structure, ramp period, quota expectations, benefits, start date. Make sure you understand all of it before you respond. If there are questions, ask them. Don't assume.
If you want to negotiate, do it before you sign. Negotiating after you accept is bad form and starts the relationship on the wrong foot. If the base salary is below what you expected, say so directly and professionally. "I'm very excited about this opportunity. I was hoping the base could come in closer to X. Is there any flexibility there?" That's it. Direct, professional and reasonable.
Two things to remember: first, in entry-level sales roles, the commission structure often matters more than the base. Before you push hard on base salary, make sure you understand what the realistic earning potential looks like when you factor in commission. Ask specifically what average first-year comp looks like and what top performers earn. That context will tell you whether the base negotiation is worth having.
The second thing is that most companies hiring entry level sales roles usually have a fixed starting base pay for the position. It may fluctuate a little according to geography and living expenses but usually it doesn’t change too much. They understand that they are in the advantageous position. You need a job that gives you the experience to launch your career, and they assume the risk that you may not work out. It’s appropriate to try and get more but you should understand that they may be limited in their pay offerings. They also want to keep it the same, so they aren’t showing favoritism they have to justify later.
When you accept, send a short confirmation email that reiterates your excitement and confirms the start date. Even after an offer is accepted verbally, a written confirmation closes the loop and demonstrates professionalism. Keep it warm and brief.
Example:
"Hi [Name], I'm thrilled to officially accept the offer for the [role] position. I'm very excited to join the team and I'm looking forward to getting started on [start date]. Thank you again for the opportunity."
Short. Clean. Confirmed. That's how you close a deal.
The Follow Up is the Job
Everything in this chapter is a skill that will serve you far beyond the interview process. Following up consistently, specifically and professionally, is one of the most valued behaviors in sales. The candidates who are great at it in the interview process are usually the reps who are great at it in the field. Hiring managers know this and they are watching for it.
The bar here is not high. Most of your competition will either not follow up at all or do it generically. If you follow the guidance in this chapter, you will automatically be in the top tier of candidates at every stage of the process. And once you have the job, the same instincts will make you a top performer. Following up is how deals get closed, how relationships get built and how careers get made.
Do it every time, do it well and do it within 24 hours. That's the whole formula.





































Chapter 11

You Got The Job, Now What?

You did it! After all the applications, interviews, preparation, nerves, follow-ups and second guessing someone finally said yes! Someone decided to bet on you. You’re not a candidate anymore. You are an employee! A salesperson. A professional. It feels incredible, for about 5 minutes. Then something else creeps in. You start asking yourself, “Now what?”. 

No one tells you how strange the first few days feel. It wasn’t that long ago that you were trying to impress people. Now those same people are going to expect results from you. It feels like just yesterday you were asking questions to get hired. Today, all of a sudden, you’re supposed to know things. Yesterday your job was to perform in interviews. Today everyone expects you to perform in real life? 

Welcome to the wonderful feeling of imposter syndrome. Deep down many new hires carry the same quiet fear: “What if they realize I have no idea what I’m doing?” Here’s the truth that no onboarding packet will tell you. Everyone feels that way at the beginning and there’s probably some people in that office who have been there for a while who still feel that way. Even that confident sales rep or the one who talks like they have seen everything and especially the one who brags all the time. 

Starting a career in B2B sales is like stepping onto a treadmill or more like being dropped into the deep end of a pool. The people who hired you are going to expect you to swim like an olympian, and fortunately no one is keeping track of the time you swim, not yet. What they really want to see from you right now is that you just try to start swimming. This is where a default to action has to take over. You have to start moving forward, you have to lean into the work. It’s the only way to calm the chaos and feelings of insecurity. Take the first step.

This chapter is your roadmap for the first 90 days, the period where careers are quietly made or quietly derailed. You read that right. If the interview was a risk assessment, your first 90 days are the evaluation period where your manager confirms their reasoning for hiring you or realizes they have made a mistake. It’s up to you to make sure they conclude that you could quite possibly be the best hire they’ve ever made. 

You need to breathe and try to relax. They knew you didn’t know much about being successful in the role when they hired you. You have to remember that they didn’t hire you based on your skillset, they hired you because you’re willing to work like crazy to shorten your runway to success as quickly as possible. They hired your effort, will to win, coachability and desire to learn! What they want you to realize is that getting hired wasn’t the finish line, it was the starting gun.

The Emotional Whiplash of Day One

Your first day will rarely match the version you imagined. You may walk in excited, only to spend hours filling out paperwork, watching training videos or sitting in meetings where everyone uses terminology you don’t understand yet. It’s overwhelming and intimidating when you start to hear all of the acronyms, internal jargon, product names and references to customers you’ve never met. You’ll probably leave day one with an armful of company swag and an overwhelming fear of being in way over your head. Not to mention the feeling of not accomplishing anything of real substance for the day. 

Then day two hits, and the expectation shifts slightly. If you didn’t meet new teammates on day one you’ll most likely start meeting them now. Someone will bring up quotas. Someone will ask about your territory. Someone says, “Once you’re up and running…” as if that moment is right around the corner. If it's not racing already, your brain will be in overdrive after speaking with everyone. You’ll be thinking, “How long until I’m supposed to produce?”, “What if I fall behind?” and “What if everyone else figures this out faster than me?” 

That’s why we call it emotional whiplash. It comes from the excitement of the moment met by the reality and anxiety of meeting expectations. You just got thrown in the deep end of the pool and now everyone is starting to yell “swim!” Keep breathing. All of this isn’t supposed to be easy. You’ve just moved from a structured environment (school, internships, part-time jobs) into a results driven environment where expectations aren’t always spelled out. Sales organizations often assume you’ll learn by doing. That can feel uncomfortable, especially if you were used to clear grading systems and defined assignments.

Lean into the conversations with the other salespeople. Those conversations are going to teach you a lot. The explanations of the acronyms, the how to’s on managing a territory, those will come from the training manuals but they will also come from a thousand conversations on the sales floor.

You are going to feel overwhelmed but you should know that doesn’t mean you’re failing. It just means you’re at the beginning and everyone else in that room had their own day 1 as well. You’ll get through it.

Has someone shown you the Dunning-Kreuger effect yet? Understanding where you stand in this will help you see that everyone starts where you are starting. Uninformed optimism is the first step. You think you are going to kill this. Then day one or week one happens and you start to move from uniformed optimism to informed pessimism. Now it feels tougher because you are starting to see what you got yourself into. Eventually you will hit the valley of despair and think ‘there’s no way I can do this’. But you will. You have to. You can’t give up. Only when you break through that valley of despair will you start to gain informed optimism. So many people give up in that valley of despair because something new comes along and then uninformed optimism takes over. Then they just rinse and repeat throughout their career. Not you. You’re going to get through this!



























Adopt the Beginner’s Advantage

I’ve seen a lot of new hires make the same mistake. They try to look impressive immediately. It’s a natural response from a fear or feeling of being overwhelmed. It’s big with the fake it till you make it crowd. It’s understandable given the insecurities and a desire to prove to themselves and others why they are in the room.  

They want to sound smart in meetings. They hesitate to ask basic questions because they don’t want to appear unprepared. They nod along even when they don’t fully understand something. It’s all backwards. The biggest advantage that you have right now is the permission to be new. You’re the one person in the room who can ask fundamental questions without judgement. Remember earlier when we talked about becoming comfortable with being uncomfortable? This is where you start living that out!

This is your opportunity to say things like, “Can you walk me through that? or “Why do we do that this way?” without anyone assuming you're incompetent. In fact, asking thoughtful questions at this point would show how engaged you really are. In this setting, curiosity beats premature confidence. A mistake that a lot of reps make is to make assumptions out loud and they are usually the wrong ones. It’s ok not to know, everyone else in the room is expecting that. 

Write everything down. Keep a notebook, a digital file, or both. Document the terminology you don’t know, processes you see happening, customer types you hear being discussed, product details, objections you hear and all the advice from experienced reps. Future you will thank you for putting in the work. This is the time to listen more than you talk, something I struggled with early in my career. You’re not staying quiet because your voice doesn’t matter, but because you don’t yet have enough context to add value consistently. Learning first will help you contribute more later.

Remember that at this stage of your early career you are trying to confirm your manager's reasoning for hiring you. Being a sponge will help make that confirmation. And when you make mistakes, which you will, correct them quickly and move on. The speed at which you absorb feedback matters more than the fact that you needed the feedback in the first place. Your job isn’t to prove you belong. Your job is to become someone who obviously does.

How You Will Actually Be Judged

New salespeople often assume their early performance will be measured primarily by results. Whether that’s closed deals, revenue or big wins. In most B2B roles, that’s not true, at least not immediately. In your first several weeks you’ll most likely be in training. And even when you start actually doing real work sales managers understand that sales cycles take time. Building a pipeline takes time. Relationships take time. They aren’t looking at pure results first, they are looking for leading indicators. In the first 90 days, you are being evaluated on behaviors.

Are you following their process? Are you coachable? Are you putting in consistent effort? Do you show up on time and you come prepared to work? Do you maintain a positive attitude when things get difficult? Do you handle rejection professionally? These are the factors that help the sales manager predict the likelihood of future success. 

Imagine for a moment that there are two new hires. One closes a deal early but cuts corners, resists feedback and operates inconsistently. The other hasn’t closed a deal yet but is disciplined, engaged and improving daily. Most experienced managers will bet on the second person's long term success and they will be watching the first one like a hawk. Why? Because sustainable success comes from habits, not luck or lying. If you focus on doing the right things consistently, results will follow. If you chase results without building the underlying behaviors they want you to have you can all but guarantee that early success will be the only success you have. 

The Two Jobs You Actually Have

Officially, your job title might be a Sales Representative, Account Executive or Business Development rep but unofficially you have two jobs right now. First, learn the business. Second, build your selling muscles.

Learning the business means understanding what your company does, who it serves, and why customers choose it. When thinking about why customers choose your product or service try to dig into why they purchase and if there’s a business case for the choice. Customers buy solutions so you should understand what solutions you’re actually selling. A sales manager will love nothing more than to see you diving into products and services, pricing structures, ideal customer profiles, competitive positioning, industry trends and internal processes. You should live, eat and breathe all of it. 

Understand that you should take full advantage of this training period before you have to prove yourself. The training period is the only time you ever won’t have a quota in your life as a salesperson. Now is the time to put in the right work focused on the right things. You don’t need to memorize everything immediately, but you do need to develop a working knowledge. When customers ask questions, you should be able to respond confidently or know where to find the answer. 

Building selling muscles is different. Sales is not just information, it’s performance. You need to practice having sales conversations, practice how to handle objections, and most importantly practice how to ask effective questions and learn how to guide prospects toward decisions. Why is learning to ask questions so important? Think of it this way: 

If you and I were 5 miles away from your home and I was driving your car and I tried to find your house without asking you which way to turn there’s no chance I could find your house. However if at every intersecting road, stop sign or stop light, I asked you which way to go there’s a 100% chance I would end up in your driveway. Sales is about following a roadmap to a sale but the only way you’re going to get to where you want to go is by asking questions to your prospect. “What are your biggest pain points when it comes to ‘x’” and “what brought you to a point where you’re looking for a solution here?” Questions help tell you how to sell to someone. Telling everyone your features and benefits will rarely move the needle. Learn to ask for directions when you’re selling.

New sales reps struggle learning to build selling muscles. Training will give you the product or service knowledge but skill only develops through repetition and that means practice. Think of it like learning a sport. Reading about techniques helps, but improvement happens on the field. Every call, meeting, and presentation is practice. Learning to think of each call like practice puts you in a better position to handle rejection. Failure isn’t personal, it's an opportunity to learn and improve. 

You Manager Is Not Your Teacher

In school, teachers structured your learning. They assigned work, monitored progress and ensured that you understood material whether that was in class or providing additional office hours. Managers will operate differently. They expect initiative. As they see it, that’s what they are paying you for. Especially when you are in training. You’re not producing revenue yet, so they see you as someone who should be tracking towards producing revenue. They assume you understand that and you will take the responsibility for your development. Their job is to provide guidance, feedback and support, but they won’t micromanage every step of your progress unless forced too. Managers are pulled in so many different directions they expect you to take initiative and be invested in your own progress. Remember when we discussed how important ownership is? This is where you start owning your outcomes.

If you sit quietly waiting for direction, you may appear disengaged. If you actively seek feedback, request clarification and demonstrate effort, you will constantly allow them to confirm you were the right hire. If you’re having one-on-one meetings, you should come to them prepared. Bring questions. Share feedback and things that you have tried. Ask for specific advice. Managers are energized by employees who treat coaching as a partnership rather than a rescue operation. 

Your manager’s job is to help you succeed. Your job is to make that help incredibly effective.

Activity Over Talent

One of my favorite quotes is by a high school basketball coach Tim Notke. He said, “Hard work beats talent when talent doesn’t work hard.” I take that even further with some reps that I know have a lot of capability but don’t put in the effort to reach their potential by telling them, “There is nothing worse than seeing a five star talent give two star effort.”  B2B sales rewards effort in many ways that other career fields don’t.

You can’t control whether a prospect says yes, You can control how many prospects you contact. You can’t force someone to buy today. You can ensure that your pipeline is full enough that some buyers will emerge over time. In a B2B sales role, volume still matters. 

Cold calls, emails, visits, follow-ups, referrals all of these activities create opportunities. Without them, even the most charismatic salesperson has nothing to work with. New reps often underestimate how much activity is required. Rejection feels personal so they slow down and slowing down reduces opportunities, which reduces results, which increases anxiety. This is where an internal default to action hopefully takes over. Your brain is hardwired to keep you safe. It will constantly tell you not to cold call because it doesn’t want to experience the rejection that comes with it. You have to push through that.

The solution is counterintuitive to what you feel like you should do. When you want to slow down, do the opposite. Increase your activity when your confidence drops. Momentum creates confidence and so does action. Doing something is better than waiting for confidence to appear. Remember what you said in your interview, you want this and you’re willing to work for it. 

Rejection Is The Job

No matter how friendly you are, how polished your pitch is, or how strong your product is, you will hear “no” far more often than “yes.” You need to know that this isn’t a flaw in the system. It is the system. Businesses don’t buy solutions they don’t need. Decision makers are busy. Budgets are limited. Priorities shift. Timing matters. Sometimes you’re simply not the right fit. If you interpret every rejection as a personal failure, the job becomes emotionally exhausting. If you interpret rejection as data, the job becomes more manageable. 

Each “no” teaches you something. Whether it’s time, messaging, qualification, the discovery process or market conditions. Over time patterns will emerge. Top performers aren’t immune to rejection. They are just not as emotionally tied to rejection as you will be at first. They understand that persistence, not perfection, will ultimately drive their results.

Put in the work to build your skill set. Are you saying the right things, are you being strategic about your discovery. Become a pro at asking questions from their business perspective.

Your Professional Identity Matters

You are transitioning from student to a sales professional. This shift is partly external and partly internal. 

Externally, it shows up in how you dress, communicate, and carry yourself. Your professional appearance communicates your seriousness before you ever say a word. How you communicate will show your competence even when you’re still learning. Maintaining your professionalism matters too. So many reps come in looking like a Brooks Brothers ad but then after a few months they start to get comfortable and so does their professional attire. Hold the line of professionalism. Customers care how you look, so do the people who are going to promote you. 

Internally, it’s about responsibility, accountability and dependability. Show up on time and ten minutes early is on time. Follow through on commitments. Managing your schedule and your territory. Those things matter to your team, the people that hired you, your prospects and your customers. When you mess up, and you will, own it, and when your team messes up, own that too. Leaders give credit and take blame, no reason not to start acting like that leader right out of the gate.

Leadership forms impressions quickly. In sales, trust is currency. Customers and colleagues need to believe you are reliable, capable and respectful. You don’t need to be flawless. You need to be consistent.

Create a Personal Operating System

Sales can be chaotic if you let it. Calls, meetings, emails, internal tasks, travel, customers, administrative work everything is competing for your time and attention. And everything feels like a top tier priority.

Without structure, you’ll spend most of your time reacting instead of progressing. My advice to new sales reps is to be violently protective of your time. I say “violent” because there is nothing more important than your time and it needs to be protected. That doesn’t mean saying ‘no’ to meetings you are required to attend; it means windshield time is your enemy. So is taking the time to present to prospects too early in the sales cycle. Sales reps who are reactive instead of proactive aren’t too far away from looking for another opportunity.

You have to develop routines and stick to them. Plan your day before it begins. Identify activities that are priorities. Block time for prospecting, preparation, and follow up. Review your pipeline regularly to ensure opportunities don’t stall or die. At the end of each day, prepare for the next. 

When I was an outside sales rep I had young kids that I wanted to spend time with when I got home and despite my intentions of doing work after they went to bed it never happened. I was tired. That plan never worked. Instead, what I learned worked better for me is to pull over just before arriving at home. I would review my notes for the day, follow up on any emails, and plan my day for tomorrow. On weekends I woke up early before my kids and I would take care of any accounts that I had sold, finish orders and do anything I needed to do before Monday morning. That helped me not to have anxiety on Sundays.

Habits like this reduce decision fatigue. Instead of starting your day wondering what you’re doing or what to do first, you already have a plan. Putting a purpose to your morning will help you keep your routine. The best thing about sales managers is that they most likely have seen a couple of different operating systems that work. Check in with your manager for tips and tweaks to your personal operating system.

You Can Learn From Everyone

Every sales organization has individuals who consistently outperform others. They may not be flashy. They may not talk about their success constantly. But their numbers are always high and always consistent. Seek them out!

Interview them. Find out what their background is and what they did before the role they are in now. Look for commonalities and find out what they learned there to be successful where they are now. Ask if you can observe their meetings, review scripts and discuss strategies. Most high performers are willing to share insights, especially with someone who shows a real effort and desire to be great. 

Pay attention to not just what they say but also to how they operate. Look for their personal operating system and see how it aligns to yours. How do they structure their day? How do they respond to setbacks? How do they prepare for conversations? 

Don’t copy their personality. Authenticity matters. Instead, adopt the principles underlying their behavior. 

Find the ‘anti mentors’ in the room too. Find the people complaining about everything. Learn what their operating systems are. Pay attention to how they respond to prospects, follow up, and make note of what their prospecting, or lack of prospecting looks like. There’s a good chance they can teach you a lot about what not to do. Just do the opposite of what they are doing.

Understand, it’s not in their best interest to see you succeed. Consciously or subconsciously they don’t want you to do well. You need to understand that if you do succeed the ownership of their failure lies solely at their own feet. If you fail, then it’s a system problem and not their fault. Don’t let them drag you down to their level of mediocrity. 

Poor performers will teach you a lot and there’s always more people failing around you than winning. Learn how to win and what not to do.

The Early Career Pitfalls

Certain patterns can derail new salespeople quickly. Be who you said you were going to be in the interview. Dive into prospecting. Sales reps who avoid prospecting waiting to be perfect at it or because it feels uncomfortable don’t last long. You portrayed a sense of ownership so blaming external factors for a lack of progress can’t be a trap you fall for.

You made sure everyone knew you were coachable and it’s one of the reasons you were hired so resisting feedback now because you think you know what you are doing doesn’t jive now. I would tell new sales reps all the time that if you fail doing what I say to do it’s on me but if you fail doing what you want then there’s no way I can save you. 

Comparing yourself to more experienced reps will discourage you and distract you from the real progress you’re actually making. Not checking in with your manager as to your personal progress will lead you off track. Competition is good but not giving yourself grace to find your own voice will make you quit before you see the success your hard work is leading you to.

I heard a newer sales rep ask his manager, “how much do I have to sell in order not to do the CRM entries?” He didn’t last three months. Not doing the administrative work isn’t an option in today’s sales world. So much data flows up in companies it’s impossible not to do the work. Forecasts are made, commitments are given and the future of the company really does start with what you enter into your CRM. If you want to sell a lot and move up in the company, put the work in on the administrative side. 

Motivation isn’t something you wait for. It doesn’t come just because you are hoping it strikes you. You have to take action. Regardless of the circumstances, keep moving forward. Whether it’s the first call of the day, one more call before you pick up your phone, or the last call of the day, keep moving. Default to action. You’ll destroy the competition.

Remember, discomfort is all a part of growth. If you interpret discomfort as a sign to stop, your progress will stall. Check in daily with yourself to make sure you're still the person that checked all the boxes that made them hire you. Be who you said you were going to be and you’ll avoid most of the pitfalls.

Protect Your Reputation

There’s a great quote by Warren Buffett where he says, “It takes 20 years to build a reputation and five minutes to ruin it. If you think about that, you’ll do things differently.” Most people think about the twenty years but you should focus on the five minutes. That’s about the amount of time it takes for people to label you. Reputation cements itself a lot quicker than people think. 

Managers, teammates, prospects and customers will notice effort, attitude, reliability, and accountability long before they see major results. Word spreads informally. People decide whether you are someone they want to support pretty quickly. I remember arguing with my boss about hiring a candidate. I wanted to hire them and the Regional VP disagreed but let me make the call. We hired that candidate and on day one he was an hour late. He ended up not being a bad rep but no matter where we were I always worried if he was going to be on time. His reputation was built on day one because he lost trust.

Treat everyone with respect, not just decision makers. Administrative staff, customer service teams, operations and support personnel often influence outcomes behind the scenes. Don’t just respect these people, appreciate them, it will make you a better human and you’ll be surprised how easy your life will be.

Your reputation is your personal brand. Consistency builds trust. Trust creates opportunities.

The Importance of Early Wins

Your first meaningful success, whether it’s a sale, a signed agreement, or a major step forward, has a psychological impact. It proves to you that the process works. It demonstrates to leadership that you are progressing. And most importantly it starts to turn action into meaningful momentum.

Learning how to count wins matters too. Did you book your first meeting? Did you move a prospect forward in the process? Did you finally track down a decision maker? Those are all wins! Somedays a win looks like just taking action. Did you pick up the phone before 9:00am? Did you stick to the plan you made the night before? Did you show up? 

Celebrating progress, not just final outcomes keeps you moving. Booking meetings, advancing opportunities, receiving positive feedback, these are all signs you’re moving in the right direction. Keep moving forward and doing the work and make sure you count the wins along the way.

Manage Energy, Not Just Time

Being successful in sales is in large part dependent on your mental and physical stamina. Long days, rejection, travel, loneliness and pressure can drain you and do so very quickly. A lot of sales reps these days, especially the new ones, are usually doing what they need to do to take care of themselves physically. It’s the mental health that you have to be cognizant of. 

Stress can affect your sleep and a lack of sleep can affect your ability to execute your day. Learning to count your wins should help your mentality and mindset but it’s important that you’re doing something to take care of the six inches between your ears. Seeking feedback will help you manage stress, so will being proud of the effort you're putting into the work. Progress will also help you manage your stress too. 

Keeping things positive, counting wins, and making progress should keep you mentally in the right spot. You also need to remember the stage you’re in. You’re learning, in sales, you’ll always be learning. If you feel like you’re still learning and being challenged rather than passing or failing. 

Energy comes from passion too. Dive in! Stay focused! Put in the work! Be Consistent! Be Coachable! Learn! And take care of yourself.

Rage rooms are pretty helpful too…

Think Beyond This Job

It’s important to remember that this is your first role in sales. While it is the foundation of your career it is definitely not the ceiling.

Read! Two really interesting statistics about salespeople. The first comes from The Sales Blog where they cite, “over 90% of salespeople may never read a single sales book.”  Which is why I wrote this book for you and not a book for them. That stat tells you it’s not hard to get ahead of your sales competition. Read, and keep reading. You’ll automatically be in the top 10% of salespeople. The second comes from Libby Kane in an article she wrote for Business Insider that says, “85% of rich people read two or more education, career-related, or self improvement books per month, compared to 15% of poor.” 

Reading is the best way to shorten your runway to success. I said it before and I’m saying it again. Keep reading!

When I first started my career in sales, I hated the phone. I would do anything to not pick it up and make a call. Yes, it earned me money. Yes, it was a part of my job. Yes, it was still impossible for me to want to pick it up and make calls. I was given all kinds of advice but what finally worked for me was Mathew McConaughey. You read that right. And no it wasn’t ‘Alright, alright, alright’ that did it. It was his 2014 Best Actor Oscar award winning speech that finally made it click. He said his hero was himself in 10 years. He was chasing being the person he wanted to become. I thought to myself, "Who do I want to be in five years?” and “Would that version of me thank me for putting the time in getting better at this today?”

Those thoughts, and Mathew McConaughey helped me see further than just today. When you’re doing the work of each day it’s easy to get lost in the weeds. Zoom out. Would the person you want to be in five years thank you for the work you put into each day? Mediocrity sits still, greatness doesn’t. Chase that better version of yourself and make yourself proud!

Careers are long. The habits you form from now on will compound over decades. Make sure you’re investing in yourself daily!

What Success in 90 Days Really Looks Like

Hopefully, this is one of the questions you got answered in your interview when you asked it. It’s a lot easier when you have their definition of success but if you don’t here’s the answer I would have given you. Success doesn’t mean instant mastery or record breaking sales. 

It means that you understand the business better each day. You’re practicing constantly. You’re starting to get more comfortable speaking with prospects over the phone and in person. The activity you put in day in and day out is consistent and you’re making progress. Opportunities are in your pipeline and you have moved them through stages. I’d like for you to have closed some accounts but this widely depends on the time you spend in training and the sales cycle of the company that hired you.

You’ve demonstrated a willingness to not just respond but you’re starting to run towards challenges with determination instead of panic. And most importantly you’ve confirmed my reasons for hiring you and you’ve proven yourself as someone I can trust and to continue to develop. You’ve positioned yourself as someone who has an ROI on my personal investment into you.

Growth is gradual. Confidence builds through action!

The Mindset That Separates Stars from Survivors

Talent helps. Intelligence helps. Personality helps. But your mindset will determine your longevity in your sales career. Successful new reps believe effort matters. They interpret obstacles as temporary. They seek improvement instead of validation. They understand that progress is uneven but it compounds.

They don’t wait to feel ready. They act, learn, adjust and act again. Executing is where they gain their edge.

A positive mindset and mentality is talked about a lot in this book and hopefully you’ve recognized that. It’s been the biggest commonality that I’ve seen in successful salespeople. If I can get a salesperson to understand their “WHY” within their current position and help them see their “FIT” in what that role does for their future, I can usually get them to perform to at or above company standards.

You Were Hired For a Reason

There were hundreds of candidates that applied to the position you got. They probably interviewed three to seven other candidates for the job but they chose YOU! Not because you were perfect, but because they believed you had potential. However, potential only matters if it’s developed and a lot of that development will depend on your engagement with you.

I say to sales reps who I’m interviewing, “I want you to understand what my job is. My job is to merely execute the consequences of your decisions. If you want to be a rockstar, you stay coachable, dive into the role and do everything you can to win and crush it, I will make sure everyone knows it. I will make sure you’re recognized, celebrated and eventually promoted. And if you choose to be a bum then I will walk your happy rear end (I use a different word) to the door. But the best news I can give you is that you get to choose who you’re going to be.”

And so do you! You are in control of your effort, engagement, willingness, coachability, desire, and persistence.

There will be days you question yourself. Days when nothing seems to work. Days when rejection feels heavier than usual. Those days are a part of the process, not evidence that you chose the wrong career.

If you stay disciplined, coachable and persistent, you will improve. Opportunities will appear and your confidence will grow! Results are the lagging indicator of effort. Getting hired changed your title. What you do next with the opportunity will determine your trajectory.

A job in outside B2B sales can be a stepping stone, a launching pad, or even a life changing career. The difference lies in how you approach these first 90 days. You don’t need to be extraordinary on day one, but you do need to keep moving forward.

In sales and in life, momentum beats perfection every time.

Chapter 12

Recommended Resources for Early Success

Landing the job is not the finish line, it’s the starting gate. In outside B2B sales, the gap between average performers and elite earners isn’t talent, personality, or even opportunity. It’s growth velocity. The best reps improve faster than the environment changes around them. Markets move, products evolve, competitors adapt and buyers continue to get more sophisticated every year. If you’re standing still in your effort to get better, you’re falling behind.

Your first role in sales is less like a job and more like a paid graduate program in human behavior, business fundamentals, resilience and personal accountability. Every call, meeting, rejection, and win are all considered data. Top performers treat that data as fuel. They ask better questions, seek feedback earlier, study their craft and deliberately refine their habits. Average reps hope experience alone will make them better. Experience alone makes for the longest possible runway when it comes to you being successful. High performers make improvement intentional.

Making constant strides in getting better compounds. A small upgrade in how you prospect, ask questions in discovery, present value, make a business case and manage your time can double and triple your results over a year. Most importantly, all of that improvement and those results build confidence. Not the loud kind of confidence but the quiet, certain confidence that you can figure all of this sales stuff out. That confidence is what allows you to stay calm under pressure, persist through rejection and earn the trust of buyers and leaders alike.

If you commit to becoming a student of the game, not just an employee in it, sales will reward you with an incredible amount of skills, income and opportunities far beyond this first role.

Here are some of the resources I have offered to sales reps knowing they will help you as you look to grow in your career.

Foundational Sales Books to Read to Get Ahead in Your Sales Career:

The following three sales books are what I recommend to every new salesperson that works for me. These books were required reading for my management teams and I think they are foundational to every successful sales reps career. You can listen to them or read them but whatever you do, don’t skip them. 

Fanatical Prospecting by Jeb Blount. This book is your B2B sales bible and while Jeb has written 16 other books, it’s my recommendation you start with this one. This book equips you with the tools to fill your pipeline through a balanced approach of all types of prospecting. It focuses on the discipline of prospecting to get rid of the ups and downs of selling. Read this book, you can thank me later!

Selling From the Heart by Larry Levine. When I saw the title of this book, I thought it was going to be light and fluffy but I was wrong. Larry Levine leads with his heart and his approach in this book is about being intentional, authentic and heart driven with how you sell. This book teaches you to be your true self, a servant leader, and creating trust that builds lasting relationships. I’m lucky enough to call Larry a friend. You should read this book!

New Sales Simplified by Mike Weinberg is a real life approach and guide to prospecting and new business development. This book provides real life strategies for creating prospect lists, crafting a compelling story and mastering calls while still being down to earth. It’s a straightforward, results driven approach to generating new business. 
 
Mindset and Performance Books:

These next books will help your mindset and mentality. You’re about to venture into a new profession with new responsibilities and what got you to this point won’t be what gets you to the next level. Growth always comes from stretching yourself. These books will help you be better in your approach, your management and with people.

Atomic Habits by James Clear is fantastic and focuses on systems over goals and making tiny changes instead of huge ones. Little changes compound and lead to incredible results and he uses the Four Laws of Behavior Change to help you alter your trajectory. If you haven’t read it, please do. It will change your approach to making changes in your life.

Grit: The Power of Passion and Perseverance by Angela Duckworth will help you understand that your success is less about talent and more about long-term passion and perseverance.  This book will show you that hard work beats talent and IQ. I’ve never been able to guarantee that I’m the smartest person in the room, but I can guarantee you that I’ll outwork everyone.

Captivate by Vanessa Van Edwards will help you decode human behavior and learn how to master body language. If you struggle navigating social situations like networking events or conversations with your boss this book will offer you new approaches for building rapport and understanding people. 

Communication and Professional Skills:

Your ability to communicate is a muscle you can grow. Please grow them. Increasing your ability to communicate is one of the best ways to make yourself successful in sales and promotable at your job. Learning how to be a great communicator will pay dividends for the rest of your life.

Crucial Conversations: Tools for Talking When Stakes are High by Kerry Patterson, Joseph Grenny, Ron McMillan and Al Switzler. This is a book that’s applicable at work or at home. It will help you learn to have productive conversations, make it safe to talk about anything and how to prepare, stay calm and turn conflict into dialogue.

Never Split the Difference by Chris Voss is a negotiation guide that teaches high-stakes, FBI trained techniques for personal and business situations. This book covers topics like asking calibrated questions, labeling and using silence to your advantage.

 Toastmasters International is a professional organization that most likely has a chapter in your area you can join. It’s a supportive setting that will help you get better at communicating and presenting to a group. The self confidence you will get being able to present and speak publicly is a skill set most people are terrified of.

Podcasts:

There are a lot of podcasts out there but if you're new to B2B sales I’d stick with the information in the following three podcasts. These podcasts are directly related to your space and they aren’t high pressure hacks who are here today and gone tomorrow. These podcasts will help you stay in your lane, ground you in fundamentals and they all have a lot of archived episodes for you to go through and speed up your learning curve.

Selling From the Heart Podcast - Larry Levine
Sales Gravy - Jeb Blount’s company Sales Gravy’s podcast
The Sales Management Simplified Podcast - Mike Weinberg
